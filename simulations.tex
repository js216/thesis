\section{Simulations}

In this chapter we will present our work on simulating several independent parts
of the \CENTREX\ experiment. First, we will look at non-adiabatic transitions
that may result in an inadvertent transfer of molecular population into
undesirable quantum states as a result of rapidly varying electric fields, as
well as how to suppress such transitions. Second, we will explore a way of
looking at the detection-region molecular dynamics in terms of the quantum Zeno
effect. Finally, we will briefly consider whether rotational cooling can be
enhanced by rapidly switching the polarization of the laser beams.

\subsection{Non-adiabatic transitions}
\label{sect:nonadiabatic}

Molecules travelling from the first state preparation region into the
electrostatic lens experience fast changes in external electric field. Due to
the large number of hyperfine levels involved, predicting level stability is a
non-trivial task. As a consequence of the von Neumann--Wigner rule
\cite{mead1979noncrossing}, levels of same symmetry will in general avoid
crossing each other. Considering only a single such avoided crossing between a
pair of states, we may model the situation using the two-level Hamiltonian
%
\begin{align}
    \label{twostate}
    H=\begin{pmatrix}
        \delta-\alpha\epsilon^2&\beta\\
        \beta&0
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
%
where $\delta\propto2\pi\times100\kHz$ is the field-free level separation,
$\alpha\epsilon^2$ is the level shift due to a quadratic Stark effect; thus
$\alpha$ sets the field $\epsilon$ at which the Stark effect cancels $\delta$
(typically, tens or hundreds of V/cm). Finally, $\beta$ is the level coupling
that gives rise to the avoided crossing.

The Hamiltonian $H$ may be diagonalized exactly to give the eigenenergies
$E_\pm$ and states $\psi_\pm$. To investigate the effect of subjecting the
molecule to a time-varying electric field $\epsilon(t)$, we can assume that at
$t=0$ the system starts in the state $\psi_+$. After solving the time-dependent
Schr\"odinger equation (\TDSE) $id\psi/dt=H\psi$ for the final state
$\psi_\text{f}$, we can evaluate the probability
$P_\text{trans}=|\braket{\psi_\text{f}}{\psi_-}|^2$ the system has transitioned
to the other field-free eigenstate.

If the electric field is chosen such that the system is taken through the
avoided crossing in a time $T_\text{fall}$, then the transition probability
$P_\text{trans}(T_\text{fall})$ approaches zero in both limits
$T_\text{fall}\to0$ and $T_\text{fall}\to\infty$ (see Fig.~\fref{floquet1}). For
intermediate times, the system exhibits a pattern of interference fringes, with
$P_\text{trans}$ very sensitive to the exact timing of the electric field
changes.

The idea of the preceding paragraph finds application in the problem of
molecular motion through regions of spatially-varying electric and/or magnetic
fields. For instance, in \CENTREX\ we prepare molecules in the particular
hyperfine state that is expected to be focused best by the electrostatic lens.
However, the molecular beam has a range of velocities, such that different
molecules will experience a different rate of change of the external fields,
with some non-adiabatically transferred to a state other than the lens state,
preventing and defeating the focusing otherwise expected of the lens.

\subsection{Floquet suppression of unwanted transitions}

Adding to the above a rapidly-oscillating \AC\ electric field can under certain
conditions suppress the transitions otherwise expected in the absence of the
\AC\ field. A plot of $P_\text{trans}$ with respect to the parameters of the
\AC\ field (Fig.~\fref{floquet2}) shows separated regions of high
$P_\text{trans}$ against a transition-free background. Furthermore, one of the
regions does not show dependence on the frequency $\omega$ of the oscillating
field, while the other regions show what appears to be polynomial dependence on
$\omega$.

\begin{figure}
    \makebox[\textwidth][c]{%
        \small
        \begin{minipage}[c]{\textwidth}
            \def\svgwidth{\textwidth}
            \input{figs/matplotlib/Ptrans2.pdf_tex}
        \end{minipage}
    }
    \caption[Transition frequency with \AC\ field.]{Adding an \AC\ field results
    (magnitude shown in left plot, dashed line) in separated regions of high
    $P_\text{trans}$ against a transition-free background (right plot). $T_0=1$,
    $T_\text{fall}^\text{\,\DC}=196.6$, $\Delta T=200$,
    $T_\text{rise}^\text{\,\AC}=T_\text{fall}^\text{\,\AC}=1$.}
    \label{floquet2}
\end{figure}

The above results can be qualitatively be interpreted in terms of Floquet theory
for `stationary' states of periodically driven systems
\cite[ch.~5]{dittrich1998quantum}. It can be proven that the \TDSE\ of such a
system,
%
\[
    i\frac{\d F(t)}{\d t}=H(t) F(t),
\]
%
has solutions of form \cite[sect.~II.A]{shirley1965solution}
%
\[
    F(t)=\exp(-i\epsilon_\alpha t\hbar)\Phi_\alpha(x,t),
\]
%
introducing the periodic Floquet modes $\Phi_\alpha(x,t)$ with energies
$\epsilon_\alpha$, which satisfy a `time-independent' Schr\"odinger equation
(\TISE) in the extended Hilbert space $L_2[0,T]\otimes H$
%
\[
\underbrace{\left[H(t)-i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial
t}\right]}_{\widetilde{H}}\Phi_\alpha(t)=\epsilon_\alpha\Phi_\alpha(t);
\]
%
(We call this equation time-independent in analogy to the usual \TISE\ of form
$H\ket{\psi}=E\ket{\psi}$ \cite[eqn~2.33]{binney2013physics}, i.e.\ a simple
eigenvalue equation of the operator $H$, eigenstates $\ket{\psi}$, and
eigenvalues $E$.)

To obtain the matrix elements of the Hamiltonian thus modified, we expand the
Floquet modes in a basis $\{\ket{k}\}$ of $H$, and also in terms of $n$th order
plane waves $e^{in\omega t}$:
%
\[
\Phi_\alpha(t)=\sum\limits_k\sum\limits_{n=-\infty}^\infty c_\alpha^{kn}e^{in\omega t}\ket{k}.
\]
%
Insert into the \TISE, pre-multiply by $\bra{j}e^{-im\omega t}$, and
time-average over one cycle to obtain
%
\[
\sum\limits_{kn}\bra{j}\widetilde{H}\ket{k}c_\alpha^{kn}=\epsilon_\alpha c_\alpha^{jm},
\]
%
where the matrix elements of the extended Hamiltonian $\widetilde{H}$ are given
in terms of the original Hamiltonian split into constant and time-varying parts
$H(t)\equiv H_\text{DC}+H_\text{AC}(t)$:
%
\begin{align*}
    \label{floqMatElems}
    \widetilde{H}_{nm}^{jk}&=
    H_\text{DC}^{jk}\delta_{nm}
    +n\hbar\omega\delta_{nm}\delta^{jk}
    &\text{(atom + light)}\\
    &+\frac{1}{T}\int\limits_0^TH_\text{AC}^{jk}(t)e^{i(n-m)\omega t}dt.
    &\text{(interaction)}
\end{align*}
%
Here, the lower indices refer to a particular `photonic' mode, and the upper
indices refer to the basis states of the original Hamiltonian. This way of
separating the Hamiltonian into the atomic, radiation, and interaction operators
resembles the `dressed state' picture for a two-level atom in a monochromatic
field of radiation (cf.\ eqn~1.18 in ref.~\cite{metcalf2002laser}); the above
Hamiltonian appears to provide a generalization beyond the two-state model,
since $H_\text{AC}^{jk}(t)$ can be of any dimensionality.

The interaction term in the above general expression can easily be found for the
case of the two-level model in eqn~\ref{twostate}. When an electric field
$\epsilon(t)\equiv\epsilon_\text{DC}+\epsilon_\text{AC}(t)$ is applied, the \AC\
part of the Hamiltonian is
%
\[
H_\text{AC}=-\begin{pmatrix}\alpha&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}
    \left(
    2\epsilon_\text{AC}\epsilon_\text{DC}\cos\omega t+\epsilon^2_\text{AC}\cos^2\omega t,
    \right)
\]
%
which may be time averaged to find the \AC\ matrix elements
%
\begin{align*}
-(H_\text{AC})^{00}_{nm}
&=\biggl[
    \epsilon_\text{AC}\epsilon_\text{DC}(\delta_{n,m-1}+\delta_{n,m+1})\\
    &+\epsilon^2_\text{AC}\left(
    \frac{\delta_{n,m}}{2}
    +\frac{\delta_{n,m+2}}{4}
    +\frac{\delta_{n,m-2}}{4}
    \right)
    \biggr].
\end{align*}
%
The Kronecker deltas give the order of the atom--photon interaction. For
instance, the 0th order term proportional to $\delta_{n,m}$ modifies the
Hamiltonian to the effective form
%
\begin{align*}
    H_\text{eff}=\begin{pmatrix}
        \delta-\alpha\left(\epsilon_\text{DC}^2+\epsilon_\text{AC}^2/2\right)&\beta\\
        \beta&0
    \end{pmatrix};
\end{align*}
%
i.e., the 0th order effect of the \AC\ field is a simple \AC\ Stark shift, where
the \RMS\ \AC\ field has the same effect as a \DC\ field. This suggests that the
mechanism through which the \AC\ field suppresses transitions is by limiting the
effective miniumum value of the \DC\ field, such that the system no longer
passes through the avoided crossing.

The higher order interactions---due to the $\delta_{n,m\pm1}$ and
$\delta_{n,\pm2}$ terms---expand the Hilbert space of the two-state Hamiltonian,
effecting a tensor product with the corresponding Kronecker matrices. The effect
on the spectrum is to replace the two states with an infinite set of pairs of
states, each pair separated from the next by the energy $\hbar\omega$ of a
single photon (Fig~\ref{dressed}). Plotted as a function of the electric field,
we note that in addition to the avoided crossings present in the `unperturbed'
Hamiltonian, new crossings appear as a result of interactions with the virtual
photons.

    \makebox[\columnwidth][c]{%
        \footnotesize
	\def\svgwidth{1.05\columnwidth}
        \mbox{}\hspace{2ex}
	\input{figs/matplotlib/dressed.pdf_tex}
    }
    \caption[Floquet eigenstates.]{Eigenstates of the $6\times6$ Hamiltonian
    $\widetilde{H}_{nm}^{jk}$, where $n,m$ are the indices of the original
    $2\times2$ atomic Hamiltonian, and $j,k$ here limited to the $\{-1,0,1\}$
    part of the infinite Hilbert space. Without the \AC\ field, note that the
    spectrum resembles that of the atomic Hamiltonian (as in
    Fig.~\fref{floquet2}), thrice replicated. As the \AC\ field is turned
    stronger, the location of the avoided crossings---the intersections between
    the straight and curved lines in this figure---moves around or disappears.
    Parameters: $\alpha=0.01,\beta=0.1,\delta=1,\omega=5$.}
    \label{dressed}
\end{figure}

The new crossings typically appear at higher fields than the original ones and
thus do not cause transitions unless the inverting \DC\ field (that is, the part
of the electric field $\epsilon_\text{\DC}$ that reverses polarity, e.g.\ the
thick black trace in Fig.~\fref{floquet2}, but changes slowly compared to
$\epsilon_\text{\AC}$) is of sufficient magnitude to reach them. In the
simulation resulting in Fig.~\fref{floquet2}, the \DC\ field is assumed to
traverse the inherent avoided crossing and not much further.  However, in the
flight of molecules towards the electrostatic lens, fields of order 30~kV/cm
get reversed in a characteristic time
$T_\text{fall}\propto10^{-4}\s\propto10/\delta$. In order to move the induced
avoided crossings between the `photonic' states beyond the reach of these
fields, the applied \AC\ field would need to have a frequency of order
$900\GHz$. Alternatively, the \AC\ field could be confined to a region where the
\DC\ field does not depart too far from zero.

\subsection{Quantum Zeno Effect}

In order to obtain the best systematics possible, the \CENTREX\ experiment
relies on the high molecular detection efficiency achievable using laser-induced
fluorescence, which allows a large number of photons to be scattered from a
single molecule. In turn, this means we can approach unity detection efficiency
for molecules even though the experimental apparatus is only able to detect a
fraction of the photons emitted by the molecules.

The theoretical exploration of the detection process is very complicated on
account of the large number of molecular quantum states. In this section, I
explore a toy model that exhibits two features of the full-scale system: the
existence of `dark states' that are not accessible to laser detection, and the
possibility of non-monotonous dependence on detection efficiency versus the
laser power, i.e.\ more laser power is not necessarily better.

We start by considering the quantum Zeno paradox in a two-level system.
Expanding to a three-level system, we are able to connect the physics to
features relevant for the detection process. Finally, we discuss potential ways
to increase the number of photons scattered in the detection.

\paragraph{Two-level system}

Consider a two level system (Fig.~\fref{level_systems}, left) where a laser with
Rabi frequency $\Omega$ and detuning $\Delta$ couples states $\ket1$ and
$\ket2$. Let the system be initially in state $\ket1$. The solution is a
superposition $\ket\Psi=b_1\ket1+b_2\ket2$, where
%
\[
   |b_2(t)|^2=\frac{|\Omega|^2}{|\Omega|^2+|\Delta|^2}
   \sin^2\left(\Omega't\right),\quad
   \Omega'=\frac{\sqrt{|\Omega|^2+\Delta^2}}{2}.
\]

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.3\textwidth]{figs/todo/twolevel.png}
  \hfill
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/threelevel.png}
    \caption[Two and three level systems.]{Two and three level systems. In the
    case of the two-level system, a third `measurement' state is implied. (See
    text.)}
  \label{level_systems}
\end{figure}

Now consider a measurement done a short time $\delta t$ after the laser is
turned on. The probability of collapsing $\ket\Psi$ to $\ket1$ is
%
\begin{align}
   \label{cosP1}
   P_1=|b_1(t)|^2\propto\cos^2(\Omega'\delta t).
\end{align}
%
As $\Omega'\delta t\to0$, the probability tends to $P_1\to1$. Suppose that over
a time $T$, equal to the length of a $\pi$ pulse, we apply $n$ equally spaced
measurement pulses. Then \cite{itano1990quantum}
%
\[
   P_2(T)=\frac12\left[1-\cos^n(\pi/n)\right],
\]
%
which tends to 0 as $n\to\infty$. Thus we can suppress the normal evolution
(Rabi oscillation) of the two-level system merely by executing frequent
measurements. This effect has been called the quantum Zeno effect. Note that
this argument fails for pure exponential decay instead of the cosine or
quadratic time dependence seen in eqn~\ref{cosP1}.

Note that, as suggested in the caption of Fig.~\fref{level_systems}, the
two-level system is actually a three (or more) level system, since the
measurement, critical to the Zeno effect, must connect to some other state that
rapidly emits a photon that can be detected. In the following paragraph, we
introduce the third state explicitly.

\paragraph{Three-level system}

Now consider adding a third level $\ket3$, making a three-level system
(Fig.~\fref{level_systems}, right) from the two-level system considered
previously. The third level is unstable and decays with a rate $\Gamma$ back to
$\ket1$. To distinguish the Rabi rate that connects $\ket1$ to $\ket2$ from the
one coupling to $\ket3$, call them $\Omega_\text{RF},\Omega_\text{laser}$,
respectively.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/rabi.png}
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/zeno1.png}
   \caption{Rabi oscillation and its suppression with readout pulses.}
  \label{rabi_sup}
\end{figure}

When the coupling to the third state is turned off ($\Omega_\text{laser}=0$),
the system behaves as a two-level system and executes Rabi oscillations
(Fig.~\fref{rabi_sup}, left). If on the other hand we periodically turn the
$\Omega_\text{laser}$ on in the form of short $\pi$ pulses (Fig.~\fref{rabi_sup},
right), executing `measurements' (e.g.\ by detecting the spontaneously emitted
photon and concluding that the system must have been in state $\ket1$), then we
see that the evolution of $\ket1$ into $\ket2$ is significantly slowed down.

In the case illustrated in Fig.~\fref{rabi_sup}, we have applied only four
measurement pulses. By making the measurements more frequent while keeping the
total evolution time the same (Fig.~\fref{strobed_vs_cont}, left), we can almost
arbitrarily suppress to evolution of $\ket1$ into $\ket2$, in accordance with
the Zeno effect. However, there is no need for the measurement laser to be
pulsed. Instead, it can be turned on with a constant power the entire time and
this achieves the same effect, suppressing the evolution from $\ket1$ to
$\ket2$ in relation to the laser power $\Omega_\text{laser}$. The equivalence
(or at least similarity) between the pulsed and continuous observation is
explored further in ref.~\cite{schulman1998continuous}.

   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.4\textwidth]{figs/todo/strobed.png}
  \hfill
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/cont.png}
   \caption[Transition probability from $\ket1$ to $\ket2$.]{Transition
   probability from $\ket1$ to $\ket2$. Same parameters as in the previous
   figure. \part{Left} Probability vs number of measurement pulses when these
   are discrete laser pulses. \part{Right} Probability vs laser power, with the
   measurement laser always on.}
  \label{strobed_vs_cont}
\end{figure}

Now look at the same situation from a different basis using the
transformation
%
\[
   U=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}
   \begin{pmatrix}
      1 &  1 & 0 \\
      1 & -1 & 0 \\
      0 &  0 & \sqrt2
   \end{pmatrix};
\]
%
in other words, we form the superpositions $\ket1\pm\ket2$ between the two
`ground' states, while keeping the excited state $\ket3$ same as before. Now let
the laser couple to the ground states $\ket1$ and $\ket2$ equally, with equal
decay rates from $\ket3$ to either of the ground states
(Fig.~\fref{bright_dark}, right). We find that the positive superposition $\ket1+\ket2$
is `bright' with respect to the laser, meaning the laser is able to pump the
population from it to the excited state, while the negative superposition is
`dark'. The quantum Zeno effect operates as before (Fig.~\fref{bright_dark}).

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/bright_dark.png}
    \caption[Zeno effect in bright/dark basis.]{Quantum Zeno effect shown in the
    transformed basis of bright and dark states.}
  \label{bright_dark}
\end{figure}

Finally, we can look at the total number of photons scattered by the three-level
system subject to a constant laser excitation. After the ground states are
promoted to the unstable excited state, the latter decays, emitting photons
which may in principle be detected in an experiment (`laser-induced
fluorescence'). Naturally, when the laser is turned off or is too weak, there is
a zero or very small probability of moving population into the excited state,
thus the number of photons emitted will be correspondingly small. However, an
initially surprising effect happens when the `detection laser' is too strong:
the number of photons emitted decreases with the increase in laser power
(Fig.~\fref{lslif})!

Of course, this ``laser-suppressed laser-induced fluorescence'' paradox is
merely an example of the continuous quantum Zeno effect. A very strong detection
laser suppresses the evolution of the dark state into the bright state, thus
reducing the amount of population that can be moved to the excited state,
resulting in a lower number of photons detected by the experiment. This has been
noted by Menskii \cite{menskii1998decoherence} in another context, saying that
``a too accurate measurement of the quantum system is not advantageous, and this
is a manifestation of the paradoxical nature of quantum mechanics''.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/lslif.png}
    \caption[Laser-suppressed laser-induced fluorescence.]{Laser-induced
    fluorescence is suppressed when the `detection laser' has too much power,
    just as when it has too little. The smaller plots go from $t=0$ to
    $t=\pi/\Delta$ for $\Delta=0.1$; the three cases are, from left to right,
    $\Omega=0.1,0.35,0.8$.}
  \label{lslif}
\end{figure}

Thus an `accurate measurement of the quantum system' involves a trade-off
between two contradictory requirements: (1) Quickly scattering photons from
bright states; (2) Having dark states evolve as fast as they can. Scattering
photons slows down the dark state evolution, while letting them evolve freely
requires scattering few photons.

\paragraph{Four-level system}

We saw in the case of the three-level system considered above that driving a
laser to an excited state effectively suppresses the evolution between bright
and dark states. In the quantum Zeno effect picture, the laser is constantly
interrogating the system whether the dark state has evolved into a bright state
yet, and that causes the dark state to never evolve. This suggests the following
question: would it be possible to add another level to the system and drive a
transition between it and the dark state with a very strong laser to
Zeno-inhibit the decays into the dark state (Fig.~\fref{4level_zeno}, left)?

The `measurement' implemented by the additional laser (Rabi frequency
$\Omega_\text{Zeno}$) would be repeatedly asking the question: has $\ket3$
decayed into $\ket{\text{dark}}$. It would not be asking any questions about
decays into $\ket{\text{bright}}$, so decays into that state would be
unaffected. In principle this could be done if we could find an auxiliary level
that we could couple to very strongly. However, there is a problem.

We noted above that the quantum Zeno effect relies on a $\cos^2(\omega t)$
probability of not decaying yet, whereas if the non-decay probability is
exponential, the argument does not work:
%
\[
   \lim_{T\to\infty}\lim_{n\to\infty}\cos\left(\frac{T}{n}\right)^{2n}=1
   ,\quad
   \lim_{T\to\infty}\lim_{n\to\infty}\exp\left(-\frac{T}{n}\right)^{2n}=0.
\]
%
In atoms the spontaneous decay of an excited state is indeed
quadratic initially, but only for $\sim10^{-16}$~s \cite{plenio1996inhibition}.
In order to suppress the decay within such a short timeframe, an impossibly
laser power would be required unless a way is found to extend the duration of
the initial quadratic part of the evolution that ultimately results in
spontaneous decay.

   \centering

  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/expdecay.png}
   \caption[Four-level system to suppress dark states.]{Four-level system to
   suppress dark states. \part{Left} Level diagram. \part{Right} For extremely
   short times, excited state decay is quadratic, then turns into the usual
   exponential decay. Figure from \cite{pascazio2014all}.}
  \label{4level_zeno}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Generalized Zeno effect}

In literature we find the terms anti-Zeno or inverse Zeno effect
\cite{panov2002inverse}, which refer to the situation where Zeno effect on one
transition is used to stop the Zeno effect on another transition. For example,
as discussed above, in the three-level system (\ref{level_systems}, right)
interrogating the $\ket1\leftrightarrow\ket3$ transition with a strong enough
laser suppresses the evolution $\ket1\leftrightarrow\ket2$. If we were to add
another level, $\ket4$, and drive an even stronger transition
$\ket3\leftrightarrow\ket4$, then the evolution to the state $\ket3$ would be
frozen out by Zeno effect (see Fig.~\fref{antiZeno}). In turn, this would resume
the Rabi oscillation $\ket1\leftrightarrow\ket2$.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/antiZeno.png}
   \caption{Anti-Zeno effect: suppression of suppression of Rabi oscillations.}
  \label{antiZeno}
\end{figure}

Unfortunately the anti-Zeno effect is not a simple way to undo the problems with
slow dark state evolution. If we were to apply this technique in our detection
scheme, we would need an additional laser to some other state whose only effect
would be to freeze out the main detection laser! This would indeed restore a
faster dark state evolution, but at the cost of turning off the laser-induced
fluorescence. Nonetheless, the concept of the Zeno effect admits of a slight
generalization which, as we shall see, has a widespread application in atomic
physics.

The fact that a continuously observed system never decays is merely
%
\begin{quote}
   a special case of a more general property of continuous measurements.
   [\ldots] Namely, if one checks by continuous observations if a given quantum
   system evolves from some initial state, to some other final state, along a
   specific trajectory in Hilbert space, the result is always positive, whether
   or not the system would have done so on its own accord
   \cite{aharonov1980meaning}
\end{quote}
%
In other words, continuously projecting state $\ket{\psi(t)}$ onto $\ket1$, we
find that $\ket{\psi(t)}=\ket1$ at all times (assuming it initially started out
in $\ket1$). Generally, continuously projecting the state onto some arbitrary
$\ket{\phi(t)}$, we find that $\ket{\psi(t)}=\ket{\phi(t)}$ at all times. This
fact was noted as early as 1932 by John von Neumann
\cite[p.~195]{von2018mathematical} and comes by many names, such as dominated
time evolution, dynamical QZE, quantum Zeno dynamics, quantum Zeno subspaces,
generalized QZE.

With frequent enough measurements, or intense enough detection lasers in case of
continuous observation, we can make sure that the quantum state stays in (or
close to) a given state. If we slowly change the measurement basis, we can thus
manipulate the quantum state almost arbitrarily.

Consider again a three-level system that starts out initially in $\ket 1$, but
now have drive two independent lasers between the two ground states and the
excited state (Fig.~\fref{genZ}, left). The measurement basis is determined by
what states the laser couples to. Initially, we turn on $\Omega_2$, so that the
laser is interrogating whether there is any population in $\ket2$. As required
by Zeno, we will find there none. Then slowly turn down $\Omega_2$ while turning
up $\Omega_1$, which will force the evolution of $\ket1$ into $\ket2$. This
technique is known as stimulated Raman adiabatic passage (STIRAP)
\cite{gaubatz1990population}.

The fact that the laser $\Omega_2$ is turned on
before $\Omega_1$, despite a zero initial population in $\ket2$, is normally
referred to as a ``counterintuitive sequence'', but when understood in terms of
the generalized Zeno effect, it is merely a continuously changing measurement
basis, interrogating whether the state we are projecting to is still empty.
The initial condition is that $\ket2$ is empty at first, and the desired outcome
is that $\ket1$ is empty afterwards, which is why $\Omega_2$ precedes $\Omega_1$.

\begin{figure}[b]
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/genZ.png}
   \caption{STIRAP as an example of the generalized Zeno effect.}
  \label{genZ}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Conclusion}

We have seen in this section that a strong pulsed or continuous laser to a
short-lived excited state suppresses dark-state evolution. The reason this
happens can be derived from basic quantum mechanics. With ``frozen'' dark
states, molecules scatter fewer photons than we can expect based on branching
ratios alone, therefore there exists an optimal detection laser power.
Furthermore, by adiabatically changing measurement basis, it is possible to
force evolution (e.g.\ STIRAP).

In the following section, we consider a different approach to influencing the
speed of molecular evolution, by continuously switching the polarization of the
laser in order to reach states that would otherwise be inaccessible due to
selection rules. In particular, this technique can be useful in the rotational
cooling part of the experiment; see sect~\ref{introduction:overview} for an
overview of the apparatus.

\subsection{Polarization switching in rotational cooling}

Consider a model where a laser drives transitions between the electronic ground
and excited states (Fig.~\fref{tm1}). The excited state has $J'=1$ and has a
lifetime of about 100~ns. In the ground state, only decays/transitions to $J=0$
and $J=2$ are allowed. 

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/tm1.png}
    \caption{Toy model for rotational cooling with polarization switching.}
    \label{tm1}
\end{figure}

The branching ratios can be found from the electric dipole matrix elements
\cite[eqn~2.57]{hutzler2014thesis}
%
\[
    |\bra{J,M,\Omega}\vec D\cdot\boldsymbol{\mathcal{E}}\ket{J',M',\Omega'}|^2
    =
    (2J+1)(2J'+1)
    \begin{pmatrix} J&1&J' \\ -\Omega&\Omega-\Omega'&\Omega' \end{pmatrix}^2
        \begin{pmatrix} J&1&J' \\ -M&M-M'&M' \end{pmatrix}^2.
\]
%
The branching ratios to $J=0$ and $J=2$ are then
%
\[
    b_0\propto
    \sum_{M=0}^{M=0}
    \sum_{M'=-1}^{M'=1}
    |\bra{0,M,0}\vec D\cdot\boldsymbol{\mathcal{E}}\ket{1,M',1}|^2
    ;\quad
    b_2\propto
    \sum_{M=-2}^{M=2}
    \sum_{M'=-1}^{M'=1}
    |\bra{2,M,0}\vec D\cdot\boldsymbol{\mathcal{E}}\ket{1,M',1}|^2.
\]
%
The normalization is simply the sum $b_0+b_2$, such that we have the branching
ratios
%
\[
    \frac{b_0}{b_0+b_2}=\frac13
    ;\quad
    \frac{b_2}{b_0+b_2}=\frac23.
\]

The system in Fig.~\fref{tm1} was entered into the Mathematica
\verb|AtomicDensityMatrix| package (by Simon Rochester) to solve for the density
matrix as a function of time (Fig.~\fref{switching}). We make use of polarization
switching since each laser polarization can only reach three of the five $J=2$
states. We see that eventually, all of the population is transferred to the
$J=0$ state, achieving rotational cooling.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/mathematica/switching.pdf}
    \caption[Population vs time with polarization switching.]{Population vs time
    with polarization switching. $\Omega$ is the laser Rabi frequency, $\Delta$
    is the splitting between the $J=0,2$ states, and $\delta$ is the laser
    detuning. The square-wave signal, whose magnitude is arbitrary, shows the
    sign of the laser polarization, $\sigma^{\pm}$. Time and frequency in units
    of $\Gamma$, the natural width of the transition.}
    \label{switching}
\end{figure}

If we change the switching time (Fig.~\fref{switching_time}, left), the
rotational cooling efficiency changes. In particular, if the switching is done
very quickly, the procedure does not work well. On the other hand, switching too
slowly means that the molecules can pass through the entire rotational cooling
region without experiencing any polarization switches. The optimal speed has to
be in between.

We can also tune the laser power, in which case we find a saturation behavior,
such that increasing the laser power further neither improves nor harms the
cooling efficiency.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/mathematica/RC_sw_time.pdf}
    \caption[Final population vs switching time and laser power.]{Final
    population vs switching time time and laser power. Time and frequency in
    units of $\Gamma$, the natural width of the transition. \part{Left}
    Population vs switching time at $\Omega=0.9$. \part{Right} Population vs
    laser power for switching time = 25. }
    \label{switching_time}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Conclusion}

\keypoint{What have we learned here?}

In this chapter looked at non-adiabatic transitions that may result in an
inadvertent transfer of molecular population into undesirable quantum states as
a result of rapidly varying electric fields, as well as how to suppress such
transitions. Second, we explored a way of looking at the detection-region
molecular dynamics in terms of the quantum Zeno effect. Finally, we briefly
considered whether rotational cooling can be enhanced by rapidly switching the
polarization of the laser beams.

\keypoint{Why is it important for CeNTREX?}

The unwanted adiabatic transitions can lead to a loss of population from the
quantum states where we intend the molecules to be. In addition to a reduction
in overall signal, the more worrying result can be an increase in backgrounds
or systematic shifts, if the molecules move to other quantum states that the
experiment is sensitive to.

The simulations of the detection region and rotational cooling were an early
attempt at understanding the factors that drive the efficacy of those regions.
Since these modules are essential to the operation of the experiment, it is
important to come to a theoretical understanding of how they work.

\keypoint{Will we revisit it in later sections?}

No. The work of this dissertation has been primarily experimental in nature. To
this end, starting with the next chapter will present an overview of the
apparatus, expanding on the section in the Introduction
(sect.~\ref{introduction:overview}). The other chapters following will focus on
some of the parts of the apparatus in greater detail.

\keypoint{What are the biggest remaining challenges to getting a measurement?}

The simulations presented here are merely the beginning of the theoretical
exploration of the systematic and operational factors behind the modules of the
\CENTREX\ experiments. A lot of the follow-up work that has been necessary
before starting construction of the vacuum chambers and other devices has been
reported in other theses related to this experiment \cite{timgren2023thesis} and
the overview paper \cite{grasdijk2021centrex}.
