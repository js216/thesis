\section{Laser Power Control}
\label{laser_power_control}

This section discusses the concepts behind laser power stabilization, as well as
the performance of the system tested using the laser power control PCB. See
Fig.~\ref{AOM_idea} for a diagram of the control system.

\subsection{Basic control theory}

In order to evaluate the performance of the power stabilization system, it is
helpful to learn some basic principles of control theory. In this section, I
define different types of control, bandwidth in analog and digital systems.

In general, there are two broad types of control systems, either open loop or
closed loop (Fig.~\ref{control_types}). Consider a linear, frequency-dependent
amplifier, where the inputs and outputs are given by
%
\begin{align*}
   V_\text{in}&=V_0\sin(\omega t)\\
   V_\text{out}&=A(\omega)V_0\sin(\omega t+\phi(\omega)).
\end{align*}
%
For this open-loop control system, the behavior is determined by the
\emph{open-loop gain} $A(\omega)$ and the phase shift $\phi(\omega)$. Thus we
can control the output if we know $A(\omega)$ and $\phi(\omega)$, i.e.,
calculate the required input to produce a certain output.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/AOM_idea.png}
   \caption{Signal path for laser power stabilization.}
  \label{AOM_idea}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/control_types.png}
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/bode.png}
   \caption[Open vs closed loop control; Bode plot.]{\part{Left} Open loop vs
   closed loop control. \part{Right} Bode plot of gain and phase versus
   frequency. Figure adapted from \cite[Fig.~4.97]{horowitz2016art}}
  \label{control_types}
\end{figure}

As an example, consider the plot of gain and phase versus frequency for a simple
single-stage $RC$ filter section (Fig.~\ref{control_types}). The output at a
constant input amplitude drops proportionally with the input frequency, i.e.\
the output voltage drops by a factor of 2 when the frequency doubles (a.k.a.,
increases by an octave). Thus the `power' decreases by a factor of 4, which can
also be represented in units of decibel as $10\log_{10}4\approx6.02$, hence the
$-6$~dB/octave roll-off noted on the figure. At the same time, as the frequency
passes the `corner frequency' $f_\text{3~dB}$, there is a 90-degree phase shift.

The open-loop gain for an opamp looks very similar. Consider the example in
Fig.~\ref{opamp} and note the similarity with an $RC$ filter. In fact, the gain
roll-off is intentionally added to the amplifier to decrease gain at high
frequencies for stability purposes. The plot of the open-loop gain demonstrates
the large gain characteristic of opamps (100~dB corresponds to a voltage gain of
$10^5$). It also suggests two ways of quoting the bandwidth of the amplifier. We
can either give the bandwidth over which the open-loop gain is relatively flat;
in the example shown, that is approx.\ 10~Hz. The more common number is the
so-called gain--bandwidth product or GBP (approx.\ 1~MHz), which can be read off
as the frequency at which the gain falls to unity. (In general, the GBP could be
different at different gains, but opamps are typically designed with a simple
one-pole roll-off, such that the GBP is independent of gain and can be most
easily found by looking at unity gain.)

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/opamp.png}
  \includegraphics[width=.49\textwidth]{figs/todo/feedback.png}
   \caption[Open and closed loop gain vs frequency.]{\part{Left} Open loop gain
   vs frequency. Adapted from a LM324 datasheet. \part{Right} Bandwidth vs
   frequency, from \cite{black1935wave} as quoted in
   \cite[Fig.~2.87]{horowitz2016art}.}
  \label{opamp}
\end{figure}

We have seen that the frequency range over which the open-loop gain of an opamp
is flat is often surprisingly low (10~Hz in the case of LM324). However, there
is a way to trade off the magnitude of the gain for an increase `flatness'
bandwidth: closed-loop control. As show in Fig.~\ref{control_types}, an inverted
fraction $-B$ of the output is fed back into the input of the amplifier $A$.
This of course decreases the output magnitude:
%
\[
   V_\text{out}=A(\omega)\left[V_\text{in}-B(\omega)V_\text{out}\right]
   \quad\to\quad
   V_\text{out}=\frac{A}{1+AB}V_\text{in}.
\]
%
In the limit of large $A$, this becomes
%
\[
   \lim_{A\to\infty}V_\text{out}=\frac{1}{B}V_\text{in}.
\]
%
Thus, the frequency dependence of the open-loop gain $A(\omega)$ drops out of
the equation and the overall gain (the closed-loop gain) of the system is almost
entirely determined by the frequency response of the feedback network $B$. The
latter can usually be arranged to have an essentially flat frequency response.
Of course, this only works at frequencies at which $A$ is sufficiently large.
See Fig.~\ref{opamp} (right side) for an illustration of how the technique of
feedback dramatically flattens out the gain vs frequency.

In the above, the open loop gain $A$ was that of an (operational) amplifier.
However, the control problem is general, and $A$ could be anything that has an
input and produces a certain output. By arranging for part of the output to be
fed back into the input, we greatly simplify the control of the output. This is
especially the case when $A$ is time-dependent or noisy. Insofar as $A$ is large
enough, we can suppress this time dependence or noise by turning up the gain $B$
of the feedback network. In the following section, I discuss how to arrange that
$A$ is large enough to make closed-loop control possible.

\subsection{Proportional and integral control}

The ideal case for a control system is that of a follower (Fig.~\ref{follower}).
When a follower is working as it should, the input is directly translated to the
output with at most a change of units. For example, if we were able to set up a
laser power control system as a follower, the input voltage (or DAC code) would
be linearly proportional to the output power, with no noise or other
time-dependence at all.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/follower.png}
   \caption{Non-ideal voltage follower: gain is only truly unity when $A$ is
   very large.}
  \label{follower}
\end{figure}

Of course, in real life $A$ is not a simple gain block where we can almost
arbitrarily arrange a very large gain. For example, in the case of laser power
control, $A$ consists of the photodiode and its electronics, an RF amplifier and
amplitude control of the RF, the sound propagation in the AOM, as well as
possible converter in case of digital system. While it is easy to insert an
electronic amplifier in this signal chain, in practice the maximum achievable
gain is limited by the time the signal takes to travel through the signal chain.
In frequency domain, this time would be considered a phase shift, and if it is
large enough can invert the sign of the feedback. Thus instead of negative
feedback, which reduces the overall gain but increases the bandwidth, we would
have positive feedback, which causes oscillation.

A simple solution would be to turn up the gain for slow perturbations, while
keeping the higher frequencies intact. This can be done using integral control.
In time domain, the integral keeps track of the total accumulated error. The
output of an integrator is given as
%
\[
   V_\text{out}(t)=K_\text{i}\int_0^t V_\text{in}(t')\d t',
\]
%
where $K_\text{i}$ is the integral constant. In frequency domain, we see that
integral control amplifies low frequencies more than the high:
%
\[
   V_\text{out}(\omega)=K_\text{i}\frac{V_\text{in}(\omega)}{i\omega}.
\]
%
An integrator inserted in the signal chain can thus help realize a nearly-ideal
follower situation at lower frequencies, without risking instabilities at high
frequencies.

\subsection{Digital systems}

If the control system's signal chain contains a digital section, we have to
consider how this affects the performance of the control system. In this
section, we briefly review the impact of digitization on the bandwidth and
noise.

\paragraph{Bandwidth}

According to Shannon's theorem, a digital system with a sampling frequency of
$f_\text{s}$ can represent a bandwidth of $f_\text{s}/2$ or less:
%
\begin{quote}
   \textsc{Theorem} 1: \emph{If a function $f(t)$ contains no frequencies higher
   than $W\,\text{cps}$, it is completely determined by giving its ordinates at
   a series of points spaced $1/2W$ seconds apart.}
   \cite{shannon1949communication}
\end{quote}
%
It is tempting to imagine that this means that the output of a DAC contains no
energy at frequencies higher than $f_\text{s}/2$, or, conversely, that any ADC
will act as a perfect low-pass filter rejecting noise above $f_\text{s}/2$. This
is of course impossible, since filters with perfectly sharp cutoff do not
exist---they would violate causality.

To illustrate the last point, imagine an ideal low-pass filter with the
frequency response
%
\begin{align*}
   H(\omega)=
    \begin{dcases*}
       C&\quad for $\omega<\omega_\text{c}$\\
       0&\quad otherwise.
    \end{dcases*}
\end{align*}
%
The impulse response is found by taking the inverse Fourier transform
%
\[
   h(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty}H(\omega)\d\omega=e^{-i\omega_\text{c}t/2}\frac{\sin(\omega_\text{c}t/2)}{\pi
   t},
\]
%
where $-\infty<t<\infty$. Thus even though the impulse function $\delta(t)$
occurs at $t=0$, the filter begins to output already at $t<0$; indeed,
infinitely far before the impulse occurs. \cite[sect~5.4.1]{proakis2006digital}

Often, converters do not include any kind of filter, let alone an ideal filter.
Instead, a DAC might output several higher harmonics of the expected output
signal, while an ADC would fold high-frequency noise into the Nyquist band. In
both cases, an anti-aliasing filter is required between the converter and the
analog world. In practice, this is most often a low-pass filter, but it could
also be a bandpass filter, allowing converter operation far above its sampling
frequency. In any case, the bandwidth of the converter is limited to half the
sampling rate, while the bandwidth of the anti-aliasing filter needs to be even
smaller since no real filter can have an infinitely sharp transition between the
passband and the stopband.

\paragraph{Noise}

Introducing anything in the signal chain is likely to increase the noise level
and digital converters are no different. In addition to the noise due to the
required associated analog circuitry, digitization is accompanied by
quantization error reflecting the difference between the real-valued analog
signal and the closest number in the finite set of quantized values.

The nature of the quantization noise depends on the input signal. For example,
if the input signal is a very small sinusoid centered exactly between two
quantized values, the digital output will periodically and predictably jump
between the two values. On the other hand, if the input signal is out of range
of the quantizer (overload condition), than the error can be unbounded.

In many situations we can assume that the quantization noise is random,
uncorrelated, and uniformly distributed between $-\Delta/2$ and $\Delta/2$,
where $\Delta$ is the step size, determined by the number of bits and the range
of the converter. In that case (which does not hold in general!), the
signal-to-quantization-noise becomes \cite[sect~6.3.3]{proakis2006digital}
%
\[
   \text{SQNR}=6.02b+16.81-20\log\frac{R}{\sigma_x}\;\text{dB},
\]
%
where $R$ is the range of the converter and $\sigma_x$ is the standard deviation
of the input signal.

There are other ways to model the quantization noise that obtain somewhat
different expressions for SQNR, but the key points are that the more bits, the
less quantization noise, and that the signal size should be as close as possible
to the range of the quantizer, but not so close that the signal is likely to go
out of range.

\subsection{Laser test setups}

In this section, I will present the iteration of test setups leading up to a
working system stabilizing the power of a UV laser. First, I discuss some
aspects of RF generation and use with the AOM. Then, I present a sequence of
boards using digital and later analog PID control of the laser power.

\paragraph{RF power calibration}

In initial testing the RF signal needed to control the AOM was generated using
an AD9910 evaluation board, or the SynthHD Pro signal generator, and amplified
using the Mini-Circuits ZHL-5W-1+ amplifier. The AOM (IntraAction Corp.,
model number ASM-853B45, serial number 452049) has a resonant frequency around
80~MHz and we need to apply approx.\ 2~W, or 33~dBm of RF power to observe a
significant amount of light in the first order.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/RF_power.png}
   \caption{The effect of RF power on the amount of light detected by the
   photodiode.}
  \label{RF_power}
\end{figure}

The AOM and RF signal path was tested using a HeNe laser (Fig.~\ref{RF_power}).
Evidently the signal generator has enough output power to nearly extinguish the
light that makes it through the aperture. This is more range than is needed for
power stabilization.

\paragraph{Digital control systems}

The first iteration of the control circuit combined a high-speed ADC,
microcontroller, and DDS RF signal generator on three separate boards that
connected together with header pins (Fig.~\ref{digital_PID}). This was tested
using a HeNe laser in the setup shown in Fig.~\ref{inloop}.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/digital_PID.png}
   \caption{PCB for digital PID control.}
  \label{digital_PID}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/inloop.png}
   \caption{Test setup for the microcontroller-based PID loop circuit. The two
   photodiodes define the meaning of in-loop and out-of-loop noise spectra.}
  \label{inloop}
\end{figure}

The main loop of the microcontroller ran the following code:
%
\begin{lstlisting}[language=C++]
void loop()
{
  // calculate error
  const int error = analogRead(inputPin) - setpoint;
  accumulator += error;

  // calculate output
  int dds_out = Kp*error + Ki*accumulator;

  // check output is positive; else make it zero
  if (dds_out < 0)
    dds_out = 0;

  // write to parallel port
  write_parallel(dds_out, 16);
}
\end{lstlisting}
%
Each iteration, it reads the analog input voltage and subtracts the setpoint to
obtain the error voltage. Then the error voltage is added to the accumulator,
which implements an integral of the error. The output is calculated as a sum of
the error (proportional control) and the accumulator (integral control).

The noise spectrum of the light following the AOM was measured on a photodiode
and is shown in Fig.~\ref{digital_BW}. Note that setpoint 3000 is only about
5\%\ of the full scale of 65,535, so we are effectively only using 12 bits.
When the setpoint is increase to 34,900, this improves the signal to noise ratio
by approx.\ 18~dB. The improvement is visible as the significantly lower noise
floor at the higher setpoint. Nevertheless, the system achieves hardly any noise
suppression of the HeNe. After considering how much delay can be expected from
each element in the signal chain, it was found that the performance is limited
by the anti-aliasing filter built-in to the ADC and set to
$f_\text{3~dB}=15$~kHz.

   \caption{Noise spectrum measured on the out-of-loop photodiode for unlocked
   laser, and locked at two different setpoints.}

\end{figure}

Ultimately, the limitation to the performance was found to be the time it takes
for the signal to propagate from the transducer to and through the laser beam in
the AOM. This is limited by the speed of sound in the AOM medium, which in this
case is $v=5.95~\text{mm}/\mu\text{s}$. For a 20~mm AOM aperture, this
corresponds to a delay of $\d t\approx3\;\mu$s, which limits the bandwidth to
$\text{BW}\approx0.35/\d t\approx100$~kHz. The laser beam can be
focused down to a smaller spot size and placed as close as possible to the RF
transducer without clipping the beam. In this way, we were able to achieve an
additional order of magnitude in the control bandwidth (Fig.~\ref{best_BW}).
Ultimately, the circuit was modified to the form presented in
appendix~\ref{app:laser_power}, which integrates several ease of use
improvements.
