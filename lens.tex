\section{Electrostatic Lens}

This chapter describes the work done preparing for testing the performance of
the electrostatic quadrupole lens. The lens is used to increase the amount of
signal by bending the trajectories of molecules towards the final detection
chamber. These molecules, emitted from the beam source over a wider range of
angles, would otherwise collide with the walls of the apparatus. According to
our early estimates \cite{grasdijk2021centrex}, the lens would provide an
estimated gain of 24 in the number of molecules making it to detection.

As of this thesis, the lens gain has not yet been measured. Instead, we describe
in the following sections an overview of the testing setup, a simulation of the
lens gain factor, and the experimental progress towards the measurement of the
lens gain.

\subsection{Testing setup}
\label{testing_setup}

We plan to test the performance of the lens by including it in an experimental
setup that is simplified from the full \CENTREX\ setup. As with the full setup,
the lens is attached to the state preparation A chamber that follows the beam
source and rotational cooling regions. However, instead of proceeding with the
state preparation B, interaction region, state preparation C, and detection
region, the modules after the lens are reduced to two detection chamber, with
the second detection chamber situated a variable distance away from the lens.
The detection regions are simplified from the one that is to be used in the
final experiment; see section \ref{reentrant_sect} for details.

Unlike the full experimental setup, here we include two detection chambers. The
reason to include the one right after the lens is to simplify the initial
configuration of the measurement. If the lens is not fully operational yet, it
will be much easier to detect a molecular signal a short distance away from the
beam source rather than the full $\approx3$ meters away.

To implement the variable distance between the two detection chambers, a number
of 1~meter-long tubes (with ISO160 flanges) will be inserted in between. With
three such tubes, the overall setup approximates the length of the full setup.

\subsection{Simulations}

Following X. Wu \cite{wu2022electrostatic}, we define the lens gain factor as
the ratio of signals detected at a given lens voltage versus that with the lens
turned off. Note that this is not equivalent to the ratio of the molecular
signals with the lens present versus absent; in our definition of the gain
factor the lens is present in both cases, although turned off in one of them.
The effect of the present but inactive lens is to prolong the beam path and
therefore reduce the overall signal.

The simulation code (written by O. Grasdijk) initializes the starting positions
and velocities of a large (between $10^5$ and $10^9$) number of molecules. The
transverse positions in the beam source cryogenic cell exit aperture are
selected at random from a normal distribution with a width similar to the size
of the exit aperture. Similarly, the velocities are selected at random from a
normal distribution approximately corresponding to the measured beam properties
(see section \ref{beam_char_section}). Here we assume the transverse velocity
spread of 39.4~m/s, and average longitudinal velocity of $v_z=184$~m/s, and a
longitudinal velocity spread of 16~m/s. In addition, the molecules are assumed
to experience a gravitational acceleration of 9.81~m/s acting downwards (in the
negative $y$ direction).

Furthermore, the code defines a number of circular apertures centered around the
beam axis that the molecules need to traverse in order to arrive at the final
detection region. Any molecule that is found outside the bounds of any of these
aperture is considered lost. These apertures are listed with their position
along the beam line and radius in Table~\ref{sim_apertures}.

Except for the lens region, containing a quadrupole electric field, the
molecules are assumed to propagate ballistically, i.e.\ with only the
gravitational force acting on them. In the lens region itself, the simulation
assumes a perfect quadrupole electric fields and solves for the molecular energy
levels. The resulting potential gradients apply a focusing force to the
molecular trajectories.

molecular distributions as a function of the two dimensions of transverse
position, $x$ (horizontal) and $y$ (vertical). Also shown are the distributions
with respect to the three components of velocity, as well as the transverse
velocity $\sqrt{v_x^2+v_y^2}$.

The operation of the lens could be verified using just the first detection
chamber, placed immediately after the lens. However, in that case the expected
lens gain factor is significantly lower, as can be seen in Fig.~\ref{lensgain}.
This is to be expected, since the lens has been designed to focus molecules onto
the detection chamber that is placed several meters away from the lens; the
focusing at the first detection chamber is thus insufficient to demonstrate a
large increase in the detected signal.

\subsection{Electrode conditioning at HV}

The four lens electrodes are connected in a quadrupole configuration, such that
two adjacent electrodes have opposite polarity. The two polarities are sourced
from two separate power supply units; to connect these to the four electrodes,
we employ `splitter' boxes shown in Fig.~\ref{splitters}. Each of these connects
one pair of electrodes to one power supply, such that there is a high-voltage
200~M$\Omega$ current-limiting resistor between each electrode and the power
supply. In case the electrodes become shorted to ground, this limits the current
to 150~$\mu$A. Due to the potential for corona discharge, the connections are
potted inside a grounded enclosure.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/splitters.png}
    \caption[`Splitter' boxes for lens high voltage connections.]{`Splitter'
    boxes for lens high voltage connections. \part{Left} Before potting.
    \part{Right} After potting.}
    \label{splitters}
\end{figure}

The lens electrodes operate at a high voltage of $\pm30$~kV with respect to the
grounded vacuum chamber. Given the centimeter-scale spacing between the
electrodes, this gives rise to large electric fields, which in turn present the
danger of sparking and the attendant X-ray generation. To monitor for these
effects, we use a commercial X-ray detector that we attach to a viewport on the
lens vacuum chamber (Fig.~\ref{monitoring_xrays}). At the same time, we monitor
the current drawn from the power supply.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/radDet.png}
    \caption[Radiation monitoring.]{Radiation monitoring using an X-ray detector
    placed at a vacuum viewport.}
    \label{monitoring_xrays}
\end{figure}

The data from two such measurements is presented in Fig.~\ref{dose_voltage}. In
the plots on the left, the lens has been at high voltage for about two hours
before a spike in the reported X-ray dose rate has been detected; the spike
corresponds to a spike in the power supply current. However, there are other
current spikes which do not correspond to spikes in the dose rate; these may
correspond to discharges that either do not generate X-rays, or whose X-rays did
not arrive at the radiation detector. During the second measurement, presented
in the plots on the right in Fig.~\ref{dose_voltage}, no such dose rate spikes
were detected, presenting tentative evidence that such events are much more
likely during the initial period when the lens has just been connected to the
high voltage.

\subsection{Re-entrant flanges for detection}
\label{reentrant_sect}

As mentioned above (section \ref{testing_setup}), the operation of the lens will
be verified by detecting the molecular signal with the lens turned on, and
comparing it with the signal detected when the lens is turned off. This
detection will be done in two detection chambers. One of these is shown in
Fig.~\ref{det_cross}. It consists of a six-way ISO160 cross, with two of the six
ports used for the molecular beam, two for the detection laser, and the other
two for for two reentrant flanges containing one \gls{PMT} each.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/det_cross.png}
    \caption[Reentrant flanges in a `cross' vacuum chamber.]{Reentrant flanges
    in an ISO160 `cross' vacuum chamber. \part{Left} view into the cross from
    the side the laser enters. Molecules would travel from right to left. The
    vertical spacing between the two reentrant flanges is approx.\ 1.75".
    \part{Right} View from the side/top onto the same chamber. Same flange is
    left open in both views.}
    \label{det_cross}
\end{figure}

In addition to increasing the amount of signal, the two-\gls{PMT} configuration
is more symmetric compared to a single-\gls{PMT} detection chamber that has been
used in other tests (such as testing the beam source, section
\ref{beam_char_section}). This can be important for measuring the profile of the
molecular beam by scanning a thin laser beam vertically across the molecular
beam. With just a single \gls{PMT}, the parts of the beam further away from the
\gls{PMT} will appear less bright than the parts closer. With two opposing
\glspl{PMT} as shown here, the decrease of signal in one detection can be
balanced by the increase in the other.

In an attempt to verify the symmetry of this detection setup, we have
constructed the setup shown Fig.~\ref{diodestick}. A standard 5~mm red (645~nm)
LED (Würth 151051RS11000) has been mounted on the end of a 0.148" OD, 0.128" ID
stainless tube (McMaster 8987K445). The tube is mounted perpendicular to a
vertical translation stage (with a Zaber T-NA08A50 actuator), such that the LED
can be translated vertically up or down. Assuming the LED emits equal light
power in all directions around the axis of the stainless tube, and if the tube
is mounted horizontally, equal amounts of light should arrive at each \gls{PMT}
when the LED is centered between the two reentrant flanges. By scanning the
vertical position of the LED, we can quantify how the signal in each \gls{PMT}
depends on the vertical position between the two flanges.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/diodestick.png}
    \caption[Testing symmetry of detection chamber.]{Testing symmetry of
    detection chamber. \part{Left} Two reentrant flanges mounted in a six-way
    ISO160 cross, suspended from aluminum rails. Each \gls{PMT} is connected to
    a high-voltage power supply (SRS PS300 series) and to an oscilloscope for
    readout. \part{Right} View from the top down into the vacuum chamber, with
    the \glspl{PMT} absent. The inset shows the LED calibration light source.}
    \label{diodestick}
\end{figure}

In order to subtract the effect of the background light, the LED is powered by a
square-wave signal generator. The voltage from the signal generator, as well as
the signal from each \gls{PMT}, is measured by an oscilloscope. The \gls{PMT}
signal shown in Fig.~\ref{calib} as a function of LED height for two sets of
\gls{PMT} voltages. In each case, the voltage of each \gls{PMT} has been chosen
to equalize the signal as per the \gls{PMT} calibration reported in
Appendix~\ref{PMT_char_app}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/calib.png}
    \caption[Detection chamber calibration example.]{Example of lens detection
    chamber calibration measurements. The absolute height with respect to the
    reentrant flanges has not been determined for this plot. Parameter
    \part{Left} Slightly lower \gls{PMT} voltages, $(-909,-836)$~V. \part{Right}
    Higher \gls{PMT} voltages, $(-1003,-920)$~V.}
    \label{calib}
\end{figure}

In practice, this calibration scheme is severely compromised by the difficulty
in achieving equal light output in the upwards and downwards directions from the
LED. Even assuming equal light output from the diode in all directions around
its long axis, which could be corrected by rotating the LED around its long
axis, the setup does not allow for precise enough pointing adjustment of the
LED. The majority of light from an LED is emitted in the forwards direction. If
the LED is mounted as little as 5\deg\ from the horizontal (which is
approximately the mechanical precision of this setup), the light going up will
be different by approx.\ 25\%\ from the amount of light power going down, as per
the LED datasheet. The calibration should be repeated with either a more precise
mounting structure for the light source, or using a more isotropic light source.
