\section{Systematics}
\label{section:systematics}

In this chapter, we will consider several sources of systematic errors. The
\CENTREX\ experiment is a search for $T$-violating phenomena that are expected
to occur at very small energy scales, far below the current threshold of
detection.

\subsection{Statistical sensitivity}

In general, the statistical sensitivity of a molecular \gls{EDM} experiment
depends on electric polarization $\mathcal{P}$, coherence time $\tau$, detection
rate $\dot N$, total measurement time $T$, and other factors encoding the
intrinsic sensitivity of the system. For measurements of the Schiff moment in a
nucleus with mass $A$ and atomic number $Z$, the sensitivity (in terms of the
smallest energy shifts proportional to $T$-violating physics that can be
detected) scales as
%
\[
    \delta f\propto\mathcal{P}\tau\sqrt{\dot N T}A^{2/3}Z^2S_0,
\]
%
where $S_0$ depends on the details of the nuclear structure.
\cite{khriplovich2012cp}

As a rough estimate, let us ignore most of the above-mentioned details, and
consider a pulse of duration $t_\text{p}$ with
$N_\text{p}=t_\text{p}R_\text{det}$ molecules. The Poissonian fluctuations of
the number of molecules is $\sqrt{N_\text{p}}$, resulting in the detection rate
shot noise of $\delta S=\sqrt{N_\text{p}}/t_\text{p}$. The corresponding error
in the frequency measurement is
%
\[
    \delta f=\delta S/\frac{\d S}{\d f},
\]
with the approximate detection curve slope $\d S/\d f$. Plugging in the numbers
from Table~\ref{parametertable}, we get a shot noise of approx.\ $5\mHz$ in a
single pulse. For 300 hours of data collected with a 50~Hz pulse rate, the final
experimental sensitivity is expected to be of the order $\approx1\uHz$. The
latter figure corresponds to (via eqn~\ref{schiffnumbers}) to a Schiff moment of
the order $10^{-12}\,e\fm^3$, or (via eqn~\ref{protonSMT}) to a proton
\gls{EDM} of $10^{-26}\,e\cm$.

\subsection{Parameter reversals, averaging, lock-in detection}
\label{systematics:averaging}

The experiment is expected to result in a null measurement of effects that are
odd under $T$-reversal. A crucial part of the experimental design is to make
sure there are no known effects that could mimic genuine $T$-odd interactions.
During data taking, we plan to continually reverse a number of parameters (see
Table~\ref{reversallist}) that in principle should not affect the measured
\gls{NMR} frequency $f$ if done perfectly, and measure the resulting shift
%
\[
S_{A,B,\ldots}\equiv f(A,B,\ldots)-f(-A,-B,\ldots).
\]
%
When the reversals are done at regular intervals, and the collected data is
averaged together, this implements a lock-in detection scheme. In the following,
I will show how this achieves bandpass filtering of the noise, with the
bandwidth of the filter being inversely proportional to the number of averages.

\paragraph{Single average}

Consider a physical signal $V(t)$ that we measure for a total time $T$ from
$t=-T/2$ to $t=T/2$. The measured signal thus is
%
$$
V_\text{meas}(t)=V(t)\cdot\Pi(t/T),
$$
%
where $\Pi(x)$ is the rectangular function
%
$$
\Pi(x)=\begin{cases}
   0,&|x|>1/2\\
   1/2,&|x|=1/2\\
   1,&|x|<1/2,
\end{cases}
$$
%
where $\Pi(\pm1/2)$ is defined to be 1/2 to be consistent with the usual
convention, even though these values are ``almost never important to specify''
\cite[ch~4]{bracewell2000fourier}.

The spectrum of the measured signal is given by the Fourier transform:
%
\begin{align*}
   \widetilde{V}_\text{meas}(\omega)&=\mathcal{F}[V_\text{meas}(t)]\\
   &=\mathcal{F}[V(t)\cdot\Pi(t/T)]\\
   &=\mathcal{F}[V(t)]*\mathcal{F}[\Pi(t/T)]\\
   &=\widetilde{V}(\omega)*\sinc(\omega T/2).
\end{align*}
%
Thus the frequency-domain effect of measuring a signal for a time $T$ is a
convolution of the signal's spectrum $\widetilde{V}(\omega)$ with the sinc
function.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
   \includegraphics[width=.75\textwidth]{figs/todo/comb.pdf}
    \caption[Frequency comb vs averaging.]{The frequency comb arises when
    averaging $N$ different segments of a measured signal. Before the segments can
    be averaged together, they have to be time shifted so they overlap; the
    Fourier transform of time shifting gives the exponentials that sum to form
    the frequency comb depicted here.}
   \label{comb}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Multiple averages}

Now consider dividing the measurement into $N$ equal segments $V_k(t)$ of length
$T_0=T/N$, where $k=1,\ldots,N$. These segments correspond to time-shifting the
original signal $V(t)$, then truncating it to a length $T_0$:
%
\begin{align*}
   V_k(t)&=\Pi(t/T_0)\cdot V(t-T/2+T_0(k-1/2)),\quad k=1,\dots,N\\
   &=\Pi(t/T_0)\cdot V(t-\Delta t_k),\quad\text{where}\;
   \Delta t_k=T/2-T_0(k-1/2).
\end{align*}
%
If we average these $N$ segments together, we get the averaged signal
%
$$
V_\text{avg}(t)=\frac{1}{N}\sum\limits_{k=1}^NV_k(t)
=\frac{\Pi(t/T_0)}{N}\sum\limits_{k=1}^NV(t-\Delta t_k).
$$
%
The spectrum of the averaged signal is
%
\begin{align*}
   \widetilde{V}_\text{avg}(\omega)&=\mathcal{F}[V_\text{avg}(t)]\\
   &=\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\Pi(t/T_0)}{N}\sum\limits_{k=1}^NV(t-\Delta
   t_k)\right]\\
   &=\sinc(\omega T_0/2)
   *\frac{1}{N}\sum\limits_{k=1}^N\mathcal{F}[V(t-\Delta t_k)].
\end{align*}
%
We can rearrange the last line using the time shifting property of the Fourier
transform,
%
$$
\mathcal{F}[f(t-t_0)]=e^{-i\omega t_0}\mathcal{F}[f(t)],
$$

\noindent
yielding the general form of a signal that has been averaged $N$ times:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{sumincluded}
   \widetilde{V}_\text{avg}(\omega)=
   \left[\frac{1}{N}\sum\limits_{k=1}^Ne^{-i\omega\Delta t_k}
   \widetilde{V}(\omega)\right]
   *
   \sinc(\omega T_0/2)
   .
\end{align}
%
It remains to examine the sum of complex exponentials in equation
(\ref{sumincluded}). Disregarding the constant phase factor, the sum is plotted
in Fig.~\fref{comb}. We see it takes the form of a frequency comb, the sharper
the greater the number $N$ of averaged traces but entirely independent of the
total measurement time.

We see from eqn~(\ref{sumincluded}) that in the frequency domain, averaging is a
two-step procedure. First, the spectrum of the original signal is multiplied by
the comb depicted in Fig.~\fref{comb}. If the desired signal occurs at the frequency
corresponding to one of the comb's peaks (or its harmonics), then the effect of
the comb is to bandpass-filter the signal and attenuate the noise away from the
desired signal. As stated above, the greater the number $N$ of averages, the
sharper the comb, i.e., the lower the bandwidth of the bandpass filter.

The second step to the procedure is the convolution with $\sinc(\omega T_0/2)$.
This gives the measured spectrum a linewidth essentially independent of the
number of averages. Underneath the sinc, however, the noise will be attenuated
through the action of the bandpass filter (or frequency comb) that derives its
sharpness from the high number of averages, irrespective of the total
measurement time.

\paragraph{Lock-in detection}

Measuring a signal for a long time, but averaging the whole trace together
(i.e., $N=1$, $T_0=T$) also yields a sharp filter (in fact, the \emph{sharpest}
\cite[sect~7.5.1]{oppenheim2010discrete}), attenuating noise outside its
passband. However, that is merely a low-pass filter. Due to unavoidable `$1/f$'
noise present in all physical systems, making a low-pass filter sharper (i.e.,
measuring for a longer time) often does not yield any improvement in the quality
of the measurement---the reduction in noise bandwidth is offset by the increase
in noise density.

As shown above, averaging for $N\gg1$ times yields a frequency comb that is
sensitive to signals away from \DC. This is seen as the peaks at
$\omega=0,1,2,\ldots$ in Fig.~\fref{comb}. If we modulate the signal of interest
at the frequency of averaging ($\omega=1$) and in phase with it, then the
sensitive signal will be located far away from the noisy low-frequency region.

Does this mean that long measurement times are not needed, since we can `chop
up' a short measurement into a large number $N\gg1$ of segments and average them
up, taking advantage of the sharp comb filter? The reason this would not work is
that after multiplying the original spectrum with the comb, the spectrum is
convolved with $\sinc(\omega T_0/2)$. If $T_0$ is very short, then we are unable
to distinguish the signal at $\omega=1$ from the noise at $\omega=0$. Furthermore,
as stated above, the `$1/f$' noise at $\omega=0$ grows with decreasing
frequency, i.e.\ increasing $N$ at a fixed $T$.

The averaged signal thus retains the low-frequency noise, as well as the noise
and signal at other integer multiples of the averaging frequency. To remove the
noise contribution from comb peaks other than $\omega=1$, we first have to make
sure that the total measurement time is long enough to distinguish the different
peaks, as explained in the previous paragraph. Then, the spectrum can be shifted
by mixing the measured signal with an $e^{i\omega t}$ term. After final low-pass
filtering, we are left with only the signal plus noise in a narrow band around
the signal frequency (bandwidth proportional to number of averages but
independent of total measurement time), plus the `leakage' of noise from the
$\sinc(\omega T_0/2)$ tails of the other peaks. This is the essence of the
lock-in measurement technique.

In this experiment as well as most (all?) other \gls{EDM} experiments, the final
$e^{i\omega t}$ shift is approximately effected by reversing some experimental
parameter(s) in sync with the averaging. This is equivalent to multiplying the
signal with a square wave. Unlike a true frequency shift, the square wave also
includes all odd harmonics. Unfortunately this means that a small contribution
of high-frequency noise around these harmonics gets included in the final
measurement result, but this is unavoidably a part of the nature of reversals.
In principle many parameter reversals (e.g., polarity of interaction-region
electric field) could be replaced by true sinusoidal modulation, but in practice
the technical complexity of this solution would not be worth the incremental
noise reduction from losing the odd-harmonic noise.

In sum, there are three components to successful lock-in detection: (1) a large
number of averages, creating a sharp frequency comb filter independent of total
measurement time; (2) a large total measurement time, allowing us to distinguish
the contributions from the different peaks of the comb; (3) parameter reversals
and final low-pass filtering, effecting the extraction of the final measurement
result.

We now proceed to look at concrete parameter reversals in terms of the specific
noise or systematic effects they are designed to counteract.

\subsection{Electric field effects}

The separation between hyperfine states in TlF is a function of the external
electric field, but not of its sign. A non-reversing part of the field, always
present at some level, can turn that into a systematic error: at 30~kV/cm, the
level spacing changes by order 31.5~mHz if the $\pm30\kVcm$ fields differ in
magnitude by 1~$\Vcm$, as may be calculated numerically by diagonalizing the
ground-state Hamiltonian in the presence of the two near-opposite fields (see
Fig.~\fref{Cslope} and Table \ref{statepairs}).

The shift $S_\text{int}$ is thus a sensitive measure of how perfectly the
interaction-region electric field is reversed. However, a measure independent of
the \gls{EDM} signal is useful. The ACME II experiment characterized the
electric field in the interaction region via microwave spectroscopy of a state
susceptible to large Stark shifts; see \cite[sect~5.1]{lasner2019thesis} for
details.

In addition to a \DC\ offset on the high-voltage electrode supply, or the finite
charging time of the parallel-plate capacitor formed between the electrodes, the
incomplete reversal of $E_\text{int}$ can be due to accumulations of charges on
non-conductive parts of the electrodes, their mounting structure, or the vacuum
chamber itself. The charge accumulations are difficult to predict and will have
to be verified experimentally from the energy shifts once the apparatus is
built. In ref.~\cite{wilkening1984search}, they were able to compensate only
partially for the charge accumulations by placing a variable \DC\ offset on the
electrodes.

The electrode charging time should be estimated once the setup is in place, and
can also be extracted from the difference $\Delta S_\text{int}$ in the shifts
$S_\text{int}$ where $E_\text{int}$ is switched from positive to negative,
compared to the negative-to-positive shift. This shift was in fact one of the
larger effects in ref.~\cite{cho1991search}, but the authors chose to suppress
it with further reversals instead of trying to locate and remove its cause.

It is also possible that the field reversals gradually change the field
magnitude over time. That would be evident from plotting $S_\text{int}$ over
time, unless another contrary effect were to conceal the gradual drift in
$|E_\text{int}|$. Indeed, any other permutations of the switching sequence could
cause systematic effects of their own, and will have to be investigated.

The polarity of the field in the quadrupole should not impact the operation of
the lens since it works on the principle of a quadratic Stark effect. For
reasons analogous to those in the preceding paragraph, however, it will be
important to verify this experimentally. In addition, we can expect part of the
quadrupole field to leak into other part of the apparatus, in particular the
interaction region. A small fraction $\epsilon$ of the leaking reversing
quadrupole field $\vec E_\text{quad}$ interacting with a constant precession
field $\vec E_\text{int}$ would effectively modify the absolute magnitude of the
latter:
%
\[
|\vec E_\text{int} + \epsilon\vec E_\text{quad}|
\ne
|\vec E_\text{int} - \epsilon\vec E_\text{quad}|,
\quad
\epsilon\ll1.
\]

The high voltage electronics has several possible effects on the measurement
systematics. For example, the current that flows to charge the electrodes to the
high voltage creates a magnetic field that can end up magnetizing the magnetic
shield even after the current has stopped flowing. This is a particularly
`scary' systematic since it occurs in phase with the electric field reversals
and can mimic an \gls{EDM}. Other effects include the noise on the high voltage
supply and leakage currents; we discuss these and others in the following.

\paragraph{HV supply systematics}

It is possible that the reversal of the interaction-region electric field gives
rise to a magnetic field, either due to the electronics effecting the reversal,
or due to currents leaking between the field plates. In
ref.~\cite{cho1991search}, they employed an additional field reversal by
manually rewiring the electrode high voltage supply (motivated by the switching
relay's frequent breakdowns \cite{cho1990thesis}), finding that the switching
electronics does not noticeably impact the \gls{EDM} measurement (at least, the
electronics are no worse than the manual electrode polarity switching). To guard
against the second type of induced magnetic field, they made sure that the
leakage current remained below 2~nA at the operating voltage, resulting in what
they claim is an entirely negligible effect.

In \CENTREX, it will also be important to verify that the \HV\ electronics, and
the plate charging/leakage currents, do not incur significant systematics. While
we are aiming for a sub-10~$\mu$G residual \DC\ magnetic field, the condition on
magnetic fields correlated with $E$-reversals is significantly more stringent as
its contribution adds up---instead of averaging out---with the reversals,
mimicking an \gls{EDM}-type effect. Our tolerance to these fields can be
estimated by comparing the energy of a proton \gls{EDM} that we would like to be
sensitive to ($d_\text{p}\sim10^{-25}e\cm$) in a typical effective field
($E_\text{eff}\sim30\kVcm$) with the Zeeman frequency shift ($\Delta
f/B\sim5\mHz/\mu$G). With these numbers, a magnetic field oscillating
synchronously with the electric field, with a magnitude greater than 150~pG
would cause a Zeeman shift larger than the proton \gls{EDM} in the effective
field. This is the field at a distance of one inch away from a long wire
carrying 2~nA of current (which is a typical upper limit for leakage currents,
and is also the charging current after $\approx16$ $RC$ time constants for
$30\kVcm$ electrodes charged via 1~M$\Omega$ resistors).

The extreme sensitivity to stray currents is somewhat ameliorated by the fact
that the resulting magnetic fields are not necessarily parallel to the $30\kVcm$
electric field; the perpendicular components cause only a quadratic, $E$-even
energy shift. In the atomic thallium experiments \cite{regan2001thesis}, the
authors note that the leakage current can be very asymmetric with respect to the
direction of the electric field, and also erratic over time. The trouble can be
mitigated to some extent by making the apparatus as symmetric as possible (using
coaxial wiring for the electrodes, making connections in the center of the
plates as far as possible, making sure the return currents flow symmetrically).
This also underlines the importance of avoiding ferromagnetic materials around
the interaction region that could retain memory of the direction and magnitude
of the charging currents.

Any currents in the vicinity of the magnetic shield can also magnetize the
magnetic shield itself, coupling together seemingly unrelated parameters. For
example, currents for charging the high-voltage electrodes in the interaction
region or the lens could do this, and cause a residual magnetic field that
reverses with the applied electric fields. In the following paragraph, we
explore this effect.

\subsubsection{Charging magnetization}

The interaction region electrodes are two parallel plates, approx.\ 15~cm wide
by 3~m long, separated by 2~cm. The plates form a capacitor of approximate
capacitance of several hundred picofarads. As the voltage on the electrodes is
increased from zero to $\pm30$~kV, the charging current $I=CdV/dt$ flows to
supply the electric charge. This current is associated with a magnetic field
which in turn induces a permanent magnetization in the magnetic shield that
remains after the current has stopped flowing.

In the section on magnetic field (\ref{shield:charging_magnetization}), we have
estimated that the magnetic field inside the magnetic shield changes by approx.\
10~nG when a current of 0.1~A flows through the inside of the shield. Assuming
the electrodes have a capacitance of 200~pF and are linearly charged to a
potential difference of 60~kV in a second, this implies a current of 12~$\mu$A.
Extrapolating, this would mean a charging magnetization of 1.2~pG, which is much
smaller than the 150~pG figure quoted above when comparing the Zeeman shift of
the main interaction resonance with the desired experimental sensitivity.

However, if the electrodes are not charged up to the final voltage using a
constant current, the instantaneous current could be much higher. For example,
if the electrodes are simply connected to a low-impedance source of high voltage,
the initial surge of current would be much higher than calculated above, then
slowing down exponentially as it approaches the final voltage. Alternatively, if
the voltage is controlled by a DAC, the steps between the discrete values could
be an occasion of very fast voltage rise, accompanied by a large charging
current and resulting magnetization.

The charging current poses a limitation on the electric field reversal
frequency, since it limits the speed at which the electrodes can be charged up
to final voltage. The currents will need to be measured experimentally to ensure
they are within the limits set by the shield charging magnetization.

\subsubsection{HV noise}

The \gls{NMR} resonance responds to electric fields with a slope of approx.\
30~mHz/(V/cm). If we want the electric field noise to result in an effect much
smaller than 45~nHz (the expected statistical sensitivity of the experiment
\cite[sect~3.1]{grasdijk2021centrex}), then the field noise needs to be limited
to far below 1.5~$\mu$V/cm. At the scale of 30~kV/cm, this is 50 parts per
trillion, a fantastically strict noise requirement (34 bits of accuracy, or 10.5
digits, or 206~dB).

However, we need to keep in mind that due to the lock-in technique inherent to
the \CENTREX\ experiment, we only care about the noise in a very narrow
bandwidth, limited by the total amount of data to be collected (about 300 hours,
corresponding to a bandwidth of order 1~$\mu$Hz), around the electric field
reversal frequency (to be determined, but expected to be between 5~mHz and
0.1~Hz). This implies a noise spectral density of 1.5~mV/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$.

Other experimental reversals may help suppress the noise even further by
shifting the central frequency from the field reversal frequency to a higher
frequency linked to the other reversals. This simplifies the HV electronics
design since the supply can be low-pass filtered using a simple $RC$ filter; the
lower the frequency, the harder it becomes to suppress noise.

\subsubsection{Reversal reproducibility}

After each field reversal, voltage has to settle at the same value it had before
the reversal.

\subsubsection{HV relays magnetization}

The electric field is to be reversed using a pair of high-voltage relays that
are pneumatically actuated to reduce the amount of magnetic fields that are
correlated with the field reversals. Assuming a slope of 5.0~mHz/$\mu$G of the
\gls{NMR} resonance with respect to magnetic fields (see
Table~\ref{statepairs}), the field due to the relay switching needs to be much
lower than $\sim10$~pG to achieve the sensitivity of 45~nHz as mentioned above.

The pneumatic actuation system itself will rely on solenoid valves, which,
however, can be located in a shielded enclosure and located far away from the
sensitive main interaction region.

\subsubsection{Leakage currents}
\label{systematics:leakage_current}

Regardless of the care taken in constructing the apparatus, a small amount of
current will flow between the two electrodes, as well as between the electrodes
and the grounded vacuum chamber. For example, there could be a surface current
on the surface of the ceramic standoffs from the electrode to the inner wall of
the vacuum chamber, or a flow through the imperfect vacuum in case any of the
residual gasses get ionized.

The leakage current $I$ causes a magnetic field of the order of $B=\mu_0I/(2\pi
R)$ a distance $R$ away from the current, and also needs to be limited to
$\ll18$~pG. Assume the molecules are located a centimeter away from this
current; then the current needs to be limited to below 90~pA. In order to
protect against a leakage current systematic, a picoammeter will be built,
tested, and inserted in the electric path supplying the electrodes with the high
voltage.

\subsubsection{Accumulated charges}

Charges can accumulate in the interior of the vacuum chamber, and on all
non-conductive surfaces. Therefore we need to coat the interior of the glass
tube with a conductive coating, and ground it, to ensure a stable electric
field.

\subsection{Magnetic field effects}
\label{systematics:magnetic}

The requirements of our experiment constrain the three spatial components of the
magnetic field: the `horizontal' field $B_x$ along the direction of the
interaction-region electric field, the `vertical' component $B_y$ perpendicular
to both the electric field and the direction of molecules, and the axial field
$B_z$ along the symmetry axis of the magnetic shield. In this section, we will
present the systematics effects that result from the static residual field, the
noise, the magnetic field that changes in phase with the electric field
reversals, and the spatial magnetic field changes (`gradients').

\paragraph{Residual field}

The allowed transverse residual field perpendicular to the interaction-region
electric field $\vec E$ is limited by the motional magnetic field experienced by
the molecules. As they fly through with a velocity $\vec v$, they experience the
motional field $\vec B_\text{mot}=\vec v\times\vec E/c^2$ that changes direction
when the electric field is reversed. (With our experimental configuration,
$|\vec E|\approx30\kVcm$ and $|\vec v|\approx180\ms$, the magnitude of the
motional field is $|\vec B_\text{mot}|\approx60\uG$.) Any residual field in the
same direction will add to $\vec B_\text{mot}$, such that the magnitude of the
total field will change with electric field reversals. Due to the Zeeman
splitting between the spin up and down states, the change in magnetic field
magnitude introduces a frequency shift. This does not constrain the other two
components (horizontal, axial) of the magnetic field.

Due to $M$ modulation, the shift is further suppressed by an order of
magnitude, such that the residual fields need only be a factor of $6\times$
lower than the motional field; that is, the average residual fields of up to
10~$\mu$G are allowed.

The $M$ modulation works as follows. In TlF, there is an internal magnetic field
$\vec B_\text{int}$ at the site of the thallium nucleus due to the rotational
motion of the molecule \cite{cho1991search}. To estimate the magnitude of the
internal magnetic field $B_\text{int}$, compare the magnitude of the Zeeman
shift due to the Tl nucleus $-(\mu_1/I_1)(\vec I_1\cdot\vec B)$ to the strength
of the Tl spin-rotation energy $c_1(\vec I_1\cdot\vec J)$. The two effects are
equal in strength at $B_\text{int}\approx50.8$~G.

The internal field adds with the motional field and the residual field $\vec
B_\text{res}$ to form the total magnetic field inside the molecule
%
\[
   |\vec B|=\sqrt{|\vec B_\text{int}|^2 + |\vec B_\text{mot}+\vec
   B_\text{res}|^2}
   \approx|\vec B_\text{int}|\left[1+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\vec
   B_\text{mot}\cdot\vec B_\text{res}}{|\vec B_\text{int}|^2}\right)^2\right].
\]
%
By flipping the sign of the projection $m_J$, the internal field can be reversed
exactly. The reversal is not expected to result in a change of the hyperfine
level separation unless the external fields $\vec B_\text{res}$ change in
magnitude in phase with the $m_J$ reversals. This could be the case if there are
leaking electric or magnetic fields from the state-selection chamber.

%If the state-selection region will feature a static magnetic field
%$B_\text{ss}$, used to select one of the $m_J=\pm1$ states, the shift

\paragraph{Noise}

The magnetic noise, such as that due to the Johnson current noise in the
electric field plates, primarily affects $B_x$, the component parallel to the
electric field, and affects the other two components only quadratically. Since
the experiment averages a number $N$ of measurements taken at regular intervals
$\Delta t~1/f_0$, the noise is effectively bandpass-filtered through a bandwidth
$f_0/N$ centered around $f_0$ (see section \ref{systematics:averaging}). We
expect to accumulate $N\approx10,000$ datapoints, collected at a frequency
$f_0\approx10$~mHz, which corresponds to a bandwidth of approx.\ 1~$\mu$Hz.
Considering the magnetic moments in TlF are of magnitude $\approx1$~kHz/G, the
integrated magnetic noise has to be below approx.\ 50~pG. While this is too low
a magnitude to measure directly with the magnetometers available in our lab, we
estimate that the magnetic shield suppresses the noise well below this level on
account of its excellent dynamic shielding (see sect~\ref{magnetic:noise}).

\paragraph{Gradients}

Here we consider two possible effects due to a spatial variation in the magnetic
field. If the molecules experience a periodic variation at the correct frequency
of order 100~kHz, this can drive transitions between the two \gls{NMR} states, much
like the \RF\ coils.

Another way spatially varying fields can affect the measurements is via
geometric phase effects.

\subsection{RF coils}

The relative phase $\phi$ of the separated oscillatory fields is switched
between $\pm\pi/2$. In either case, if the two thallium spin projections
acquired no relative phase delay during the molecular precession time, we expect
to read out two equal populations. (In addition, we can also switch the cables
used to connect the coils to their sources---their different lengths will cause
different phase shifts.) Indeed, ref.~\cite{wilkening1984search} found no
significant effects on the \gls{EDM} shift arising from the nominally
$\pm1^\circ$ phase accuracy between the two coils. However, in \CENTREX\ we are
aiming for a more precise measurements, and it is worthwhile estimating the
allowed phase error.

The phase error $\Delta\phi$ can either reverse with the phase reversal so that,
overall, the coil phase difference is $\pm(\pi/2+\Delta\phi_\text{R})$, or it
can stay constant (so that $\phi=\pm\pi/2+\Delta\phi_\text{NR}$). A small
reversing error $\Delta\phi_\text{R}$ cancels out when taking the difference of
the signals between the two signs of the phase, as was noted by Ramsey in
ref.~\cite{ramsey1951phase}. The non-reversing part $\Delta\phi_\text{NR}$,
however, does not, and changes the overall signal by about 2~\%\ per degree of
$\Delta\phi_\text{NR}$. Given that the signal changes by 9.4~\%\ per Hz of
\gls{NMR}
resonance frequency change, a one-degree non-reversing phase error causes a
spurious 0.18~Hz frequency error.

A further shift under simultaneous reversal of $B_\text{ss}$ and $m_J$ is due to
the so-called Millman effect \cite{millman1939determination}, due to the spatial
curvature of the fields inducing the \gls{NMR} transition. The oscillating field can
be decomposed into two rotating fields, and only one of them is actually
inducing the transition, depending on the direction of the magnetic field. In
the frame of the molecule, the frequency of the oscillating field changes with
the curvature of the fields, resulting in $B$-dependent change in the resonant
frequency. \cite{wilkening1984search} For two separated coils, to first order
the shift due to the spatial curvature of the field is proportional to the
geometrical angle $\theta$ between the coils, and inversely proportional to the
time of flight $T$ of the molecules between the two coils, resulting in a
frequency shift $\theta/2\pi T$. \cite[eqn~12]{cho1991search}

The Millman effect could be distinguished from an ambient magnetic field by a
beam velocity reversal (impractical in the \CENTREX\ setup), or by varying the
beam velocity so as to change $T$. In a pulsed beam, the natural velocity spread
means that the time of detection is strongly correlated with the velocity. By
analyzing the data separately for different velocity classes, we should be able
to find the extent of the Millman effect. As a crude estimate, assume the
minimum and maximum times of flight $T$ are simply
$L_\text{sd}/(v_\text{CM}\pm\alpha)$. Then the uncertainty due to the Millman
effect is
%
\[
    \Delta f_\text{Millman}
    =
    \frac{\theta}{2\pi}
    \left(
        \frac{1}{T_\text{min}} - \frac{1}{T_\text{max}}
    \right)
    =
    \frac{\alpha\theta}{\pi L_\text{sd}}
    \approx
    \frac{15\mHz}{\text{deg}}\theta,
\]
%
using the numbers from Table~\ref{parametertable}.

The counter-propagating term, ignored when making the rotating-wave
approximation, causes a small \AC\ Zeeman shift, called a Block--Siegert shift.
The effect is proportional to the \RF\ drive power and can be observed
experimentally by applying a power slighly higher or lower than that required
for $\pm\pi/2$-pulses. Since the \gls{SOF} signal exhibits only a quadratic
dependence on the drive power, we can thus distinguish the two effects. At any
rate, the shift only depends on the magnitude of the \RF\ power and will thus
drop out when averaging over the appropriate parameter reversals.

\subsection{Detection region systematics}

In previous experiments \cite{cho1991search}, it was also important to consider
the shifts in beam intensity corresponding to parameter reversals. In contrast,
we are able to measure both spin-up and spin-down populations (as explained in
the overview of the detection scheme in section \ref{introduction:overview}),
making us insensitive to changes in beam intensity and the resulting
systematics. By studying the ratio of the difference and the sum of the two
populations, the overall beam intensity is in principle cancelled out unless the
intensity change is different for the two \gls{NMR} states, or for the states they
connect to in the state preparation regions around the main interaction region.
We have not yet found that to be the case.

\subsection{Conclusion}

\keypoint{What have we learned here?}

This chapter does not present a comprehensive overview of all systematics
effects that can affect a precision-measurement experiment such as is \CENTREX.
Instead, we have defined an estimate for the statistical sensitivity that can be
expected, and explained how this can be achieved using the lock-in technique.
Next, we looked at some potential systematic errors to do with electric and
magnetic fields.

\keypoint{Why is it important for CeNTREX?}

For an experiment with an expected null result it is essential to consider as
many potential sources of systematic offsets as possible. Very likely, the true
value for the Schiff moment in thallium fluoride is significantly smaller than
the value likely obtained by a measurement of this type. The discrepancy needs
to be explained via systematic shifts that most likely give rise to it.

\keypoint{Will we revisit it in later sections?}

Systematics affect nearly every single part of the experiment. For example, a
magnetic field that reverses with the interaction-region electric field directly
mimics a $T$-violating phenomenon, thus the need for very precise magnetic field
control (chapter~\ref{mfc_chapter}) combined with well-isolated high-voltage
control (chapter~\ref{HV_chap}).

\keypoint{What are the biggest remaining challenges to getting a measurement?}

Since this treatment is not comprehensive, the effects considered in this
chapter will need to be complemented with other potential systematics effects
before the measurement can be properly interpreted.
