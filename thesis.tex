\section{Introduction}

\noindent The \CENTREX\ experiment aims to employ a molecular-beam resonance
technique to measure the nuclear Schiff moment of $^{205}\Tl$ in thallium
fluoride as a test of physics beyond the Standard Model. In the following, I
outline the motivation from $CP$-violation and baryon asymmetry, define the
Schiff moment and suggest how it can be measured, review relevant features of
the TlF molecule, and present previous experiments and the measurement scheme
proposed in this work.

\paragraph{Motivation}

Before the early 1960s, it was believed that $CP$ is a good symmetry of nature.
Landau pointed out that that makes it impossible for fundamental particles to
have electric dipole moments \cite{landau1957conservation}. When beginning their
neutron \EDM\ experiment, Purcell and Ramsey were aware of this objection
\cite{purcell1950possibility}. Indeed, were such an \EDM\ to be detected, that
would provide clear evidence of $CP$-violation (\CPV).

It has been found since that while most processes preserve $CP$, certain rare
weak interactions violate it as observed in neutral $K$ decays at the 0.003
level \cite{christenson1964evidence} and other systems, where it can be as high
as 0.7. \cite[chapter 13]{PDG16}. To account for the experimental evidence, the
flavor-changing part of the standard model quark sector includes a
\CPV\ phase in the \CKM\ quark-mixing matrix. \cite{peccei1995} This
so-called Kobayashi--Maskawa mechanism, which introduced the third quark
generation precisely to explain \CPV\ \cite{kobayashi1973cp},
agrees with all particle physics experiments to date.  \cite{hou2011source}

No corresponding flavor-neutral \CPV\ has been observed yet. In
principle, the \QCD\ Lagrangian could contain a term of the form
\cite[eqn~1]{pospelov1999theta}
%
\begin{equation}
   \L = \theta
   \frac{g^2}{32\pi^2}G_{\mu\nu}^a\widetilde{G}^a_{\mu\nu},
\end{equation}
%
where $G^a$ is the gluon field tensor. Experimental evidence suggests that the
strength of this term relative to the usual strong interaction is
$|\theta|<9\cdot10^{-11}$.  Presently accepted theories cannot explain why
$\theta$ should be that small; this fine-tuning issue is known as the strong
$CP$ problem. See \cite{graham2015experimental} for a brief review of a few
possible solutions.

At present, searches for \EDM s give the most sensitive constraints on
flavor-diagonal \CPV. Conversely, given a particle physics model with
\CPV\ we can predict what the expected \EDM\ of the particles is. For
example, in the Standard Model, the \CKM\ phase can produce a lepton \EDM\ no
larger than $10^{-38}\;e\cm$. Part of the reason the \EDM\ is so suppressed is
that it cannot appear below the four-loop level.
\cite{pospelov1991electric} Given how small these effects are in the Standard
Model, \EDM s thus provide nearly background-free
evidence of new physics. In fact, the \CKM\ background would only become
apparent when probing effects beyond the energy scale of $10^{11}\GeV$.
\cite{pospelov2005electric}

The second major motivation for searching for new physics comes from the baryon
asymmetry of the unverse (\BAU). Compared to the current number density of photons in
the universe ($n_\gamma \approx411\cm^{-3}$), the current baryons density
($n_\text{B}\approx2.54\cdot 10^{-7}\cm^{-3}$) is some $1.6\cdot10^{9}$ times
smaller \cite[Table 17]{bennett2013nine}. In contrast, the antibaryon density
$n_{\bar{\text{B}}}$ is smaller still; the range of upper bounds for the
antimatter-to-matter number ratio has been reported as $10^{-15}$ to $10^{-6}$
\cite{canetti2012matter}. I should note that the antimatter-to-matter ratio
tends not to be used as a measure of \BAU; instead, authors tend to
prefer the ratio
%
\begin{equation}
\eta =
   \frac{n_B-n_{\bar{B}}}{n_{\gamma }}
   \approx
   \frac{n_B}{n_{\gamma }}
   \approx 10^{-10}.
\end{equation}

There is as of yet no commonly accepted baryogenesis mechanism, i.e.,
explanation for how the imbalance between the number of particles and
antiparticles---the $C$ asymmetry---came to be. In a 1967 paper, Sakharov
hypothesized that \CPV\ is necessary to explain the present $C$
asymmetry \cite{sakharov1991violation} if we assume the initial conditions of
the universe were $C$-symmetric.

However, it is claimed that the `amount' or the `strength' of \CPV\ in the
\CKM\ matrix is not enough to explain the extent of the current \BAU. A common
argument to this effect compares the size of the dimensionless constants used to
parametrize the two effects. For \BAU\ we have $\eta\approx10^{-10}$
as defined above, whereas for \CPV\ the constant is no larger than $10^{-20}$
\cite[sect 2.3]{riotto1999recent}. The latter number refers to the \CPV\
invariant that can be defined in terms of quark masses as
\cite[eqn~3]{hou2011source}
%
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
   J = A(&m_\text{t}^2 - m_\text{u}^2)
         (m_\text{t}^2 - m_\text{c}^2)
         (m_\text{c}^2 - m_\text{u}^2)\\
        &(m_\text{b}^2 - m_\text{d}^2)
         (m_\text{b}^2 - m_\text{s}^2)
         (m_\text{s}^2 - m_\text{d}^2),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
%
where $A$ is twice the area of any ``unitarity triangle'' of the \CKM\
matrix (see \cite[fig 12.1]{PDG16}). With quark masses from \cite[p40, ``Quark Summary
Table'']{PDG16} and $A\approx3\cdot10^{-5}$ from
\cite[p7]{kleinknecht2006unitarity}, we get $J\approx121\cdot
10^3\GeV^{12}$. The electroweak phase transition, occurring at
temperature $T_\text{EW}\approx100\GeV$, was the latest point in the
evolution of the universe when \BAU\ could have been generated
\cite{fodor2000EW}. Comparing $J$ and $T_\text{EW}$, we get the desired
dimensionless quantity $J/T_\text{EW}^{12}\approx10^{-20}$ to
within factors of order unity and we see it is ten orders of magnitude smaller
than the constant parametrizing the baryon asymmetry. Thus, new sources of \CPV\
are needed.

\paragraph{Schiff moment}

Additional $CP$-violating forces would likely give rise to a nuclear \EDM,
whether through the \EDM\ of unpaired protons or neutrons, via \CPV\
interactions between valence nucleons and the core of the nucleus, or other
mechanisms. \cite{safronova2018search} The \EDM\ $\vec d$ would result from a
redistribution of nuclear charge $\delta\rho$ as
%
\begin{equation}
   \vec d=e\int\vec r\delta\rho(\vec r)\d^3 r,
\end{equation}
%
and would violate $CP$, as follows. Since the nuclear spin $\vec I$ is the only
preferred direction in a nucleus, the dipole moment also has to point along it:
$\vec d=d\vec I/I$. Under time-reversal, the \EDM\ would stay the same but the
spin would flip direction. Assuming $CPT$ to be a good symmetry, the
$T$-violation then leads to the $CP$-violation.

However, Schiff proved in 1963 that an \EDM\ of a nonrelativistic point nucleus
would not be observable outside of the atom. More precisely, the interaction
energy of nonrelativistic point charged electric dipoles in an arbitrary
external electrostatic potential has no term linear in the \EDM; relativistic
and second-order effects are extremely small \cite{schiff1963measurability}.
Physically, the system rearranges itself so as to screen the external field
completely \cite[p33]{safronova2018search}. Thus, a $CP$-violating \EDM, if
existent, would be very hard to detect experimentally without a mechanism to
bypass Schiff's theorem.

In heavy atoms, the limitation posed by Schiff's theorem is lifted by the finite
size of the nucleus. This necessitates writing $P, T$-odd nuclear potentials in
the form \cite[eqn~22]{sushkov1984possibility}
%
\begin{equation}
   \delta\phi=e\int
   \frac{\delta\rho(\vec r)\d^3r}{|\vec R-\vec r|}
   +\frac{1}{Z}(\vec d\nabla)\int
   \frac{\rho_0(r)\d^3 r}{|\vec R-\vec r|},
\end{equation}
%
where $\rho_0(r)$ is the spherically symmetric nuclear charge distribution. The
second term is responsible for the screening since it exactly cancels out the
potential due to a dipole. As shown in \cite[eqn~29]{sushkov1984possibility},
the above potential may, under certain assumptions, be written in the form
%
\begin{equation}
   \label{nabla}
   \delta\phi = -4\pi\vec S\cdot\nabla\delta(\vec R),
\end{equation}
%
where $\vec S$ is the Schiff moment (\SMt)
%
\begin{equation}
   \label{simpleSchiffMoment}
   \vec S=\frac{e}{10}
   \left[
      \ex{r^2\vec r} - \frac{5}{3Z}\ex{r^2}\ex{\vec r}
   \right].
\end{equation}

To get an intuitive grasp on the expression $\nabla\delta(\vec R)$, consider the
definition of the delta function as the limit of Gaussian distributions:
$\delta(r)\sim\lim_{a\to0}e^{-r^2/a^2}$ (Fig.~\ref{graddelta}a). Taking the
derivative of the Gaussian, we get a certain shape that is negative for $r<0$,
positive for $r>0$, zero at $r=0$ and also vanishing for $r\to\pm\infty$. That
shape (Fig.~\ref{graddelta}b), stretched to infinity but keeping the area
constant, is then representative of $\nabla\delta(\vec R)$.

We may think of eqns~\ref{nabla}--\ref{simpleSchiffMoment} as the potential due
to a constant electric field along the direction of the nuclear spin $\hat I$
inside a nucleus of radius $R$. This potential is linear in the coordinate $r$,
truncated outside of the nucleus (i.e., at $|r|>R$). In the limit of a small
nucleus, this will have a similar shape (Fig.~\ref{graddelta}c) as the
expression $\nabla\delta(\vec R)$ discussed in the preceding paragraph.

A more sophisticated approach using the spherical multipole formalism gives a
more general form for the leading-order Schiff moment operator which reduces to
eqn~\ref{simpleSchiffMoment} under certain assumptions. As discussed in
\cite[sect~III]{liu2007atomic}, these may not in fact be justified.

We can estimate the magnitude of the Schiff moment caused by a single valence
nucleon with a dipole moment $d_\text{n}$. Assume it moves in a uniform
spherical core of radius $R\approx R_0A^{1/3}$, where $R_0\approx1.2\fm$ is the
characteristic nuclear size \cite[eqn~3.8]{krane1987introductory}. Then the
magnitude of the \SMt\ is
%
\begin{equation}
    \label{schiff}
   |\vec S|\sim d_\text{n}A^{2/3}R_0^2
\end{equation}
%
to within
factors of order unity \cite[eqn~8.76]{khriplovich2012cp}. The $A^{2/3}$ scaling
indicates that heavier atoms are preferrable for \SMt\ experiments. Note,
however, that given an \SMt\ measurement, the preceding estimate provides only
an upper bound on the nucleon's \EDM\ since the nucleonic contribution to the
\SMt\ tends to be 10 to 100 times smaller than that due to the $P,T$-violating
interactions between valence nucleons and the core.
\cite{sushkov1984possibility}

\paragraph{Molecular techniques}

For nuclei inside a molecule, the Schiff moment and other $P,T$-violating
effects give rise to an effective interaction of form
%
\begin{equation}
   \label{interaction}
   \H=-d\hat\sigma\cdot\hat\lambda,
\end{equation}
%
where $\hbar\hat\sigma/2$ is the nuclear spin, and $\hat\lambda$ is a unit
vector along the internuclear axis in the molecule (in TlF, pointing towards the
F nucleus). The interaction strength $d$, also written in frequency units as
$\nu\equiv d/h$, can then be interpreted in terms of a molecular \EDM, proton
\EDM, Schiff moment, or other quantities. For example,
\cite[eqn~26]{cho1991search} use a calculation from \cite{coveney1983parity} to
obtain the Schiff moment $S$ from the interaction strength $\nu$:
%
\begin{equation}
   S=\frac{\nu}{5.67\cdot10^5}\frac{e\fm^3}{\Hz}.
\end{equation}

Without an external electric field, the interaction of eqn~\ref{interaction}
fails to produce any first order effects in any given rotational state.
According to \cite{hinds1980experiment,wilkening1984search}, this is because the
Tl nuclear spin is perpendicular to the internuclear axis in an unpolarized
molecule. Alternatively, the rotation of the molecule averages $\hat\lambda$ to
zero. \cite{cho1989tenfold, cho1991search} However, if we apply an external
field, the molecule becomes polarized, which destroys the inversion symmetry and
a nonzero expectation results.

Compared to atoms, molecules are polarized much more readily in laboratory
fields, making them ideal for such an \EDM\ measurement. Thus Sandars suggested
that a molecular-beam resonance experiment could be used to probe the existence
of an electric dipole moment on the proton, so long as the molecule has a heavy
atom with an unpaired proton in the nucleus. \cite{sandars1967measurability}

We can estimate the strength of the laboratory field required to efficiently
polarize the molecule by comparing the molecular rotational level spacing
$2B_\text{rot}(J+1)$ with the dipole energy $d\cdot E$. Using the TlF values
$d=4.2~\text{Debye}$ and $B_\text{rot}=6.7~\text{GHz}$, we can expect a 76~\%\
alignment in a field of about $25~\text{kV/cm}$ for the $J=1$ state, and the
internal electric field can reach the order of $10^9$~V/cm, a factor of $10^5$
improvement over a typical laboratory field. \cite[sect~9.1]{khriplovich2012cp}

To obtain an upper bound on $\nu$, typically a pair of states is chosen such that
one of the states moves up in energy due to the interaction in
eqn~\ref{interaction}, and the other one down by the same amount, increasing the
level separation by $2\nu$. Upon reversal of externally applied fields, the
directions of the level shifts change and a level separation of $-2\nu$ obtains
compared to the levels in absence of the interaction. Comparing the two
perturbations of the level spacing, a $4\nu$ difference (together with its error
bars) can be quoted as the main result of molecular-beam symmetry-violation
experiments.

The spacing between the levels in the preceding paragraph can be measured by a
simple Rabi oscillation technique. Consider two states, $\ket p$ and $\ket q$,
separated by an energy $\hbar\omega_0$. Subjecting the two-state system to an
oscillating field of form $\hbar b e^{-i\omega t}$, the population transfer
probability is given by \cite[eqn~V.10]{ramsey1956molecular}
%
$$
P_{p,q}=
\frac{(2b)^2}{(\omega_0-\omega)^2+(2b)^2}\sin^2
\left\{
\frac{t}{2}
\sqrt{(\omega_0-\omega)^2+(2b)^2}
\right\}.
$$
%
We see the transfer is maximised for a resonant field, $\omega=\omega_0$, and
the full width at half maximum (\FWHM) is given by $\Delta\omega=4b$. For a
resonant $\pi/2$ pulse of duration $t=\pi/2b$, the \FWHM\ will be
$\Delta\omega=2\pi/t$, and the longer the precession period $t$, the more
precisely we can determine the resonant frequency $\omega_0$. In principle, an
infinitely precise measurement could be obtained by simply extending the length
$l$ of the precession region. In practice, however, this meets with the
following problems: (1) the beam intensity decay to zero if the beamline is too
long; (2) we may want to measure the precession in a region where the
oscillating field cannot be applied; (3) it is difficult to maintain uniform
externally applied fields over an extended length.

It turns out that the oscillating field does not need to extend over the entire
length of the precession region. In Ramsey's resonance scheme, \emph{separated}
oscillating fields are only applied in two small regions at the beginning and at
the end of the interaction region, each applying a $\pi/2$ pulse. The resulting
\FWHM\ is about 40\%\ smaller that that obtained in a simple Rabi oscillation
scheme, and the resonances are less broadened by the non-uniformity of the fixed
field. \cite{PhysRev.78.695}

\paragraph{The TlF molecule}

TlF is a suitable system to look for $P$ and $T$ violating interactions for a
number of reasons: a molecular beam of thallium fluoride can readily be obtained
by following the lead of a number of previously published papers (see next
section); the properties of the molecular states and transitions are available
in literature; the thallium nucleus being very heavy, the Schiff moment is
correspondingly relatively large (as per eqn~\ref{schiff}) and contains an
unpaired proton. \cite{sandars1967measurability} Furthermore, the existence of
an optical cycling transition in TlF \cite{hunter2012prospects} allows for
near-unity detection efficiency despite a limited ability to collect and detect
individual photons. The large number of photons that can be scattered off a
single molecule could potentially also be used to slow or trap molecules in an
attempt to increase experimental sensitivity beyond that projected for the
initial version of the \CENTREX\ experiment.

\paragraph{Previous experiments}

The first $P,T$-violation experiment using a molecular beam of thallium fluoride
was done in Oxford in 1969 \cite{harrison1969experimental}, the result being
reported as $\nu=0.08\pm0.11\Hz$, or, in terms of proton \EDM, as
$|d_\text{p}|=(7\pm9)\times10^{-21}e\cm$. They employed the triple resonance
scheme: first, the $J=1$ molecules were transferred from $m_J=0$ to $m_J=\pm1$.
The second resonance which changed the Tl nuclear spin projection from $\pm1/2$
to $\mp1/2$ is not directly observable; however, after the molecules are
transferred back to $m_J=0$, the amount of the non-observable spin flips can be
estimated and related to the desired frequency shifts $4\nu$.

About a decade later, Hinds and Sandars \cite{hinds1980experiment} reported a
similar experiment giving the null result of $\nu=11\pm42\mHz$ or
$|d_\text{e}|=(1.8\pm7)\times10^{-21}e\cm$. Looking for $P$
and $T$ violating interactions in $^{205}$TlF, they tried to measure shifts of
the resonant frequency which depends on the relative directions of externally
applied parallel electric and magnetic fields. The experiment used a beam of TlF
produced by vapors effusing from a hot oven through a state-selecting region
that approximately transmits only molecules with $J=1$, $m_J=-1$,
$m_\text{Tl}=+\nicefrac{1}{2}$, $m_\text{F}=-\nicefrac{1}{2}$. In the
interaction region, separated oscillating magnetic fields induce some molecules
to flip the Tl spin to $m_\text{Tl}=-\nicefrac{1}{2}$. Finally, a second state
selector transmits only the molecules without the spin flip, and the final beam
intensity is a sensitive measure of the shift in the resonance given that the
10~m separation of the oscillating coils results in a 20~Hz linewidth. However,
the experiment gave an accuracy comparable to the experiment in
\cite{harrison1969experimental} because of an unexpectedly large reduction of
the resonance signal and high background rate.

Using a similar setup, Wilkening, Ramsey, and Larson \cite{wilkening1984search}
reduced the error bars to $\nu=8\pm21\mHz$ or
$|d_\text{e}|=(1.3\pm2.0)\times10^{-21}e\cm$. In their experiment, the thermal
beam of TlF passes through an electrostatic quadrupole field that focuses the
$J=1$, $m_J=0$ state, leaving the others unselected. Thereafter, a `subsidiary
resonance' region transfers the molecules into the state $m_J=-1$,
$m_\text{Tl}=-\nicefrac{1}{2}$, $m_\text{F}=-\nicefrac{1}{2}$. However, instead
of using oscillating fields to do so, it relies on adiabatic transitions that
conveniently happen to transfer population into the desired state. As was
the case in the preceding experiment, the main interaction region causes the Tl
nuclear spin to flip. Finally, second subsidiary resonance region transfers the
flipped molecules to the $m_J=-1$ hyperfine state which is defocused by the
second quadrupole, causing a change in the beam intensity measured at the end of
the beamline.

Cho, Sangster, and Hinds \cite{cho1989tenfold, cho1991search} found the upper
bound $\nu=(1.4\pm2.4)\times10^{-4}\Hz$ or
$|d_\text{e}|=(-1.4\pm2.4)\times10^{-25}e\cm$. Again, a quadrupole selects
$J=0$, $m_J=0$ molecules, which subsequently transition to one of the $m_J=\pm1$
sublevels in a combination of static and \RF\ electric fields, either
$(m_J,m_\text{Tl},m_\text{F})=(-1,+\nicefrac{1}{2},-\nicefrac{1}{2})$ or
$(+1,-\nicefrac{1}{2},+\nicefrac{1}{2})$. Then, separated oscillating magnetic
fields in the presence of a strong electric field induce a nuclear spin flip in
Tl, observed by a second state selector and quadrupole filter. The improved
upper bound was due to a more intense and rotationally colder beam of TlF
monomers, and due to an additional reversal, switching between the two hyperfine
states as listed above.
