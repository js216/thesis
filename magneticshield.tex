\section{Magnetic field control}

The magnetic field control system consists of the magnetic shield, the set of
shim coils, and the internal and external magnetometers. Together, these
components allow for interaction region operation with low levels of ambient
magnetic background present. This section will discuss the design requirements
of the system, present the physical details of its implementation, as well as
the performance achieved with the present shielding system.

While the design, building, and characterization of the shield can be done in a
straightforward manner by emulating past work, the underlying science is
frequently not obvious and is often left out of the literature on the topic.
This is understandable, since understanding how the shield works is rarely of
interest to the experimentalist desirous of getting an atomic or molecular beam
operational. In what follows, I will also have to leave several lines of
exploration unfinished as they are not immediately required in order to proceed
with the \CENTREX\ experiment. However, it may be a starting point for future
work requiring higher performance.

\subsection{Physical structure}

The magnetic shield consists of four concentric cylindrical layers with open
ends (See Figs.~\ref{concentric} and \ref{longitudinal}). Three of these are
built by wrapping metglas foil around PVC pipes, while the fourth, innermost
layer has been welded out of two sheets of mu-metal rolled into a tubular form,
and annealed. The mu-metal tube, being 1 mm thick, is relatively fragile; in
order to limit its motion, it is sandwiched between the hard plastic PVC tubes
of the inner metglas layer and that of the shim coils. The inner two metglas
layers are spaced apart using small-diameter PVC tubing; the same method is
employed to suspend the glass vacuum chamber of the interaction region centered
inside the shim coils tube. The outermost tube is mounted on an 80/20 frame
using a set of seven aluminum semi-circular plates.

\paragraph{Layers, spacers, mounting}

The three metglas layers are all built in the same way. First, the plastic tube
(provided by the Harrison Machine \&\ Plastic Corp., Garrettsville, OH) is cut
to size with a handsaw if necessary (see Table \ref{shieldlayers} for layer
lengths and material details) and roughly cleaned with water and paper towels on
the outside and, as far as possible, also on the inside to remove the dirt it
comes with, probably due to outdoors storage. Then, I cut $8\times12$ pieces of
metglas ($7\times12$ for the 16" layer) to the length required to wrap around
the tube with approx.\ 1" of overlap. The pieces are secured to the PVC tube and
to each other with a few short pieces of Kapton tape (McMaster p.n.\ 7648A63) so
that they do not fall down during assembly.

The prepared and cleaned tubes are put on a stand with wheels under them so they
can be rotated, facilitating the circumferential wrapping of the metglas foil.
The first piece of tape is wrapped about an inch from the end of the tube;
leaving this much bare PVC helps with handling the approx.\ 100--200 lb tubes.
The second piece is wrapped so as to have about an inch of overlap with the
first, and the remaining six pieces are attached analogously. Once the first
layer is done, the wrapping resumes at the position of the first metglas piece.
This procedure is repeated for a total of 12 layers total. (Due to axial and
circumferential overlaps, the true thickness of the shield is not perfectly
homogeneous, but in practice we find that this is unimportant as long as the
overall shielding is sufficiently high.)

To secure the metglas pieces and prevent them from coming loose in case the
Kapton tape fails, the entire tube is wrapped in plastic foil. Following that,
there are 12 turns of degaussing wire wrapped in a toroidal fashion around the
tube. The wire is 22 or 24 gauge lacquered copper wire, sufficiently thick for
approx.\ 2 amps of degaussing current. The 12 turns are uniformly distributed
around the circumference of the tube; we believe this may be important for the
performance of the degaussing. On the outermost (20") tube only, we also have a
solenoidal coil with approx 1 turn per in spacing for rough cancellation of
axial fields. (Given the metglas wrapping geometry, this solenoidal coil would
not be effective for degaussing.) Finally, each tube is again wrapped in plastic
wrap to secure the degaussing and (for 20" tube only) the solenoidal coil.

The mu-metal layer does not, of course, require wrapping with metglas, only the
degaussing coil and the outer plastic foil wrap. However, care must be taken to
reinforce the insulation of the degaussing wire at the ends of the cylinder; the
sharp edges can easily strip the magnet wire coating and short the turns of the
wire to the conductive cylinder, which does not happen with the PVC tubes. I
used heat shrink tubing to do this, but it required cutting the wire at each
turn and at both ends, adding insulation, soldering the wires back together and
heating the insulation to make it shrink and adhere. It would have been better
to use a wire with more durable insulation instead.

\paragraph{Shim coils}

The set of shim coils consists of 16 segments of three dimensions each (see
Fig.~\ref{shimcoils}). The two transverse dimensions ($B_x$ and $B_y$) are
implemented as pairs of one-loop saddle coils (8" by 60\textdegree each), while
the axial field is controlled by simple circular current loops (spaced 8" apart
from each other). This gives a total of $5\times16=80$ degrees of freedom. The
22g magnet wires are fixed in place by being glued into a series of grooves on
the surface of the 14" PVC tube using neutral-cure RTV silicone sealant (Dow
Dowsil 737) and wrapped in plastic foil for added protection. During the curing
of the silicone, the wires are held in place by Kapton tape, which is removed
after the silicone hardens.

The size of the tube makes it impractical to use standard milling machines.
While the circular grooves can easily be cut using a hand saw, the axial ones
were done on a custom-made setup (Fig.~\ref{grooves}) involving a hand drill
clamped on a 80/20 frame, which in turn is mounted using linear bearings to a
larger frame. The setup is not as rigid as a true milling machine would be,
which is why it is important to use a constant force while moving the carriage
forward to avoid cutting wavy grooves. I used a system of ropes, pulleys, and
weights to achieve satisfactory results.

With 80 different pair of wires (twisted into twisted pairs using a hand
drill), cable management is essential so as not to confuse the cables with each
other. For each coil, a linear groove is cut from one end of the tube to
the location of the coil; thus all coils can be connected to at one end of the
shield. There, the sets of wire pairs belonging to each dimension are bundled
together into five bundles of 16 pairs using zip ties; each wire pair is
temporarily labelled by a numbered piece of Kapton tape at the end. For ease of
connecting and disconnecting, these wires are attached to adapter boards that
are permanently attached to the 80/20 support frame of the shield. The adaptor
boards bundle four pairs of wires into one RJ45 jack to enable using standard
patch cables for ease of connection.

\paragraph{Support structure}

The considerable total weight of the magnetic shield (780 lbs of plastic, plus
metglas, mu-metal, and other materials) requires a sufficiently strong support
structure. In our case, it is assembled from 80/20 rails (profile number 3030)
and placed on eight caster wheels {McM p.n.\ 9813T71) so the setup can in
principle be moved by one person. The support structure consists of three
four-foot segments mounted together to form a rectangular box. Rails going
across the top of the box hold aluminum plates (1/4" thick) with semi-circular
cutouts. To keep the plates from damaging the delicate outer metglas layer, the
sharp corners of the cutouts are protected with hard nylon tape.


\subsection{Operation and measurements}

The characterization and use of the shield is an iterative process requiring a
repeated sequence of three operations: magnetic field measurement, degaussing,
and shimming.

I distinguish two types of measurement of the magnetic field of interest to us.
Residual field is the field inside the shield assuming no changes in the
external magnetic conditions. In our four-layer shield, this is the dominant
magnetic field inside the interaction region and it is driven primarily by the
interaction between the magnetization of the ferromagnetic shield layers with
the external magnetic field. Despite our best attempts at degaussing, these
residual fields are typically of the order of 50--200~$\mu$G in the center of
the shield.

\paragraph{Field measurement}

During shield characterization, the primary measurement is of the field in the
central region of the shield. During the use of the shield in a beam-line
experiment, this region is difficult to access; it is also the region of
greatest interest since all molecules included in the experiment pass through
it. During characterization I attached a $4"\times4"$ aluminium rectangular tube
by suspending it from them shield mounting structure at each end. I used a
system of pulleys to pull the sensor carriage using a stepper motor and a rotary
encoder to measure the position of the sensor along the length of tube. The
sensor itself (Bartington Mag03MS-100) is a 3-channel fluxgate magnetometer with
a measuring range of 1~G and a typical electronic offset of several hundred
microgauss. (The specified noise level of 0.1~$\mu$G/$\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ at 1
Hz, integrated over the 3~kHz bandwidth of the sensor, is below the mechanical
stability of the sensor translation setup with a typical repeatability of approx
2~$\mu$G.)

The electronic offset of the sensor and its associated electronics is
significant. It is specified as 50~$\mu$G, with a temperature coefficient of
1~$\mu$G/\textdegree C, although in practice offsets of several hundred
microgauss are typical. The offset calibration is done by taking a measurement
and immediately repeating it with the sensor rotated upside down. This is
accomplished by mounting the sensor in a short rectangular aluminum tube with
enough plastic tape on the outside so that the sensor carriage fits tightly inside
the long square tube going through the center of the shield. After a measurement
is done, the sensor is pulled out of the long tube and reinserted rotated
upside down. The accuracy of the offset calibration then depends on how
well-centered the sensor is in the carriage, how much the field inside the
shield changes during the time it takes to perform the calibration, how much
the electronic offset itself changes in the same time, and how perfectly square the
cross-sections of the long tube and the carriage are.
