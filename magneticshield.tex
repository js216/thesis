The magnetic field control system consists of the magnetic shield, the set of
shim coils, and the internal and external magnetometers (see
Fig.~\fref{simple_diagram}). Together, these components allow for interaction
region operation with low levels of ambient magnetic background present. This
section will discuss the design requirements of the system, present the physical
details of its implementation, as well as the performance achieved with the
present shielding system.

While the design, building, and characterization of the shield can be done in a
straightforward manner by emulating past work, the underlying science is
frequently not obvious and is often left out of the literature on the topic.
This is understandable, since understanding how the shield works is rarely of
interest to the experimentalist desirous of getting an atomic or molecular beam
operational. In what follows, I will also have to leave several lines of
exploration unfinished as they are not immediately required in order to proceed
with the \CENTREX\ experiment. However, it may be a starting point for future
work requiring higher performance.

\subsection{Design requirements}
\label{shield:requirements}

The effect of magnetic field imperfections has been discussed in detail in
section \ref{systematics:magnetic}. In the following, I will summarize the
requirements on the static residual field and the magnetic noise.

The static residual field, so called since it remains even after our best
attempt at degaussing and shimming of the field, was the primary consideration
in designing this magnetic shield. In order to suppress its effect below the
level of the statistical sensitivity of our experiment, the $B_y$ (``vertical'')
component of the field (see Fig.~\fref{simple_diagram} for axes) has to satisfy
%
\begin{equation}
   \label{By_criterion}
   \langle B_y\rangle < 10~\mu\mathrm{G},
\end{equation}
%
averaged over the volume covered by the molecular beam between the two \RF\
coils. We do not identify any constraints on the other two components of the
residual field, but will confirm experimentally that they do not have a
measurable effect on our results.

The Schiff moment signal is sensitive at first order only to noise in $B_x$, the
component parallel to the electric field, and affects the other two components
only quadratically. With our experimental parameters, we were aiming to achieve
a dynamic shielding sufficient to suppress the lab magnetic noise to below
approx.\ 50~pG, integrated over a 1~$\mu$Hz bandwidth (approx.\ 300~h
integration time), i.e.\ 50~nG/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ around 25~Hz, in order to
achieve the projected sensitivity of our experiment. With the $^{205}$Tl
and $^9$F magnetic moments of order 1~kHz/G, the 50~pG constraint corresponds to
a noise of $\approx50$~nHz as required by the planned experimental sensitivity
of \CENTREX.

\subsection{Theory}

In this section, I will explain the factors that determine the ability of a
magnetic shield to attenuate external magnetic fields. We first consider a
simple model of the shield as a paramagnetic cylinder placed in an external
field and derive an expression for the field inside. Then, we expand the model
to consider what happens to the shield during the `degaussing' or equilibration
procedure (sect.~\ref{better_than_expected}). The last two subsections discuss a
way of thinking about the slow field changes that happen in the shield over
longer periods of time.

\subsubsection{`Paramagnetic' shielding}
\label{paramagnetic_shielding}

Consider a long, hollow cylinder of length $L$, radius $R\ll L$, and thickness
$t\ll R$, made of a linear material with permeability $\mu$, placed in a region
of initially uniform magnetic flux density $\vec B_0$. According to the usual
formulae \cite[eqns~3.29--30]{khriplovich2012cp}, the flux density inside the
shield will be attenuated by the shielding factors
%
\begin{equation}
    \label{singlelayer}
S_\text{T}=\frac{\mu t}{2R},
\quad
S_\text{L}\approx\frac{2\mu tR^{1/2}}{L^{3/2}},
\end{equation}
%
referring to the transverse ($S_\text{T}$) and longitudinal ($S_\text{L}$)
shielding, respectively. For an $n$-layer shield where each layer has a radius
$R_i$, length $L_i$, and shielding factors $S_\text{T}^i$, $S_\text{L}^i$, the
formulae can be generalized to \cite[eqns~3.32--33]{khriplovich2012cp}
%
\begin{equation}
    \label{multiplelayers}
S_\text{T}=
S_\text{T}^n\prod_{i=1}^{n-1}S_\text{T}^i
\left[1-\left(\frac{R_i}{R_{i+1}}\right)^2\right],
\quad
S_\text{L}=
S_\text{L}^n\prod_{i=1}^{n-1}S_\text{L}^i
\left(1-\frac{L_i}{L_{i+1}}\right).
\end{equation}
%
One must merely keep in mind that formulae
\ref{singlelayer}--\ref{multiplelayers} are intended to be used for a linear
material. To a degree, that is true even of highly ferromagnetic materials (see
example in Fig.~\fref{numlayers}), as long as we are very careful about how we
define the permeability $\mu$ (skip to section \ref{degaussing_hysteresis} for
the relevant discussion). In particular, we need to distinguish the static
shielding factor (attenuation of static fields following degaussing, see
sect.~\ref{better_than_expected}) from the dynamic shielding factor (attenuation
of small time-varying field perturbations).

    at a low frequency (around 10~Hz). \part{a} Along the central axis of the
    shield, as a function of position. \part{b} The average shielding factor in

    $\mu=67,300$, $2R=16$", and each layer is 18~$\mu$m thick.}
  \label{numlayers}
\end{figure}

In the following, we will consider two idealized cases in more detail. Looking
at the shielding inside a single highly permeable spherical shell, we will
interpret the shielding effect in terms of `$\cos\theta$' surface current
distributions which serve to oppose the lab magnetic field. Afterwards, we solve
for the field distributions of an infinite-length cylindrical shell in order to
derive the transverse shielding factor formula stated above in
eqn~\ref{singlelayer}.

\paragraph{Spherical shell shield}

Consider a spherical shell of inner and outer radii $a,b$ respectively, made of
a material with relative permeability $\mu$. The shell is placed in a uniform
external field $H_0$.
%
Introducing a magnetic scalar potential $\Phi_\text{M}$, where $\vec
H=-\nabla\Phi_\text{M}$, the magnetic field everywhere can be computed from the
expressions \cite[eqns~5.117--118]{jackson1998classical}
%
\begin{align}
   \label{spherical_potential}
   \Phi_\text{M}=
    \begin{dcases*}
       \delta_1r\cos\theta&\quad for $r<a$\\
       \left(\beta_1+\frac{\gamma_1}{r^3}\right)r\cos\theta&\quad for $a<r<b$\\
       \left(-H_0+\frac{\alpha_1}{r^3}\right)r\cos\theta&\quad for $r>b$,
    \end{dcases*}
\end{align}
%
where the coefficients $\alpha_1,\beta_1,\gamma_1,\delta_1$ are given by
\cite[eqn~5.120]{jackson1998classical}:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{sphere_coeffs}
   \begin{split}
      \alpha_1A/H_0&=(2\mu+1)(\mu-1)(b^3-a^3)\\
      \beta_1 A/H_0&=-3(2\mu+1)\\
      \gamma_1A/H_0&=-3a^3(\mu-1)\\
      \delta_1A/H_0&=-9\mu\\
      A        &=(2\mu+1)(\mu+2)-2(a^3/b^3)(\mu-1)^2.
   \end{split}
\end{align}
%
We see that the field $E=-\nabla\Phi_\text{M}=-\delta_1\hat{z}$ inside the
cavity ($r<a$) is uniform and has the magnitude $-\delta_1\propto\mu^{-1}$ for
$\mu\gg1$ as expected for a magnetic shield:
%
\[
    -\delta_1=\frac{9\mu H_0}{(2\mu+1)(\mu+2)-2(a^3/b^3)(\mu-1)^2}=
    \frac{1/2}{(1-a^3/b^3)\mu}+O(\mu^{-2})
    \quad\text{for large $\mu$.}
\]
%
In the opposite limit of a small
$\mu\ll1$, the field inside scales as $-\delta_1\propto\mu$:
%
\[
    -\delta_1=\frac{\mu/2}{1-a^3/b^3}+O(\mu^2)
    \quad\text{for small $\mu$.}
\]
%
It is interesting to observe that the
shielding is identical for materials with permeability $\mu$ as for $1/\mu$. Of
course the shielding effect for most real materials with $\mu<1$ would be
negligible since the permeability is very close to unity. However, the
superdiamagnetism ($\mu=0$) of superconductors has been exploited for
constructing magnetic shields
\cite{claycomb1999superconducting,ohta1999neuromagnetic,al2021study}, although
with the disadvantages of requiring cryogenic temperatures.  For a non-permeable
material, $\mu=1$, there is no shielding.

Furthermore, we can calculate the magnetization of the shell, given that it is
made of a linear material. We have, for $a<r<b$,
%
\begin{align}
   \label{magnetization_in_sphere}
   \vec M&=(\mu-1)\vec H\nonumber\\
   &=-(\mu-1)\nabla\Phi_\text{M}\nonumber\\
   &=(\mu-1)\left[-\beta_1\hat{\vec
   z}+\frac{\gamma_1}{r^3}\left(2\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec
   r}+\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta}\right)\right].
\end{align}
%
Evidently the magnetization (as well as the field inside the material of the
shell) consists of a uniform term proportional to $\beta_1$, as well as a
magnetic dipole term proportional to $\gamma_1$. Due to the overall factor of
$\mu-1$, there is of course no magnetization for a non-permeable shell;
moreover, \emph{there is no shielding without magnetization.}

It can be enlightening to think about the shield in terms of an equivalent
current distribution. Given some distribution of magnetization $\vec M(\vec x)$
at position $\vec x$ inside a volume $V$ bounded by a surface $S$, the
corresponding magnetic vector potential is
\cite[eqn~5.103]{jackson1998classical}
%
\begin{equation}
   \label{potential_vs_magnetization}
   \vec A(\vec x)=
   \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}
   \int_V\frac{\nabla'\times\vec M(\vec x')}{|\vec x-\vec x'|}\d^3x'
   +
   \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}
   \oint_S\frac{\vec M(\vec x')\times\hat{\vec n}'}{|\vec x-\vec x'|}da',
\end{equation}
%
where $\vec n$ is the normal to the surface $S$ at point $\vec x$. The first
term is a volume current $\vec J=\nabla\times\vec M$, while the second is a
surface current $\vec K=\vec M\times\hat{\vec n}$.

The volume current vanishes since the magnetization is proportional to $\vec
H=-\nabla\Phi_\text{M}$ and $\nabla\times(\nabla f)=0$ for any $f$.
Alternatively, by Amp\`ere's law we have $\nabla\times\vec
M=(\mu-1)\nabla\times\vec H=0$ where there are no free currents.

The normal vector is $\hat{\vec n}=\pm\hat{\vec r}$ on the outer (inner)
surface, so the surface current is
%
\begin{align*}
   \vec K&=\pm(\mu-1)\left[-\beta_1\left(\hat{\vec z}\times\hat{\vec
   r}\right)+\frac{\gamma_1}{r^3}\left(2\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec
   r}+\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta}\right)\times\hat{\vec r}\right]\\
   &=\pm(\mu-1)\left[-\beta_1\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}-\frac{\gamma_1}{r^3}\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}\right]\\
   &=\mp(\mu-1)\left[\beta_1+\frac{\gamma_1}{r^3}\right]\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi},
\end{align*}
%
where of course $r=a$ on the inner surface, and $r=b$ on the outer. The
paramagnetic shell shield can thus be seen as two counter-rotating
`$\sin\theta$' surface currents on the surfaces of the shell. Inserting the
coefficients from \ref{sphere_coeffs}, we see that
\begin{equation}
   \label{sphere_currents}
   \begin{split}
      \vec
      K_\text{inner}&=-\frac{9\mu}{A}(\mu-1)H_0\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}\\
      \vec
      K_\text{outer}&=\frac{3}{A}(\mu-1)\big[(2\mu+1)+(a^3/b^3)(\mu-1)\big]H_0\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi},
   \end{split}
\end{equation}
%
with the same $A$ as in eqn~\ref{sphere_coeffs}. The internal field in the
cavity is $|H(r<a)|=-\delta_1$, so
%
\begin{align*}
   \vec K_\text{inner}=-(\mu-1)H(r<a)\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}
\end{align*}
%
The field $\vec H(r<a)$ inside the cavity points in the same direction as the
external uniform field $\vec H_0$, but is attenuated by the spherical shield.
The `magnetization current' of its inner surface `rotates' around the sphere
(i.e., flows in a circumferential matter) in the opposite sense to the field and
thus causes the attenuation of the field. 

In the limit of infinite permeability, the two currents become
%
\begin{align*}
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}\vec K_\text{inner}=-\frac{9/2}{1-a^3/b^3}H_0\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}
   ;\quad
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}\vec K_\text{outer}=3\frac{1+a^3/(2b^3)}{1-a^3/b^3}H_0\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}.
\end{align*}
%
The counter-rotating current on the external surface of the shell works
\emph{against} the shielding since it increases the internal field, but its
magnitude is smaller than that of the inner current so the shielding still
works, i.e.\ $-\delta_1\propto\mu^{-1}$:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{high_paramagnetic}
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}\frac{\vec K_\text{inner}}{\vec
   K_\text{outer}}=-\frac{3}{2+a^3/b^3}
   ;\quad
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}(-\delta_1)=\frac{9/2}{\mu(1-a^3/b^3)}H_0.
\end{align}
%
In the limit of an infinitesimally thin shell ($a/b\to1$), the two currents are
equal and opposite and the shielding effect disappears. Of course in that limit,
there is no magnetized material, reinforcing the notion that there is no
shielding without magnetization.

In the superdiamagnetic limit ($\mu=0$), the shielding is perfect
($-\delta_1=0$) and effected exclusively by the current on the outer surface:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{superdiamagnetic_shell}
   \lim_{\mu\to0}\vec K_\text{inner}=0;\quad
   \lim_{\mu\to0}\vec K_\text{outer}=-\frac{3}{2}H_0\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}.
\end{align}
%

\paragraph{Cylindrical shell shield}

Rather than a spherical shield, the geometry of our experiment can be better
described by an infinite cylindrical shell of inner (outer) radius $a$ ($b$),
placed in a uniform external field $\vec H_0=H_0\hat{\vec x}$, such that the
field is perpendicular to the long axis of the cylinder $\hat{\vec z}$. The
calculation of the resulting magnetization everywhere, and in particular the
shielding inside the cylinder, is the subject of Jackson
\cite{jackson1998classical} problem 5.14.

As was the case with the spherical shell, again we can find the magnetic field
$\vec H$ from a magnetic scalar potential via $\vec H=-\nabla\Phi_\text{M}$
since $\nabla\times\vec H=0$ by Amp\`ere's law. Since
$\nabla\cdot\vec H\propto\nabla\cdot\vec B=0$, this implies that the potential
satisfies the Laplace equation, $\nabla^2\Phi_\text{M}=0$. This is a
two-dimensional problem with the general solution
\cite[eqn~21.31]{riley2006mathematical}
%
\[
   \Phi_\text{M}=(C_0\ln r+D_0)+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(A_n\cos
   n\theta+B_n\sin n\theta)(C_nr^n+D_nr^{-n}).
\]
%
Since we are solving the Laplace's equation in a region containing the origin,
we have to take $C_0=0$ \cite[sect~21.3.1]{riley2006mathematical}. Furthermore,
the constant term $D_0$ will not contribute to $\vec H=-\nabla\Phi_\text{M}$
and we can ignore it. By symmetry, there are no $\sin n\theta$ terms, and no $\cos
n\theta$ for $n>1$. Inside the sphere, we cannot have $D_n$ terms since they would
diverge at $r=0$, and outside we cannot have $C_n$ terms which diverge as
$r\to\infty$. The following terms survive:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{cylindrical_potential}
   \Phi_\text{M}=
    \begin{dcases*}
       \delta_1r\cos\theta&\quad for $r<a$\\
       (\beta_1+\gamma_1/r^2)r\cos\theta&\quad for $a<r<b$\\
       (-H_0+\alpha_1/r^2)r\cos\theta&\quad for $r>b$.
    \end{dcases*}
\end{align}
%
The relevant boundary conditions are, as for the spherical case, that
$H_\theta=-(1/r)\partial\Phi_\text{M}/\partial\theta$ and
$B_r\propto\mu(r)H_r=-\mu(r)\partial\Phi_\text{M}/\partial r$ be continuous
across the interfaces at $r=a,b$, noting that $\mu(r)=\mu_0$ outside the
material, and $\mu\mu_0$ inside given the relative permeability $\mu$ of the
shell. The boundary conditions give the system of equations
%
\begin{align}
   \label{cyl_system}
   \alpha_1-b^2\beta_1-\gamma_1&=b^2H_0\\
   \alpha_1+\mu b^2\beta_1-\mu\gamma_1&=-b^2H_0\\
   a^2\beta_1+\gamma_1-a^2\delta_1&=0\\
   \mu a^2\beta_1-\mu\gamma_1-a^2\delta_1&=0,
\end{align}
%
which should be compared to the system corresponding to the spherical shell
given in \cite[eqn~5.120]{jackson1998classical}. The cylindrical potential in
eqn~\ref{cylindrical_potential} only differs from the spherical case in
eqn~\ref{spherical_potential} in that instead of a $r^{-3}$ dipole potential, we
have $r^{-2}$. The system has the solution
%
\begin{align}
   \label{cyl_coeffs}
   \begin{split}
      \alpha_1A/H_0&=-(a^2-b^2)(\mu^2-1)\\
      \beta_1 A/H_0&=-2(\mu+1)\\
      \gamma_1A/H_0&=-2b^2(\mu-1)\\
      \delta_1A/H_0&=-4\mu\\
      A        &=(\mu+1)^2-(a^2/b^2)(\mu-1)^2.
   \end{split}
\end{align}
%
The shielding factor (external field divided by the internal field) is therefore
$\text{SF}=H_0/(-\delta_1)=A/(4\mu)$. In the limit of strong paramagnetic or
diamagnetic permeability, we get the first-order expressions
%
\[
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}\text{SF}=\frac{\mu t}{2R}
   ,\quad
   \lim_{\mu\to0}\text{SF}=\frac{(1/\mu)t}{2R},
\]
%
defining shield thickness $t=b-a$ and radius $R\approx a\approx b$. The
$\mu\to\infty$ case corresponds to the expression given in
eqn~\ref{singlelayer}, while the $\mu\to0$ case illustrates the curious fact
that the same shielding obtains for $\mu$ as for $1/\mu$.


\subsubsection{Static shielding better than expected}
\label{better_than_expected}

As pointed out in ref.~\cite{dickerson2012high}, the dynamic and static shielding would
be identical in case of a purely linear material. This does not apply, however,
to shielding static fields with nonlinear materials such as mu-metal and
metglas. For example, for a single-layer 14" diameter ($R=17.8$~cm) shield of
length 3.5~m with $\mu=70,000$ and $t=0.2$~mm, we expect from
eqn~\ref{singlelayer} that $S_\text{T}\approx40$ and $S_\text{L}\approx2$. Thus
our roughly 400~mG lab magnetic field should be attenuated down to 10~mG
(200~mG) if oriented perpendicular (parallel) to the shield.

In practice (see example in Fig.~\fref{beforeafter}), the field inside a shield
can be greatly reduced by driving a sinusoidal magnetic field around it with a
gradually decreasing envelope. We call this procedure `degaussing the shield',
by analogy to the procedure used to protect World War II ships against magnetic
mines \cite{hook1941medical}. After degaussing, we find residual fields much
smaller than expected (approx.\ 0.2~mG and 4~mG, resp.) from simple paramagnetic
theory.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/beforeafter.png}
    \caption[Residual fields before and after degaussing.]{Magnetic fields along
    the central axis of a single layer cylindrical magnetic shield, constructed

    dotted lines indicate the position of the overlaps between the eight

    degaussing (difficult to predict theoretically).  \part{b} After degaussing.
    Simple model (eqn.~\ref{singlelayer}) would predict 0.2~mG and 4~mG for the
    transverse and longitudinal components.}
  \label{beforeafter}
\end{figure}

A common explanation for the decrease in the internal magnetic field is as
follows. The sinusoidal magnetic field of the `degaussing' coil drives the
ferromagnetic material into saturation. Regardless of the initial magnetization
of the material, it will move around its hysteresis curve (see
Fig.~\fref{degaussing_theory}) and lose all memory of the initial condition. The
external magnetizing current that is passed through the degaussing coils is
applied symmetrically around zero (necessitating some form of DC-blocking, such
as a transformer or capacitive coupling), so when the amplitude of the
degaussing current is gradually decreased to zero, no net magnetization will
remain (e.g., \cite[sect.~3.4.2]{khriplovich2012cp}).
Ref.~\cite{thiel2009demagnetization} calls the resulting state of minimized
internal energy a `demagnetized state'.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=.5\textwidth]{figs/mathematica/jiles_atherton.pdf}
  \includegraphics[width=.5\textwidth]{figs/mathematica/jiles_atherton_offs.pdf}
    \caption[Magnetization curves from Jiles--Atherton model.]{Magnetization
    curves, found by numerically integrating eqn~\ref{jiles_atherton_eqn} with
    the parameters given in the figure. See page \pageref{jiles_atherton_par}
    for a discussion of the model. \part{Left} Degaussing: naive picture,
    an oscillating applied field $H_0$ with no offset decreases till the
    material spirals into the origin of the $M$--$H$ or $B$--$H$ space
    (demagnetization). All the \emph{minor loops} are centered around the
    origin. See end of section \ref{degaussing_hysteresis} regarding the
    negative slope. \part{Right} Degaussing means applying the oscillating field
    with decaying amplitude in the presence of an offset (lab) field. As the
    amplitude of the oscillations goes to zero, the material settles on a point
    on the \emph{anhysteretic curve} (dashed line). Notice how the anhysteretic
    curve is much steeper than the minor loops, implying a greater effective
    permeability. See also Fig.~\fref{hysteresis} (\pageref{hysteresis}) for
    curves measured on metglas.}
    \label{degaussing_theory}
\end{figure}

Unfortunately, this explanation only applies for when a single grain of the
material is degaussed in a region with no net external field---hardly applicable
to magnetic shielding. In practice, the shield would be situated in a region
with significant external field, such that the total externally applied field is
\emph{not} symmetric upon current reversal. Similarly, over the course of the decrease
of the current, the magnetization would ideally settle on a value that as much
as possible \emph{opposes} the stationary lab field. Rather than equating
degaussing with a demagnetization process, it would be more appropriate to
consider it a particular case of inducing a non-zero magnetization in the
material through the process of equilibration, of settling into a state of
minimum magnetic energy. In fact, \emph{a perfectly demagnetized shield would be
perfectly useless} until it gets re-magnetized by an external field. (Even
`paramagnetic' shielding, discussed above, relies on the fact that the shield
develops a certain non-zero magnetization when placed in an external magnetic
field.) There is no shielding without magnetization.

Any magnetic shield can be modelled as a suitably magnetized shell surrounding
the region of interest for an experiment. Of course, we cannot in general expect
uniform magnetization to result from a degaussing procedure applied on a
cylindrical shell, especially when shielding transverse fields. The form of the
current distribution obtaining after degaussing is likely determined by a
combination of geometric factors (e.g., constraining the spatial current
distribution to the surfaces of a cylindrical shell), and the details of the
motion of hysteresis loops of a given material.

To find what happens to the magnetization of the shield during degaussing, we
can begin by recalling that any system will likely arrive at the state of lowest
energy it can reach, so long as it has enough activation energy to leave its
current local minimum. To reiterate:

\vspace{1em}

\label{framed_principle}
\noindent\fbox{\parbox{\textwidth}{Our model for the effect of degaussing (and
the resulting large shielding of purely static fields) is that the degaussing
ends with the particular residual magnetization of the shield material that acts
to minimize the total energy in the magnetic field eveywhere in space.}}

\vspace{1em}

Given that the magnetic flux density $B$ corresponds to a
magnetic energy density $u=B^2/2\mu_0$, it is reasonable to expect that the
shield is striving to minimize the magnetic field, though subject to two
constraints.

On the one hand, the magnetic domains are not entirely free to reorient, as they
may get pinned at material imperfections; degaussing helps with this issue by
moving the domain walls to reach a more optimal configuration with respect to
minimizing the total energy. Thus we may expect that the more of these
imperfections, the less effective the degaussing; hence the importance of
annealing magnetic shields and handling them carefully. (The innermost layer,
being closest to the molecules that require shielding, is thereby the most
sensitive to material imperfections.)

On the other hand, given that magnetic field lines are closed loops, the field
inside the shield that opposes an external field eventually loops around in such
a way as to increase the total field energy outside. (This point will be
illustrated in the following section.) I see no reason to conclude that in the
tradeoff between increased field outside and the decreased field inside (or the
other way around), the shield would settle at a zero magnetic field inside the
shield. In other words, it is likely that some nonzero residual field has to
remain inside the shield.  To predict its magnitude, and thus the theoretical
limit on the static performance of a degaussed shield, we need to look for the
particular current distribution (or, equivalently, magnetization), that
minimizes the total energy for a given shield geometry.

In the following section, I consider some models of how ferromagnetic
shields after degaussing may exhibit better shielding than that expected from a
paramagnetic argument of section \ref{paramagnetic_shielding}.

\subsubsection{Degaussing toy models}

After degaussing, ferromagnetic shields are in a state of nonzero magnetization.
To model this state of ``frozen in'', or hard-magnet, magnetization, we consider
a fixed current distribution in the simplest geometry: a sphere.

\paragraph{Rotating sphere of charge}

Consider a spherical shell of radius $R$ that carries a uniform surface charge
density $\sigma$ and spins with an angular velocity
$\boldsymbol\omega=\omega\hat{\vec z}$ around some axis $\hat{\vec z}$
\cite[Ex.~5.11]{griffiths2013introduction}.  This is equivalent to a surface
current distribution of the form $\vec
K_0(\theta)=K_0\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi},$ where $K_0=\sigma R\omega$.
This is also equivalent to a stationary solid sphere of radius $R$ and a uniform
magnetic polarization $\vec M$ inside \cite[sect~5.10]{jackson1998classical} if
we make the identification $\vec M=\sigma R\boldsymbol\omega$. Of course, we
expected the magnetization $\vec M$ and the surface current density $\vec K$ to
have the same magnitude, since they are related by $\vec K=\vec M\times\hat{\vec
n}$.

The magnetic vector potential for such a uniformly magnetized sphere is given by
\cite[eqn~5.69]{griffiths2013introduction}
%
\begin{equation}
   \label{rotating_sphere_potential}
   \vec A(r,\theta,\phi)=
    \begin{dcases*}
       \frac{\mu_0M}{3}r\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}&\quad for $r\leq R$\\
       \frac{\mu_0R^3M}{3}\frac{\sin\theta}{r^2}\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}&\quad for
       $r\geq R$,
    \end{dcases*}
\end{equation}
%
where $\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}$ is the unit vector in the azimuthal direction.
Taking the curl of the potential, we find that the magnetic field inside the
sphere is uniform \cite[eqn~5.70]{griffiths2013introduction}:
%
\begin{equation}
   \label{rotating_sphere_field_inside}
   \vec B_\text{in}^{R,M}=\nabla\times\vec A_\text{in}
   =\frac{2\mu_0R\omega\sigma}{3}
   (\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec r}-\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta})
   =\frac{2\mu_0}{3}\vec M,
\end{equation}
%
while the field outside is that of a magnetic dipole:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{rotating_sphere_field_outside}
   \vec B_\text{out}^{R,M}(r,\theta)&=\nabla\times\vec A_\text{out}\nonumber\\&=
   \frac{\mu_0R^3M}{3}\left[\frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\right)\hat{\vec
   r}-\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial
   r}\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\hat{\boldsymbol\theta}\right]
   \nonumber\\&=\frac{\mu_0R^3M}{3r^3}\left(2\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec
   r}+\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta}\right).
\end{align}
%
This is the field of a dipole with a dipole moment \cite[eqn~5.107]{jackson1998classical}
%
\[
   m
   =\frac{4\pi R^3}{3}M
   =\frac{4\pi R^3}{3}K_0
   =\frac{4\pi R^3}{3}\vec \sigma R\omega.
\]

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figs/todo/jackson5_11.png}
    \caption[$B$ and $H$ for a uniformly magnetized sphere. ]{Magnetic fields
    $B$ (left) and $H$ (right) for a uniformly magnetized sphere. Figure
    reproduced from Jackson \cite[Fig.~5.11]{jackson1998classical}.}
  \label{jackson5_11}
\end{figure}

The field is plotted in Fig.~\fref{jackson5_11}. Observe that at $r=R$, both
$\vec B_\text{in}^{R,M}$ and $\vec B_\text{out}^{R,M}$ take the same magnitude,
$|\vec B|=(2/3)\mu_0M$, but the directions of the two vectors are different.
This is as it should be since the field lines of $\vec B$ are closed loops due
to $\nabla\cdot\vec B=0$, but their direction changes suddenly as the field
transitions from the uniform field inside the sphere to the dipole field
outside.

As emphasized in the framed paragraph on page \pageref{framed_principle}, in our
model of degaussing, the total magnetic energy is minimized during degaussing.
Thus we next consider the magnetic energy corresponding to the above magnetic
field distribution.

For a linear material, the total magnetic energy is
\cite[eqn~5.148]{jackson1998classical}
%
\begin{align}
   \label{linear_energy}
   W=\frac{1}{2}\int\vec H\cdot\vec B\;\d^3x.
\end{align}
%
We emphasize that this relation only holds for linear materials, including,
trivially, vacuum, where $\vec B=\mu_0\vec H$. For a general magnetic medium,
including ferromagnetic substances, the total work done by a magnetic field is
instead given by the following integral over \emph{all of space}
\cite[eqn~5.147]{jackson1998classical}
%
\begin{align}
   \label{general_energy}
   u=\int\vec H\cdot\vec\d\vec B,
\end{align}
%
where to find the total energy, \textbf{the integral is done over all space}. In
the present example, we do not have any magnetic material present, so we can use
eqn~\ref{linear_energy} together with the magnetic fields given in
eqns~\ref{rotating_sphere_field_inside} and \ref{rotating_sphere_field_outside}
to find the total magnetic energy density $u^2=B^2/2\mu_0$ when the sphere is
placed in a region with a constant uniform field:
%
\[
   \vec B_0=B_0\hat{\vec z}=B_0(\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec
   r}-\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta}).
\]
%
The magnetic energy density is proportional to:
%
\begin{align}
   u_\text{in}&\propto(\vec B_0+\vec B_\text{in})^2
   =B_0^2+2B_0M\frac{2\mu_0}{3}+M^2\left(\frac{2\mu_0}{3}\right)^2\nonumber\\
   u_\text{out}&\propto(\vec B_0+\vec B_\text{out})^2
   =B_0^2+2B_0M\frac{\mu_0R^3}{3r^3}f(\theta)+M^2\left(\frac{\mu_0R^3}{3r^3}\right)^2g(\theta),\nonumber
\end{align}
%
with angular factors
%
\begin{align}
   f(\theta)&=(\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec r}-\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta})
         \cdot(2\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec r}+\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta})
      =\frac{1+3\cos2\theta}{2}\nonumber\\
   g(\theta)&=(2\cos\theta\;\hat{\vec r}+\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta})^2
      =\frac{5+3\cos2\theta}{2}.\nonumber
\end{align}
%
The total energy inside the sphere is simply $U_\text{in}=(4/3)\pi
R^3u_\text{in}$. The energy outside the sphere is found by integration:
%
\begin{align}
   U_\text{out}=B_0^2&\int\d^3x\nonumber\\
   &+2B_0M\frac{\mu_0R^3}{3}2\pi\int\limits_R^\infty\frac{r^2\d
   r}{r^3}\underbrace{\int\limits_0^\pi f(\theta)\sin\theta\;\d\theta}_0\nonumber\\
   &+M^2\left(\frac{\mu_0R^3}{3}\right)^22\pi\underbrace{\int\limits_R^\infty\frac{r^2\d
   r}{r^6}\int\limits_0^\pi g(\theta)\sin\theta\;\d\theta}_{4/(3R^3)}\nonumber\\
    =B_0^2&\int\d^3x+\frac{8\pi}{27}(\mu_0M)^2R^3.\nonumber
\end{align}

The first term is of course infinite. This makes sense, since it would take an
infinite amount of work to set up a uniform magnetic field throughout all of
space.

Note that while the internal energy can be lowered by choosing a correct value
for $M$, the external energy always increases whenever $M$ is changed from zero.
Thus the lowest-energy configuration is, as remarked at the end of the previous
section, a tradeoff between decreased field (energy) inside and an increased
field (energy) outside. I see no obvious reason why after degaussing, the
internal field should entirely vanish rather than settling on a small but
nonzero value.

To minimize the total energy, consider the derivative
%
\begin{align}
   \frac{\d}{\d M}(U_\text{in}+U_\text{out})
   =\frac{4\pi R^3}{3}\left[2B_0\frac{2\mu_0}{3}+2M\left(\frac{2\mu_0}{3}\right)^2\right]
   +2M\left(\frac{\mu_0R^3}{3}\right)^22\pi\frac{4}{3R^3}.\nonumber
\end{align}
%
Set the derivative to zero and solve for $M$:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{single_shell_optimum}
   \frac{\d}{\d M}(U_\text{in}+U_\text{out})=0
   \quad\to\quad
   M=-\frac{B_0}{\mu_0}.
\end{align}
%
With this value of $M$, the total field inside is (cf.\
eqn~\ref{rotating_sphere_field_inside})
%
\begin{align}
   B=B_0+\frac{2\mu_0}{3}M=\frac{1}{3}B_0.\nonumber
\end{align}
%
Therefore a single shell with a `$\cos\theta$' surface current distribution,
when the current is set to minimize the total magnetic energy, does not shield
the ambient field very effectively. Of course, a realistic magnetic shield is a
shell of nonzero thickness and as such consists of two concentric surfaces. In
the following paragraph, we expand the model to include this observation.

\paragraph{Two `$\cos\theta$' shells}

Let there be two concentric spheres with radii $R_1<R_2$. As in the
previous example, there are three equivalent physical pictures: (1) rotating
shells with uniform surface electric charge densities $\sigma_i$; (2)
solid spheres with uniform magnetic polarizations $M_i$; (3) spherical
shells carrying surface current distributions of form
$K_i\sin\theta\;\hat{\boldsymbol\phi}$, for $i=1,2$. For consistency with the
one-shell case, we will proceed with using the $M_i$ symbols.

We now consider the total (integrated) magnetic energy in three separate regions
of space. Inside the smaller of the two shells, $r\leq R_1$, the magnetic field
is uniform, giving the energy density
%
\[
   U_\text{in2}\propto\frac{4\pi R_1^3}{3}\left(\vec B_0+\vec
   B_\text{in}^{R_1,M_1}+\vec B_\text{in}^{R_2,M_2}\right)^2.
\]
%
In the space between the two shells ($R_1<r<R_2$) we place a material
with the relative permeability $\mu$, which changes the magnetic
energy contained in that region. We need to integrate over the field internal to
the larger sphere, and external to the smaller:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{Uinternal}
   U_\text{mid2}\propto
   \frac{2\pi}{\mu}\int\limits_{R_1}^{R_2}\d r
   \int\limits_0^\pi\d\theta
   \left(\vec B_0+\vec B_\text{out}^{R_1,M_1}+\vec B_\text{in}^{R_2,M_2}\right)^2.
\end{align}
%
Finally, outside the two shells ($r>R_2$) we have
%
\[
   U_\text{out2}\propto
   2\pi\int\limits_{R_2}^\infty\d r
   \int\limits_0^\pi\d\theta
   \left(\vec B_0+\vec B_\text{out}^{R_1,M_1}+\vec B_\text{out}^{R_2,M_2}\right)^2.
\]
%
We need to solve the following minimization problem:
%
\[
   \frac{\d}{\d
   M_i}\left(U_\text{in2}+U_\text{mid2}+U_\text{out2}\right)=0,\quad i=1,2;
\]
%
using Mathematica, the solution is
%
\begin{align}
   \label{magnetization_two_shells}
\begin{split}
   M_1&=-\frac{3(\mu-1)\mu}{(2\mu+1)(\mu+2)-2(R_1/R_2)^3(\mu-1)^2}\frac{B_0}{\mu_0}\\[10pt]
   M_2&=\frac{3\left[(R_1/R_2)^3(\mu-1)^2-(2\mu+1)\right]}{(2\mu+1)(\mu+2)-2(R_1/R_2)^3(\mu-1)^2}\frac{B_0}{\mu_0},
\end{split}
\end{align}
%
and the internal magnetic field is \cite[cf.\ eqn~5.121]{jackson1998classical}
%
\begin{align}
   \label{sphere_shielding}
   B_\text{in2}=
   B_0+\frac{2\mu_0}{3}M_1+\frac{2\mu_0}{3}M_2
   =\frac{3\mu}{(2\mu+1)(\mu+2)-2(R_1/R_2)^3(\mu-1)^2}B_0,
\end{align}
%
which is exactly three times smaller than the field inside the spherical
paramagnetic shield considered in a previous section ($-\delta_1$ in
eqn~\ref{sphere_coeffs}).

For a non-permeable material ($\mu=1$), the magnetizations in
eqn~\ref{magnetization_two_shells} reduce to $M_1=0$ and $M_2=-B_0/\mu_0$. Thus
the current on the inner shell is turned off, and the outer one reproduces the
single-shell situation from eqn~\ref{single_shell_optimum}, where the internal
field is $B_0/3$. This is in contrast to the paramagnetic shield, where there is
no magnetization, no surface currents, and therefore no shielding when $\mu=1$.

In the limit of high permeability, the two magnetizations are
%
\begin{align}
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}M_1=-\frac{3/2}{1-(R_1/R_2)^3}\frac{B_0}{\mu_0};\quad
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}M_2=\frac{3/2}{(R_2/R_1)^3-1}\frac{B_0}{\mu_0}
\end{align}
%
and the internal magnetic field in this limit is
%
\begin{align*}
   \lim_{\mu\to\infty}B_\text{in2}=\frac{3/2}{\mu(1-R_1^3/R_2^3)}B_0.
\end{align*}
%
Just as for the superdiamagnetic shell considered earlier in
eqn~\ref{superdiamagnetic_shell}, the shielding is perfect ($B_\text{in2}=0$)
and effected exclusively by the current on the outer surface when $\mu=0$:
%
\begin{align}
   \lim_{\mu\to0}M_1=0;\quad
   \lim_{\mu\to0}M_2=-\frac{3}{2\mu_0}B_0;\quad
   \lim_{\mu\to0}B_\text{in2}=\frac{3\mu/2}{1-R_1^3/R_2^3}B_0.
\end{align}

The example presented in this section demonstrates a situation where minimizing
the total magnetic energy in and around a magnetic shield yields a lower
internal magnetic field (i.e., better shielding) than we would expect from
simple `paramagnetic' theory involving linear materials. However, it must be
admitted that this example is somewhat contrived, in that it we manually insert
the factor $1/\mu$ into the expression \ref{Uinternal} for the energy density
inside the shielding material, as would be the case of a shell made of a linear
material, while ignoring how this linear material would affect the magnetic
fields. In the following, we consider the behavior of a minimum energy state of
real ferromagnetic materials.

\subsubsection{Hysteresis during degaussing}
\label{degaussing_hysteresis}

Recall that, as stated emphatically in the framed paragraph on page
\pageref{framed_principle}, that our model for the effect of degaussing is that
the degaussing ends with the particular residual magnetization of the shield
material that acts to minimize the total energy in the magnetic field everywhere
in space. In other words, the total magnetic energy is minimized during
degaussing.

In general, finding the minimum-energy magnetization $\vec M$ of a ferromagnet
placed in an external field $\vec B_0$ is a very challenging task. In this
section, I will review theoretical expressions for a ferromagnet with and
without domain wall pinning from the theories of Weiss \cite{weiss1907hypothese}
and Jiles--Atherton \cite{jiles1984theory}, respectively. Afterwards, I relate
the two situations to the behavior of a magnetic shield during and after
degaussing, which will explain the mystery of the significantly better static
than dynamic shielding.

No attempt is made here to try to present a quantitatively valid description of
the ferromagnetic materials used in our experiments and to relate the parameters
of the models to measurable material quantities. The theories presented below
serve merely as examples of results obtained from a more thorough study of the
magnetic minimum energy state, going far beyond the simple toy models presented
above. However, the study of ferromagnetic hysteresis is a rich and complex
field; I refer the interested reader to textbooks
\cite{mayergoyz2003mathematical} for further information.

\paragraph{Weiss mean field theory}

Consider the energy of a magnetic domain, with magnetic moment per unit volume
$\vec m$, coupling to the bulk magnetization $\vec M$, with the energy
%
\[
   E=-\mu_0\vec m\cdot(\vec H+\alpha\vec M),
\]
%
where $\alpha$ is a parameter that represents interdomain coupling. Applying
Maxwell--Boltzmann statistics at a temperature $T$ leads to the magnetization
\cite[eqn~1]{jiles1983ferromagnetic}
%
\begin{align}
   \label{weiss}
   \frac{M}{M_\text{s}}=\coth\frac{\mu_0(H+\alpha M)}{\kB
   T/m}-\frac{\kB T/m}{\mu_0(H+\alpha M)},
\end{align}
%
where $M_\text{s}$ is the saturation magnetization of the material. Note that
the right hand side of the above equation has the form of a Langevin function,
$L(x)=\coth(x)-1/x$, so
%
\begin{align}
   \label{weiss_langevin}
   \frac{M}{M_S}=L\left(\frac{H+\alpha M}{a/\mu_0}\right).
\end{align}
%
Here we introduce the third parameter of the theory, $a=\kB T/m$, which
quantifies domain wall density in the material. With no interdomain coupling,
$\alpha=0$ and the magnetization is directly given by $M(H)=L(\mu_0 H/a)$ as in
the classical Langevin theory of paramagnetism. When $\alpha\ne0$, the equation
\ref{weiss} has to be solved numerically. See Fig.~\fref{weiss_plot} for a few choices
of the parameter $\alpha$. We see from the figure that the greater the
interdomain coupling parameter, the steeper the plot in the linear regime around
$H=0$.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figs/mathematica/weiss.pdf}
    \caption[Ferromagnet magnetization vs applied field.]{Magnetization of an
    annealed ferromagnet as a function of the applied field $H$, as found by
    solving eqn~\ref{weiss} for interdomain coupling parameter $\alpha=0,1,3$.}
  \label{weiss_plot}
\end{figure}

The modified Langevin expression \ref{weiss_langevin} gives only the global
energy minimum, or thermal equilibrium, state of a ferromagnet. We know that in
a real material, the motion of domain walls is impeded by the presence of
various defects, such as impurities or mechanical stresses. These `pinning
sites' cause a decrease of the magnetic permeability of the material as the
material gets stuck in metastable states above the global equilibrium, which is
observed as hysteresis on a $M$--$H$ diagram. \cite{jiles1984theory}
Nonetheless, the global equilibrium state can be reached when the material is
either annealed, by a controlled heating and cooling of the material, or
degaussed by a slowly decaying oscillating external field. On a hysteresis
diagram, such as \ref{degaussing_theory}, the degaussing procedure will appear
as a spiraling motion from the outermost hysteresis loop into a point on a curve
given by eqn~\ref{weiss_langevin} and called the \emph{anhysteretic curve}.

In other words, the state of the magnetization that is created by degaussing is
qualitatively similar to that of a paramagnet with a very large effective
anhysteretic permeability $\mu_\text{an}$, found from the slope of the
anhysteretic curve at the field of interest. This number is rarely quoted by the
manufacturers of magnetic shielding materials since it requires a measurement of
the magnetization after degaussing for a range of DC magnetic field offsets.
However, it is my hypothesis that the anhysteretic curve normally lies
approximately in the middle of the major (saturated) hysteresis loops, allowing
one to estimate the anhysteretic permeability in the lab (see
Fig.~\fref{hysteresis} on page \pageref{hysteresis}) or from supplied hysteresis
curves.

Having introduced the distinction between the hysteresis curves and the
anhysteretic curve, and their corresponding permeabilities, we can formulate the
essential point of this section:

\vspace{1em}

\noindent\fbox{\parbox{\textwidth}{When calculating the expected shielding of
static fields following degaussing, we have to use $\mu_\text{an}$ to obtain a
realistic estimate. However, unlike a real paramagnet, the degaussed ferromagnet
will not dynamically change its state of magnetization as dramatically as we
might expect from the huge value of $\mu_\text{an}$. Instead, the attenuation of
small dynamic perturbations to the magnetic field has to be calculated using the
much smaller dynamic permeability $\mu=\d B/\d H$ obtained from the slope of the
$B(H)$ curves, which are minor loops on the hysteresis diagram
(Fig.~\fref{degaussing_theory2}).}}

\vspace{1em}

These loops are not predicted by the Weiss theory. In the following paragraph,
we will consider an extension of the theory that can predict the behavior of the
hysteresis loops in a magnetic material with domain pinning.

\paragraph{Jiles--Atherton model}
\label{jiles_atherton_par}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.5\textwidth]{figs/mathematica/jiles_atherton_offs.pdf}
    \caption[Magnetization curves from Jiles--Atherton model.]{Magnetization
    curves from eqn~\ref{jiles_atherton_eqn}. Observe the difference in slope
    between the anhysteretic curve (dashed) and the minor loops it intersects.
    Figure reproduced from Fig.~\fref{degaussing_theory} (right).}
    \label{degaussing_theory2}
\end{figure}

Under small perturbations in the external field, the magnetic domains are not
entirely free to align their magnetization axis with the applied field because
they are impeded from doing so by the presence of pinning sites in the form of
inclusions in the material and other inhomogeneities. By considering these
pinning sites to be uniformly distributed throughout the material,
Jiles and Atherton \cite{jiles1986theory} were able to derive a differential
equation describing the trajectories in the $M$--$H$ plane for a ferromagnet, as
follows:
%
\begin{align}
   \label{jiles_atherton_eqn}
   \frac{\d M}{\d H}=\frac{1}{(1+c)}\frac{M_\text{an}-M}{\pm
   k/\mu_0-\alpha(M_\text{an}-M)}+\frac{c}{(1+c)}\frac{\d M_\text{an}}{\d H}.
\end{align}
%
Here we introduce two new parameters, $k$ and $c$, and note that the $\pm$ in
the denominator is $+$ for $\d H/\d t>0$ and $-$ for $\d H/\d t<0$. I have
plotted the solutions to this equation for a particular choice of parameters in
Fig.~\fref{degaussing_theory} (here reproduced as Fig.~\fref{degaussing_theory2}),
with an initial condition of saturation magnetization at a negative $H$. As
above, I shall make \emph{no attempt whatsoever} to relate the magnitude or
units of these parameters to measurable material quantities, since this would
distract from the thread of the argument.  As far as this demonstration is
concerned, the model parameters are merely a set of numbers that happens to
reproduce what appears like a `stereotypical' hysteresis loop. The reader
interested in the diversion is encouraged to peruse the plethora of papers on the
topic \cite{jiles1983ferromagnetic, jiles1984theory, jiles1986theory,
lewis1996modeling}.

The plots show the `spiraling' motion characteristic of a degaussing process;
the integration of the differential equation proceeds on an interval
$[H_\text{i},H_\text{f}]$, where $H_\text{f}$ gets reduced by 20~\%\ each time
$\d M/\d t$ gets reversed. (Of course the Jiles--Atherton theory is not time
dependent, so this merely refers to the $\pm$ sign in the denominator of
eqn~\ref{jiles_atherton_eqn} above.)

As seen in Fig.~\fref{degaussing_theory} (page \ref{degaussing_theory}) and
noted in its caption, equation \ref{jiles_atherton_eqn} can have minor loop
solutions with negative slopes, which is unphysical. We see that $\d M/\d H$
would change sign when the magnetization $M(H)$ crosses the anhysteretic
magnetization $M_\text{an}(H)$ if $c$ is small enough and $k$ large enough. The
original papers presenting the theory
\cite{jiles1983ferromagnetic,jiles1984theory,jiles1986theory} do not discuss
this feature at any great length, except for noting that in the $c=0$ case, the
rate of change of magnetization is proportional to the displacement from the
anhysteretic. This rate of change, which is equal to the permeability of the
material, can then therefore be zero or nonzero with either sign. See
\cite{carpenter1991differential} for a discussion of ways around this issue.

Since the publication of the model in the 1980s, a lot of work has focused on
offering improvements and interpretation of the theory, such as a generalization
to vector magnetization \cite{bergqvist1996simple}, relation of model parameters
$M_\text{s},a,\alpha,k,c$ to physical attributes of real materials
\cite{lewis1996modeling}, behavior of hysteresis under tensile and compressive
stress on the material \cite{sablik1987model}, and parameter identification
\cite{lederer1999parameter,leite2004real,fulginei2005softcomputing}.

These generalizations of the hysteresis model would be a good starting point for
a quantitative modeling investigation of ferromagnetic materials used in
magnetic shielding. However, the presentation above suffices for our
purpose here. We have introduced two models, each explaining one key facet
of the behaviour of a magnetic shield. The Weiss mean field theory
introduced the concept of the anhysteretic cure, which accounts for the
better-than-expected performance of degaussed shields in attenuating static lab
fields. On the other hand, the Jiles--Atherton model introduced hysteresis and
the concept of minor loops, with a smaller corresponding permeability, and hence
lesser shielding for non-static fields.

Having concluded the theoretical discussion of degaussing, we turn to what
happens to the shield after degaussing.

\subsubsection{General aspects of magnetic noise}

The previous sections discussed the behavior of the magnetic shield during and
just after degaussing. However, in an actual experiment we are also interested
in what happens to the field afterwards. In general, a magnetization may
gradually develop due to a combination of factors internal and external to the
shield, such as changes in temperature and the lab magnetic fields, or
mechanical stresses applied to the material. These changes are often of a
stochastic nature, precluding prediction of the exact time-dependence of the
fields in a way that would render them amenable to calibration. In this section,
we consider the time-dependence of the magnetic field in the frequency domain as
a \gls{NSD}, which is one of the key design requirements.

There are two ways of obtaining a plot of the \gls{NSD}, either directly using a
spectrum analyzer, or by measuring the signal as a function of time and taking a
Fourier transform with an appropriate scaling. A spectrum analyzer commonly
gives a related quantity, the power spectral density, in units of dBm/Hz. We
first convert this into linear units,
$P_\text{mW/Hz}=(1\,\text{mW})\cdot10^{P_\text{dBm/Hz}/10}$. Then, we convert
the power units into voltage units assuming a nominal 50~$\Omega$ impedance for
the spectrum analyzer:
$V_\mathrm{V/\sqrt{Hz}}=(P_\text{mW/Hz}/1000\cdot50\,\Omega)^{1/2}$. Finally, we
need to convert the voltage into magnetic units as given by the specified
sensitivity and gain of the sensor.

If the spectrum is calculated manually from the voltage signal $V(t_i)$ sampled
at discrete time points ($t_i$, $i=1,\dots,N$, with $t_{i+1}-t_i=\Delta t$)
using a real-valued \gls{FFT} \cite{cooley1965algorithm}, then the correct way
to interpret it as a power spectral density is given by
%
$$
\text{PSD}=2\frac{\Delta t}{N}\left|\mathcal{F}\left[V(t)\right]\right|^2.
$$
%
The `2' in front of the right-hand side of the expression is due to the fact
that for a real-valued \gls{FFT}, the negative-frequency components are folded
into the positive ones. (Here we ignore the DC component corresponding to the
mean value of the recorded voltages.) The ratio $\Delta t/N$, with units of
reciprocal frequency, allows the interpretation of the above as a spectral
density. If any windowing function $w(t_n)$, $n=1,\dots,L$ is applied by
convolving it with the signal $V(t)$, the spectral density has to be multiplied
by the \gls{ENBW}\ correction factor,
%
$$
c_\text{w}=L\frac{\sum_n|w(t_n)|^2}{|\sum_n w(t_n)|^2}.
$$
%
This expresses the fact the window function determines the bandwidth of each
frequency bin. More precisely, it expresses the width of a rectangular window
with the same peak power that accumulates the same noise power.
\cite{harris1978use} For a boxcar window, $c_\text{w}=1$, since it is the window
with the narrowest frequency resolution as is well known in signal processing
\cite[sect~5.2.4]{broughton2018discrete}. This comes at the cost of a reduced
dynamic range due to large sidelobes of the sinc function. Other window
functions, such as Hann, Hamming, or Blackman come with significantly smaller
sidelobes, but a wider main lobe. \cite{proakis2006digital,
oppenheim2010discrete} In the magnetic field measurements done with our magnetic
shield, we are not primarily concerned in distinguishing nearby frequencies from
each other---a good application for the very selective boxcar window---but are
instead interested in integrated, broadband noise, which is better displayed
using a `smoother' window such as the Hann.

In the next section, we consider the low-frequency part of noise using a
different approach: that of a random walk.

\subsubsection{Random walk}

The magnetic noise, as characterized in the previous section using a \gls{NSD},
directly adds to the noise of the final measurement through the Zeeman shifts in
the hyperfine structure. Since the \CENTREX\ experiment is based on the lock-in
technique, we only have to consider the noise in a very narrow bandwidth as
explained in section \ref{shield:requirements}. However, the very low frequency
part of the spectrum is also of interest. Here we refer to the very slow drifts
of the residual field over the course of days, weeks, or months, corresponding
to absolute frequencies around and below $1\uHz$. In particular, we are
interested to find out how long we may expect the magnetic shield to keep its
initial state of a low `static' residual field, or, alternatively, what is the
probability that over the course of a given time period, the average magnetic
field has increased beyond what is acceptable according to our design
requirements? To answer this question, I present a simple model for the
stochastic time dependence of the average field inside the magnetic shield.

Consider the case of random walk of some quantity $W$ (Brown noise, Brownian
motion; related to a Wiener process). In particular, a continuous random walk
$W(t)$ starting from the origin where the $dW$ increments are normally
distributed (with variance $\propto t$) random variables. Such noise may be
obtained by integrating white noise (with a flat spectrum, such as resistor
Johnson noise with $\text{PSD}=4k_\text{B} T R$), or, expressed in the frequency
domain, by applying a single-pole low-pass filter (attenuating higher
frequencies proportionally more than the lower ones).
%
It is interesting to observe that as defined above, the process $W(t)$, while
continuous everywhere, is differentiable nowhere. This can be readily seen by
recalling that the standard deviation of $W(t)$ goes as $\sqrt{t}$, such that
$\d W/dt\propto\lim_{t\to0}t^{-1/2}\to\infty$. Physically, the limit
$\lim_{t\to0}$ corresponds to an infinite bandwidth. To avoid this, we
implicitly assume some high-frequency cutoff.

In this work, we are interested in the low-frequency behaviour of the noisy
system. In particularly, we want to know what is the probability that the
magnetic field $B(t)$ reaches some threshold value $B_T$ in a time $T$. For a
random walk, the probability density function takes the form of a L\'evy
distribution. \cite{jacobs2010stochastic} Further work would be required to
provide the qualitative model of this distribution as applied to our (or any
other) magnetic shield.

While a large number of magnetic shields have been described, few are directly
applicable to our experiment due to being too small (and not easily scalable),
too large (such as the magnetic shielded rooms, as in \cite{altarev2015large}),
or not good enough (with residual field of the order of several milligauss). In
other cases \cite{sasada1988effective, sasada1996large, sasada1996performance,
sasada2000design, nagashima2002high}, the papers do not present sufficient
information to evaluate the shields' suitability to our work, most commonly by
failing to report a spatial scan of the residual field. In what follows I will
describe a few of the shields that are comparable to our design and thus
potentially appropriate for molecular-beam experiments. See
Table~\ref{oldshields} for an overview of these shields.

Fierlinger et al.\ \cite{wodey2020scalable} report building a 10~m-long two-layer
permalloy magnetic shield with an octagonal cross-section and openings to
accommodate a 22~cm vacuum chamber, weighting approx.\ 7500~kg. Constructed out
of a large number of discrete flat and angled permeable sheets, the design is
scalable to an arbitrary length. It is notable that the thickness of the shield
varies from 3~mm at the ends to 8~mm in the central region in order to avoid
saturation effects due to the increased density of magnetic flux lines captured
by the material in the middle compared to the ends. The transverse shielding
factor of 4,000 would be almost sufficient for our experiment and easy to
augment with one or two additional outer metglas layers. The reported 40~$\mu$G
residual field along the shield symmetry axis over the central 8~m of length,
while impressive for a shield of this geometry, would nonetheless have to be
shimmed with internal coils down to our desired 10~$\mu$G. (The field off-axis
is not reported in the paper.) The improved performance compared to the shield
reported in this chapter comes with increased cost of materials and a
considerably more complex mechanical assembly.

Plaster et al.\ \cite{malkowski2011technique} investigated different assembly
methods for shields constructed from metglas wrapped on a non-magnetic
cylindrical forms (cardboard tubes in their case, PVC pipes in \CENTREX). In
one of the experiments reported in the paper, metglas strips are secured on the
form with circular wrapping, whereby each strip is wrapped around the form
circumferentially. There is no overlap between strips of a single layer;
however, successive layers are offset by 1~in to cover the gaps between strips).
(By comparison, our magnetic shield also includes overlaps between the strips of a
single layer in order to eliminate all gaps, as detailed in the following
section. The effect of these gaps is not evident at the level of sensitivity
reported in the paper, but becomes visible when measuring lower magnetic fields
such as is the case in our magnetic shield.) While the techniques shown in this
paper (circular metglas wrapping around tube forms, Kapton tape and plastic film
to hold the assembly together, toroidal degaussing coils) have been a key
inspiration behind our magnetic shield, the performance they demonstrate with
their 0.08~mm thin single-layer shield is insufficient for our purposes and has
to be improved via a multi-layer design to be suitable for precision
measurements.

In ref.\ \cite{dickerson2012high}, Kasevich et al.\ constructed an 8.7~m-long
three-layer shield made entirely of mu-metal. Each layer consists of 14 segments
of length 65~cm that have been welded together and then hydrogen annealed as one
piece in an enormous annealing furnace. Despite the substantial effort and
expense they must have incurred with the welding, annealing, transportation, and
mounting structure (not discussed in the paper, but essential to avoid stressing
the long and relatively thin mu-metal tubes), the overall static performance of
the assembly is limited to approximately 0.5~mG$_\text{rms}$ transverse and
axial residual field, largely due to field inhomogeneities apparent at the
location of the welded joints. (The absolute offset of the magnetic field is
reported to within `a few mG' with no reasons given for absence of sensor
calibration.) The authors unfortunately do not attempt to corroborate the
predicted transverse shielding factor of 400,000 to 3,000,000 with experimental
dynamic shielding factor measurements. The disappointing performance receives
no comment in the paper, but is most likely due to the small inner diameter of
the shield (19~cm) compared to our work (37~cm) or the state of the art (45~cm)
reported in \cite{wodey2020scalable}. The smaller inner diameter places the
region of interest close to the shield walls, where the fields likely due to
pinned omains in the shield material may be substantially larger than at the
center of the shield. It is not inconceivable that the residual field would be
improved by removing the innermost layer. 


\subsection{Physical structure}

The \CENTREX\ magnetic shield consists of four concentric cylindrical layers
with open ends (See Figs.~\ref{concentric} and \ref{longitudinal}). Three of
these are built by wrapping metglas foil (FINEMET Wide Format Film, Hitachi Part
MS-FR 470-100M-T0, containing 18~$\mu$m-thick FINEMET FT-3M metglas between
protective layers of plastic) around PVC pipes, while the fourth, innermost
layer has been welded out of two sheets of mu-metal rolled into a tubular form,
and annealed. The inner mu-metal cylinder is fabricated from two identical
rectangular sheets of mu-metal, 22.79"~$\times$~120", that are joined together
with two 180\deg-opposed planished (i.e., flattened) welds, and the entire
cylinder annealed after fabrication. The mu-metal tube, having a wall thickness
of only 1~mm, is relatively fragile; in order to limit its motion, it is
sandwiched between the hard plastic PVC tubes of the inner metglas layer and
that of the shim coils. The inner two metglas layers are spaced apart using an
array small-diameter PVC tubes; the same method is employed to support the glass
vacuum chamber of the interaction region centered inside the shim coils tube.
The outermost tube is mounted on an 80/20 frame using a set of seven aluminum
semi-circular plates.

\subsubsection{Layers and spacers}

The three metglas layers are all built in the same way. First, the plastic tube
(provided by the Harrison Machine \&\ Plastic Corp., Garrettsville, OH) is cut
to size with a handsaw if necessary (see Table~\ref{shieldlayers} for layer
lengths and material details) and roughly cleaned with water and paper towels on
the outside and, as far as possible, also on the inside to remove the dirt it
comes with, probably due to outdoors storage. Then, I cut 96 pieces (eight
segments, 12 layers each) of metglas for each of the two outer layers (and 84
pieces, or seven segments of 12 layers each, for the inner 16" layer since it is
15" shorter than the other two) to the length required to wrap around the tube
once with approx.\ 1" of circumferential as well as longitudinal overlap between
adjacent pieces in a layer. The pieces are secured to the PVC tube and to each
other with a few short pieces of Kapton tape (McMaster p.n.\ 7648A63) so that
they do not fall down during assembly.

The prepared and cleaned tubes are put on a stand with wheels under them so they
can be rotated, facilitating the circumferential wrapping of the metglas foil.
The first piece of metglas is wrapped about an inch from the end of the tube;
leaving this much bare PVC helps with handling the approx.\ 100--200 lb tubes.
The second piece of metglas is wrapped so as to have about an inch of overlap with the
first, and the remaining six pieces are attached analogously. Once the first
layer is done, the wrapping resumes at the position of the first metglas piece.
This procedure is repeated for a total of 12 layers total. (Due to axial and
circumferential overlaps, the true thickness of the shield is not perfectly
homogeneous, but in practice we find that this is unimportant as long as the
overall shielding is sufficiently high.)

To secure the metglas pieces and prevent them from coming loose in case the
Kapton tape fails, the entire tube is also wrapped in polyethylene plastic
stretch wrap. (I did not keep track of the number of wraps of the plastic
stretch wrap; it is approx.\ 3--5 wraps.) Following that, 12 turns of degaussing
wire are wrapped in a toroidal fashion around the tube. The wire is 22 or 24
gauge lacquered copper wire, sufficiently thick for approx.\ 2 amps of
degaussing current. The 12 turns are uniformly distributed around the
circumference of the tube; we believe this may be important for the performance
of the degaussing but unfortunately I do not have conclusive evidence for this
oft-repeated statement (D. DeMille, personal communication, 2020--22). On the
outermost (20") tube only, we also have a solenoidal coil with approx 1 turn per
in spacing for rough cancellation of axial fields. (Given the metglas wrapping
geometry, this solenoidal coil would not be effective for degaussing
\cite{malkowski2011technique}.) Finally, each tube is again wrapped in plastic
wrap to secure the degaussing and (for 20" tube only) the solenoidal coil.

The mu-metal layer does not, of course, require wrapping with metglas, only the
degaussing coil and the outer plastic foil wrap. However, care must be taken to
reinforce the insulation of the degaussing wire at the ends of the cylinder; the
sharp edges of the cylinder can easily strip the magnet wire coating and short
the turns of the wire to the conductive cylinder, which does not happen with the
PVC tubes. I used heat shrink tubing to do this, but it required cutting the
wire at each turn and at both ends, adding insulation, soldering the wires back
together and heating the insulation to make it shrink and adhere. It would have
been better to use a wire with more durable insulation instead.

As shipped, the mu-metal tube had a visibly non-circular cross section at one
end, potentially due to deformations that took place during shipping. This did
not pose difficulties with the shield assembly, since the tolerances of the
design (as given by the dimensions of the plastic tubes, see
Table~\ref{shieldlayers}) allow for sufficient deviations from circularity, as
well as because the mu-metal tube is flexible enough to accommodate small (say,
approx.\ 1/4" difference from the nominal diameter) shape changes.
Unfortunately, I do not have data to determine to what extent these deformations
that inevitably take place during assembly cause magnetic domain wall pinning or
other effects that may ultimately result in a larger than expected residual
magnetic field.

At each end, the magnetic shield has a large circular opening matching the
diameter of each layer. (Mechanically, this is the inner diameter of the plastic
tubes. Magnetically, it would be the outer diameter of the same plastic tubes,
or the inner diameter of the metglas wrapping. For the mu-metal layer, this is
the inner diameter of the mu-metal tube.) We have considered attaching
custom-fabricated mu-metal endcaps to extend the innermost layer, as well as to
reduce the diameter of the opening. However, this would pose difficulties since
the caps would most likely have to be removed whenever the vacuum chamber is to
be opened, such as during initial assembly or subsequent maintenance. Since the
mu-metal cylinder is not perfectly circular in cross section, attaching the
endcaps would involve bending the cylinder and the cap sufficiently for them to
be of a matching cross section, or else designing endcaps large enough to
accommodate the non-circular shape. The latter case would likely be a bad idea
on account of poor magnetic contact between the main cylinder and the caps. In
the end, since we found that the shield performs according to our requirements
(see sect~\ref{magnetic_shield_performance_residual}) even without any endcaps,
we have decided to forgo them.

After assembling all four layers into the completed shield, I measured the
resistance of degaussing wires to ensure continuity. The measured values match
the expected ones (as determined from coil length, gauge, and resistivity of
copper) to within 5~\%, showing no evidence of a short or open circuit.

\subsubsection{Shim coils}
\label{shim_coil_section}

To further attenuate the static residual fields within the magnetic shield, the
innermost (14") PVC pipe is covered with an array (described in the following)
of 80 single-turn magnetic field coils. Given a measurement of the field inside
the shield, the coils can be set to the appropriate current in each one so as to
minimize the resulting residual static magnetic field inside.

The set of shim coils consists of 16 segments of three dimensions each (see
Fig.~\fref{shimcoils}). The axial field is controlled by simple circular current
loops (spaced 8" apart from each other). The two transverse dimensions ($B_x$
and $B_y$) are implemented as pairs of one-loop saddle coils (8" by 60\deg\
each). These consist of a rectangle `bent' onto the surface of a cylinder,
resulting in two straight sides and two curved sides. The saddle-coil geometry
allows for the calculation of the exact magnetic field expressions
\cite{hanssum1983exact}, although in practice we rely on the measured fields
instead (see section \ref{shimming_operation} below).

The coils listed above give a total of $5\times16=80$ degrees of freedom. The
22 gauge magnet wires are fixed in place by being glued into a series of grooves
on the surface of the 14" PVC tube using neutral-cure RTV silicone sealant (Dow
Dowsil 737) and wrapped in plastic foil for added protection. During the curing
of the silicone, the wires are held in place by Kapton tape, which is removed
after the silicone hardens.

\begin{figure}

  \caption{Shim coil geometry.}
  \label{shimcoils}
\end{figure}

The size of the tube made it impractical to use standard milling machines to cut
the grooves for the shim coils. While the circular grooves were easily cut using
a hand saw, the axial ones were done on a custom-made setup (Fig.~\fref{grooves})
involving a hand drill clamped on a 80/20 frame, which in turn is mounted using
linear bearings to a larger frame. The setup is not as rigid as a true milling
machine would be, which is why it is important to use a constant force while
moving the carriage forward to avoid cutting wavy grooves.  I used a system of
ropes, pulleys, and weights to achieve satisfactory results.

  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/grooves.png}
    \caption[Making shim coils.]{\part{a} Milling machine for cutting
    longitudinal grooves. \part{b} Wires taped inside grooves while silicone
    hardens.}

With 80 different coils (whose leads are twisted into twisted pairs using a hand
drill), cable management is essential so as not to confuse the cables with each
other. For each coil, a linear groove is cut from one end of the tube to the
location of the coil; thus all coils can be connected to at one end of the
shield. There, the sets of wire pairs belonging to each dimension are bundled
together into five bundles of 16 pairs using zip ties; each wire pair is
temporarily labeled by a numbered piece of Kapton tape at the end. For ease of
connecting and disconnecting, these wires are attached to adapter boards
(Fig.~\fref{shim_adapters}) that are permanently attached to the 80/20 support
frame of the shield. The adapter boards bundle four pairs of coil leads into the
pins of one RJ45 jack to enable using standard patch cables.

These adapter boards also serve to organize the coils in case the
temporary Kapton-tape labels fall off. In case the coils need to be disconnected
from the adapter boards, the coils must be checked to see if the labels are
still attached. However, if the coils do get confused, this can easily be
remedied later, when the magnetic fields corresponding to each coil are
measured. The coils are sufficiently separated in space so they can be easily
identified by their magnetic field pattern.

The shim coils are driven by a home-build current source PCB
(Fig.~\fref{current_source}) inspired by the current source shown in Horowitz and
Hill \cite[Fig.~5.69A]{horowitz2016art}. An Arduino microcontroller board, which
is connected via USB to a computer, controls two 8-channel \DAC s (AD5676R) via
a digital isolator (ADUM7441) to remove potential noise from the USB port. Each
\DAC\ output is connected to the non-inverting terminal of a difference
amplifier, directly controlling the voltage drop over a precision 100~$\Omega$
resistor. This implements a voltage to current converter, allowing for
computer-controlled $\pm10$~mA adjustment to each of the 80 coils individually
(using five of the PCBs).

\subsubsection{Magnetic shield support structure}

The considerable total weight of the magnetic shield (780 lbs of plastic, plus
140 lbs of mu-metal, metglas, and other materials) requires a sufficiently strong support
structure (see Fig.~\fref{support}). In our case, it is assembled from
quad-profile 80/20 rails (profile number 3030, total weight of the structure
approx.\ 300~lbs) and placed on eight caster wheels (McMaster p.n.\ 9813T71) so
the setup can in principle be moved by one person.  The support structure
consists of three four-foot segments mounted together to form a rectangular box.
Rails going across the top of the box hold aluminum plates (1/4" thick) with
semi-circular cutouts. To keep the plates from damaging the delicate outer
metglas layer, the sharp corners of the cutouts are protected with hard nylon
tape.

\subsection{Characterization and optimization}

The characterization and optimization of the shield is an iterative process
requiring a repeated sequence of three operations: magnetic field measurement,
degaussing, and shimming. The main goal of these procedures is to meet the
specification on residual static magnetic field of 10~$\mu$G inside the shield
(see section \ref{shield:requirements} more more information).

I distinguish two properties of the magnetic field of interest to us. We refer
to residual field as the field inside the shield assuming no changes in the
external magnetic conditions. In our four-layer shield, this is the dominant
magnetic field inside the interaction region. Despite our best attempts at
degaussing, these residual fields are typically of the order of 50--200~$\mu$G
in the center of the shield.

The other property of interest is the response of the shield to external
time-varying fields. Primarily, we are interested in the attenuation of external
dynamic fields during the time when the external field is present. We are also
interested in fields that remain \emph{after} the external field has been turned
off; in other words, in the shield magnetization caused by an external field
transient.

\subsubsection{Residual field measurement}

During shield characterization, the primary measurement is of the field in the
central region of the shield. During the use of the shield in a beam-line
experiment, this region is difficult to access; it is also the region of
greatest interest since all molecules included in the experiment pass through
it. During characterization I attached a $4"\times4"$ aluminium rectangular tube
by suspending it from them shield mounting structure at each end. I used a
system of pulleys to pull a sensor carriage using a stepper motor and a rotary
encoder to measure the position of the sensor along the length of tube (see
Fig.~\fref{pulleys}). The sensor itself (Bartington Mag03MS-100) is a 3-channel
fluxgate magnetometer with a measuring range of 1~G and a typical electronic
offset of several hundred microgauss. (The specified noise level of
0.1~$\mu$G/$\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ at 1 Hz, integrated over the 3~kHz bandwidth of
the sensor, is below the mechanical stability of the sensor translation setup
with a typical repeatability of approx. 1~$\mu$G as determined by measuring the
field across the length of the shield two times in a row.) The fluxgate signal
is amplified by a signal conditioning unit (Bartington SCU-1), then read out by
a home-built circuit board containing an \ADC\ (AD4115), as described further in
appendix \ref{decapsu_adc_board}.

    across pulleys using rope, to drive the magnetometer carriage inside a
    $4"\times4"$ aluminium rectangular tube.}

\end{figure}

The sensor is specified to have 100~nG/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ of noise at 1~Hz. To
verify this, I measured the noise spectrum while the sensor was located inside
the shield. Given the dynamic shielding factor and the magnetic noise level in
the lab, we expected the sensor noise to be significantly larger than the true
magnetic noise inside the shield. This was indeed the case; the results are
shown in Fig.~\fref{sensornoise}.

The electronic offset of the sensor and its associated electronics is
significant. It is specified as less than 50~$\mu$G, with a temperature
coefficient of 1~$\mu$G/\degC, although in practice we have found that offsets
of several hundred microgauss are typical. The offset calibration is done by
taking a measurement and immediately repeating it with the sensor rotated upside
down. This is accomplished by mounting the sensor in a short rectangular
aluminum tube with enough plastic tape on the outside so that the sensor
carriage fits tightly inside the long square tube going through the center of
the shield. After a measurement is done, the sensor is pulled out of the long
tube and reinserted rotated upside down. The accuracy of the offset calibration
then depends on how well-centered the sensor is in the carriage, how much the
field inside the shield changes during the time it takes to perform the
calibration, how much the electronic offset itself changes in the same time, and
how perfectly square the cross-sections of the long tube and the carriage are.

Rather than attempt to estimate the magnitudes of these effects individually, I
estimate the precision/accuracy of the calibration from the calibration curve
itself; see Fig.~\fref{offsetcal} for a typical example. It shows the two
transverse components of the magnetic field (here, labeled as $B_x$ and $B_y$)
as measured by the sensor in its normal orientation, as well as with the sensor
inverted. The data is presented in the symmetrized form:
$(\text{normal}+\text{inverted})/2$, which corresponds to the sensor offset, and
$(\text{normal}-\text{inverted})/2$, which corresponds to the physical magnetic
field plus any calibration imperfections.  Given the time scale of the changes
in the electronic offset is primarily driven by temperature changes, which are
negligible in the time it takes to do the calibration (around two minutes), we
expect the curves showing the offset to be perfectly flat within the noise of
the measurement, but that is typically not the case. The \RMS\ deviations from
the average value I identify as the error in the offset calibration, consisting
of all the aforementioned factors.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
   \includegraphics[width=.75\textwidth]{figs/todo/offset_calibration.png}
    \caption[Magnetometer offset vs position in shield.]{Electronic offset of
    the magnetometer as a function of position along the length of the center of
    the magnetic shield. Only the two transverse dimensions are shown.
    \part{Top} The $B_x$ component of the magnetic field measured with the
    sensor in its usual orientation ($B_{x,\uparrow}$) compared to the sensor

    significantly during the sensor reversal and re-measurement.}
   \label{offsetcal}

While one may take the $(\text{normal}-\text{inverted})/2$ superposition as the
best measurement of the physical fields, doing so means that the error is
different at each location along the shield. Since we expect the offset to be
essentially constant during the measurement, I believe it a better practice to
estimate the offset from the average value of the superposition, and then simply
subtract this constant value from all subsequent measurements. This way, the
overall offset is subject to an uncertainty, but the shape of the field pattern
is known much more precisely.

The typical repeatability of a measurement, including the effects of translating
the sensor back and forth within the magnetic shield, can be within
$\pm1$~$\mu$G, as shown in the example measurement in Fig.~\fref{repeatability}.
In addition to the random errors introduced by the mechanical instability of the
measurement setup, we also have to pay attention to the systematic errors
induced by the signal chain from the sensor onwards. The sensor and its
preamplifier have a limited bandwidth; 3~kHz for the sensor, and a settable
low-pass filter for the preamp.  This must not be set too low in relation to the
speed of the sensor motion. In practice, I use the lowest reasonable LPF cutoff
frequency, verifying the choice by repeating the measurement with the next
higher available frequency. If the two measurements are sufficiently close to
each other, I use the lower of the two frequencies. I use a similar process to
decide upon the digital LPF included with the \ADC.

\subsubsection{Off-axis magnetometry}

When the magnetic shield is in use, the central axis will not be accessible to
magnetic sensors. To control the magnetic field, we planned on using a set of
four magnetometers distributed around the vacuum chamber. The sensors are in
rectangular aluminum enclosures inside square fiberglass tubes sliding along the
length of the magnetic shield. In principle, the idea was to use the information
about the off-axis magnetic field to deduce the on-axis field, but in practice
the correlation was hard to establish. Fig.~\fref{on_off_axis} shows the (lack of
a) correlation between the on-axis and off-axis sensors, when the two sensors
are located at a fixed point inside the shield, one approximately on top of the
other. The data is low-pass filtered by convolution with a window function to
reveal long-term trends. We see that at different times, the slope of the curve
can be anything from zero to $\pm\infty$, reflecting the obvious fact that the
magnetic field at two different points in space is not correlated.

It would have been necessary to measure the off-axis field at several points
along the shield, as shown in an example measurement in Fig.~\fref{onoff}.
However, this approach may require greater positioning accuracy than that
achievable from the current system of ropes and pulleys. Assume a $1~\mu$G
change in the off-axis fields corresponds to a $1~\mu$G change in the on-axis
field. Consider that the gradient in the off-axis fields (lower plot in
Fig.~\fref{onoff}) can be as high as approx.\ $60~\mu$G/cm, so the sensor would
have to be positioned within 0.1~mm for $1~\mu$G precision.
Fraction-of-millimeter precision is not impossible to achieve, but would require
improving the sensor motion mechanism, such as by using a timing belt, or a
rigid rack driven by pinion gears. More importantly, future work would have to
establish the data analysis method to deduce the on-axis fields from off-axis
fields, perhaps by finding the linear combination of off-axis fields at various
locations that best corresponds to the on-axis field at a given location. See
ref.\ \cite{pollok2023magnetic} for an innovative approach using generative
adversarial networks to predict magnetic field in a region of space using field
measurements around (but of course not inside) that region.

\subsubsection{Degaussing}

The hardware used for degaussing in this experiment is as follows
(Fig.~\fref{degaussing_system}). Each of the four shielding layers has its own
12-turn degaussing coil. These are connected to a \PCB\ with a set of relays
that can connect each of them to the degaussing current in parallel (although
only one should be connected at a time). The circuit board includes a high-power
precision 1~$\Omega$ resistor wired in series with the coils. Opamp followers at
each side of the resistor enable a buffered readout of both the current flowing
through the coils as well as the voltage drop; together, these may be used to
calculate the impedance of the coils (Fig.~\fref{impedance}). The impedance
should change as a function of degaussing current as the shield saturates,
giving one way to find if the degaussing current is sufficient to saturate the
shield.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/degaussing_system.png}
    \caption[Degaussing circuit.]{Simplified diagram of the degaussing circuit.
    The four degaussing coils are switched on sequentially during the degaussing
    procedure, but never at the same time.}
  \label{degaussing_system}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/impedance.png}
    \caption[Impedance of degaussing coils.]{Real and imaginary parts of the
    impedance of the degaussing coils wrapped around the innermost two layers of
    the shield.}
  \label{impedance}
\end{figure}

The other way to ensure shield saturation is to directly observe the hysteresis
loops during degaussing. In Fig.~\fref{hysteresis}, the abscissa gives the degaussing
current used as a proxy for the applied field. Responding to the sinusoidal
degaussing current, voltage gets induced in a single-turn pickup coil also
wrapped around the shield. By Faraday's law, the magnetic flux density is
proportional to the time integral of the induced voltage.

During degaussing, the outer layer is always connected first, followed by the
second outermost layer, followed by the innermost metglas layer, followed by the
mu-metal layer. Since the residual field is primarily determined by the
innermost layer, that one is degaussed last lest it be magnetized by accident
during the degaussing of the other layers. The relays \PCB\ is in turn connected
to an isolation transformer (Hammond 169J) for the purpose of DC blocking. We
have considered alternate forms of DC blocking, such as a capacitor, but the
required capacitance was too high at the low frequencies typically used in
degaussing (down to 1~Hz).

The transformer is driven by a power amplifier (Kepco BOP36-12M), which in turn
is driven by a home-built \DAC\ board. The \DAC\ board provides a connection
between a USB-controlled Teensy 4.0 microcontroller breakout board (PJRC,
Sherwood, OR, USA) and the \DAC\ (AD5761BRUZ) via a digital isolator
(ADUM7441ARQZ-RL7). The \DAC\ output is filtered by a 100~$\Omega$, 1~$\mu$F
$RC$ filter and buffered by an opamp follower (AD8675ARZ-REEL7).

Depending on the orientation of the shield with respect to the room magnetic
field, it can be advantageous to supply the solenoidal coil on the outermost
layer with a constant electric current (Fig.~\fref{solenoid}). As a result, the
region of the magnetic shield where the axial field is low is extended by tens
of centimeters. Of course, if the direction of the shield or the room field were
to change significantly, the optimal solenoid current has to be recalibrated.
For small changes, I do not expect a large effect given that the solenoid mostly
impacts the outermost layer.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/solenoid.png}
    \caption[Residual field vs axial solenoid current.]{The axial component of
    the residual field after degaussing, with shim coils on (solid line) and off
    (dashed line). \part{a} $B_z$-cancelling solenoid turned off; \part{b}
    solenoid supplied with a constant current.}
  \label{solenoid}
\end{figure}

A wide range of degaussing waveforms have been used in the literature; for
example, linear or polynomial envelopes or envelopes specifically optimized for
a linear decrease in the magnetization of the ferromagnetic material
\cite{thiel2007proposal}. In this experiment, I use a finite number of
sinusoidal degaussing pulses. The pulses can optionally be separated by an
adjustable delay to probe the evolution of the residual field from pulse to
pulse. Each subsequent pulse has its amplitude decreased by a constant factor
compared with the preceding pulse; thus our protocol is similar to, but not
identical to, using an exponentially decreasing envelope multiplying a sine
wave.

Despite spending significant amounts of time on optimizing the degaussing
parameters, I am only able to claim the following. The degaussing frequency
must be slow enough that the skin effect does not significantly affect the
degaussing; the initial amplitude has to be large enough to drive the shield
into saturation; the final amplitude has to be small enough that the shield has
recovered from saturation and is circling near a point around the origin of the
hysteresis curve; the number of degaussing pulses has to be large enough so that
the decrease in amplitude is gradual. The exact values of the parameters
mentioned in the preceding statements need to be found experimentally for the
shield geometry used.

See Fig.~\fref{deg_wfm} for the degaussing waveform used in this work. Note that
the degaussing power amplifier has a gain of 3.6, while the degaussing coils
have a DC resistance of 5.0, 7.8, 7.2, and 4.8 ohms, respectively from the
outermost layer to the innermost layer. Thus the coils are driven with
approximately 10--20 amp-turns of current. The frequency is chosen with respect
to the skin effect; for mu-metal ($\mu\approx50,000$,
$\rho\approx0.6\;\mu\Omega\cdot\text{m}$), skin depth at 3~Hz is approx.\ 1~mm,
while for metglas ($\mu\approx70,000$,
$\rho\approx1.2\;\mu\Omega\cdot\text{m}$), 1~mm skin depth corresponds to a
frequency of approx.\ 4~Hz. However, the metglas layer is thinner (0.2~mm) than
the mu-metal layer.

  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/deg_wfm.png}
    \caption[Degaussing waveform.]{Degaussing waveform. The first three pulses,
    applied to the metglas layers, are identical; the last pulse is different,
    since it is applied to the mu-metal layer. The two detail plots on the right
    show the beginning of the first and last pulse, respectively, and the titles
    show the frequency and exponential decay time constant.}
  \label{deg_wfm}
\end{figure}

Exploration of these parameters is further complicated by the fact that there
are four layers to degauss and degaussing one may inadvertently magnetize the
others. I have tried degaussing several or all layers in parallel, but have not
found any significant improvement and thus have not further explored that
method. A further difficulty with studying the degaussing process is the fact
that it is not obvious how to put the magnetic shield in a known `non-degaussed'
initial state. In an attempt to optimize the degaussing parameters, I have tried
to use a single large-amplitude sinusoidal pulse to magnetize the shield, but it
is not clear to what extent this was successful and reproducible.

While degaussing, one may observe the shapes of the hysteresis loops (see
example in Fig.~\fref{hysteresis}) as a function of the degaussing amplitude.
These are a useful tool to verify that degaussing is driving the shield into
saturation, which we believe essential for the success of the procedure.
However, it is difficult to correlate the degree of saturation to its effect on
the effectiveness of degaussing. Future work may be done establish the nature of
this correlation, which may help optimize the degaussing procedure.

Given the importance of the long-term stability of the magnetic fields
inside the shield, it is interesting to explore how stable the degaussing
process is, i.e., how much does the field change when degaussing repeatedly. See
Fig.~\fref{continuously_degaussing} for the measured evolution of average
internal magnetic field. It is difficult to conclude whether degaussing
consistently increases or decreases the average field; most likely the change is
randomly distributed around zero mean.

\subsubsection{Shimming the residual field}
\label{shimming_operation}

Once the shield has been degaussed as well as is possible, the remaining
magnetic field has to be cancelled, to the extent possible, using the system of
shim coils. The accuracy of this depends on the measurement of the residual
field, as well as on the geometry of the coils themselves. During shield
characterization, we have access to the center of the shield and can measure the
fields to within 1--2~$\mu$G.  Thus the limit is set by the number of coil
segments and their size.

The shimming process consists of two steps. First, the individual field patterns
generated by each of the 80 shim coils have to be measured. I do this by flowing
a constant current through the coil under test, measuring the magnetic field,
then reversing the direction of the current and repeating the measurement. The
stored calibration curve is generated by subtracting the two measurements,
applying smoothing, and masking the parts of the curve where the coil field is
expected to be negligible (see Fig.~\fref{calibration} for an example calibration
curve). Once the calibration curves for all the coils are known, I use a simple
least-squares minimization routine to determine the optimal currents to use so
as to minimize the magnitude of the residual field inside the shield. While I
could limit the optimization routine to only minimize the field along the
vertical axis, this being the component we are most sensitive to in the
molecular beam experiment, I do not anticipate a significant improvement in the
shimming by doing so given that the three dimensions of the magnetic field
accessed by the coils are orthogonal to each other.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/continuously_degaussing.png}
  \caption{Average internal magnetic field while degaussing every 200 seconds.}
  \label{continuously_degaussing}
\end{figure}

In practice, I found it is most effective to repeat the shimming procedure
several times in a row, alternating with degaussing until the field converges to
a more or less constant value. After the first shimming, the field is greatly
reduced compared to the degaussed-only values, but is nonetheless not as low as
would be expected from the least-squares calculation. Furthermore, following the
initial shimming with degaussing is likely to increase the residual field. After
several rounds of shimming, degaussing, and re-measuring, the field settles at
the value predicted from least squares (to within measurement accuracy) and is
only minimally affected by subsequent degaussing.

\subsubsection{Dynamic field measurement}

The attenuation of the external fields is not perfect. If an infinitely large
Helmholtz pair were to drive a perfectly uniform and sinusoidally
varying magnetic field in the region of space occupied by the magnetic
shield, then a smaller sinusoidal variation of the field inside the
shield could be detected that is of the same frequency as the external
field. In practice, there are practical limits to the size of external coils. In
one version of the measurement, I have attached a room-sized loop of
wire to the walls of the lab and drive them with a function generator while
passing the magnetometer reading through a lock-in amplifier. Repeating the
measurement in the absence of any shielding gives the magnitude of the
external field variation. Dividing this by the magnitude measured with
the shield in place gives the shielding factor.

The disadvantage of this method is that the magnetic shield has to be removed to
measure the external magnetic field, since the shape of the external coils is
difficult to measure precisely. (Future work: build room-sized coils of known
geometry to measure SF better.) To find the SF of the final shield, I have used
smaller rectangular coils of well-known dimensions centered around the magnetic
shield; the field they apply, while not uniform, can be calculated using a
closed-form expression. \cite[eqns~8--10]{hampton2020closed} This allows me to
define an effective shielding factor as the ratio of the theoretically predicted
field of the coils divided by the field measured inside the shield.

The pair of rectangular coils lying in vertical planes (used to apply horizontal
transverse field) have dimensions 26"~$\times$~144" and are spaced by 30". This
means they apply a central field of approx.\ 525~mG/A. (The corresponding values
for the horizontal pair of coils are 30"~$\times$~144", spaced by 26", with a
central field of 620 mG/A.) Both coils are made using 50 turns of 22-gauge wire,
giving a resistance of around 20~$\Omega$, allowing the use of a low-voltage
power supply to cancel (some of) the Earth's magnetic field.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/calibration.png}
    \caption[Shim coil calibration curve example.]{Calibration curve for the 8th
    vertical saddle coil pair (coil dimensions, and other details, are given in
    section \ref{shim_coil_section}).}
  \label{calibration}
\end{figure}

I used the SR530 analog lock-in amplifiers to measure the internal field
magnitude. In order to extend the measurement below the 0.5~Hz limit of this
instrument, I have also tried to measure the fields inside with the ADC used in
residual-field measurement, but without any further pre-amplification and
filtering. However, I have not yet succeeded in reconciling these measurements
with those done with the SR530. In particular, the software lock-in reported
non-zero internal field at zero external excitation. In the future, I may verify
why that is the case, and spend more time calibrating the software lock-in as
compared to commercial instruments.

To calibrate the externally applied field, I measured the current flowing
through the rectangular coils by measuring the voltage drop over as series
1~$\Omega$ resistor with an oscilloscope. At low amplitudes, this measurement
is susceptible to noise picked up by the wiring because there is no filtering
between the resistor and the oscilloscope. This could be improved by using a
lock-in amplifier to calibrate the current versus function generator amplitude
and frequency settings.

\subsubsection{Charging magnetization}
\label{shield:charging_magnetization}

As specified in the section about shield design requirements
\ref{shield:requirements}, the shield may get magnetized due to high-voltage
electrode charging current, but the effect needs to be constrained below
approx.\ 30~pG. A field that small would be difficult to detect directly using
the fluxgate magnetometers we use for shield characterization. The sensor is
specified to have noise lower than 100~nG/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ at 1~Hz. To achieve
30~pG, the averaging time would have to be as long as five months---which would
correspond to a measurement frequency of 80~nHz, at which the sensor noise
density would be significantly larger due to $1/f$ noise.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/charging.png}
    \caption[Effect of charging current on shield magnetization.]{Effect of
    charging current on shield magnetization. \part{a} Electric current during
    for each direction. It is not clear why the noise for positive direction is
    much larger, but it is insignificant compared to the current magnitude.
    \part{b} The `charging magnetization' is non-zero for the vertical $B_y$
    component of the field. \part{c} $B_y$ is higher than before flowing the
    current in the positive direction. \part{d} $B_y$ is lower than before
    flowing the current in the negative direction.}
  \label{charging}
\end{figure}

Rather than improving the measurement sensitivity, we can instead increase the
signal size by increasing the electrode `charging' current we pass through the
inside of the shield. At a more realistic integration time of a couple seconds,
we should be able to detect fields as small as 100~nG, which is about
3,000$\times$ larger than 30~pG. If, upon applying 72~mA (a current
3,000$\times$ larger than the realistic electrode charging current of
24~$\mu$A), we find no evidence of magnetizations at the 100~nG level, then it
is likely that the 24~$\mu$A would not pose problematic shield magnetizations.
Of course, the exact extrapolation to smaller field values is complicated by the
severely nonlinear response of the ferromagnetic shield.

In the test setup, rather than using high voltage electrodes, I used two wires
separated by 2~cm (matching the electrode spacing) passing approximately through
the center of the shield. The magnetic field a distance $R$ away from a wire
carrying a current $I$ is given by $B=\mu_0 I/(2\pi R)$. Thus the field 4" away
from the two parallel wires (2~cm separation, currents going opposite
directions) is approx.\ 4~mG/A, or 100~nG at 24~$\mu$A.

I used a set of relays to reverse the current passed through the cables. The
measurement procedure is to repeat $N$ times the following sequence of steps:
(1) Flow a current $I$ through the wires for a time $t_\text{charge}$; (2)
Measure the magnetic field for a time $t_\text{meas}$, making sure to wait long
enough for the low-pass filters to settle; (3) Reverse the current direction and
repeat preceding steps. By averaging the difference of the fields that
correspond to opposite directions of the charging current, we effect a lock-in
detection (equivalent to a sharp bandpass filter), whereby we avoid the majority
of $1/f$ noise that a simple long-time measurement would be susceptible to. See
Fig.~\fref{charging} for an example of such a measurement.

The error bars in the legends of Fig.~\fref{charging} are calculated as the
standard deviation of the data points divided by the square root of the number
of the points. This matches the data for magnetometer noise shown in
Fig.~\fref{sensornoise} (page \pageref{sensornoise}) as follows. The center
frequency of the measurement, as given by the reciprocal of $t_\text{meas}$, is
$f_0=0.15$~Hz. We can read from Fig.~\fref{sensornoise} that the sensor noise at
that frequency is $\text{NSD}=170$~nG/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$. The bandwidth is the
center frequency divided by the number of datapoints, in this case
$\text{BW}=f_0/N=0.30$~mHz.  The total integrated noise in the lock-in bandpass
filter is thus $B_\text{rms}=2\cdot\text{NSD}\cdot\sqrt{BW}=5.9$~nG, as compared
to the 6.0~nG shown in the legend of Fig.~\fref{charging}(b).

The value $f_0=0.15$~Hz represents a compromise of two competing factors.
Choosing a higher $f_0$ would reduce the amount of $1/f$ noise. On the other
hand, a higher $f_0$ would mean measuring the field for a shorter amount of
time, which would reduce the duty cycle of the measurement given the fixed
$t_\text{charge}$ and the settling time of the sensor and ADC low-pass filters.
At a lower $f_0$, of course, we would be susceptible to the significantly
increased sensor noise.

We can read from Fig.~\fref{charging}(b) that with 1~A of current, the shield's
residual field changes by 114.7~nG. Repeating the measurement at 0.1~A, I
obtained a field change of 14.5~nG. Extrapolating these values to 24~$\mu$A, we
are led to expect a magnetization of 2--4~pG, well below the 30~pG requirements.
Furthermore, the 24~$\mu$A corresponds to charging the electrodes within one
second, whereas realistically they can be charged up to final voltage even
slower if desired. The 30~pG constraint merely requires us to charge electrodes
slower than within 50~ms assuming a constant charging rate. If the electrodes
charge up with an exponential time profile, care must be taken to limit the
maximum charging current to values given previously in this paragraph.

Note that the assumption of a constant rate will not hold if charging the
capacitive electrodes via a resistor, in which case the initial current will be
large, tending asymptotically to zero. The current-limiting resistor needs to be
large enough that at no time the current can exceed the above bounds. This is
true even if we attempt to implement a constant charging rate using a \DAC; in
this case it is important to ensure the voltage does not suddenly jump when
transitioning between nearby codes.

\subsection{Performance and limitations}

\subsubsection{Residual field}
\label{magnetic_shield_performance_residual}

The design requirements for the shield specified a 10~$\mu$G maximum average
residual field. This has been met; see Fig.~\fref{shimmed} for a typical
measurement of the fields inside after shimming.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/shimmed.png}
    \caption[Magnetic field development after shimming.]{The magnetic field
    \part{a} before shimming, \part{b} immediately after shimming, and \part{c}
    three weeks later.}
    \label{shimmed}
\end{figure}

Unless otherwise noted, all measurements have been taken along the axis of
symmetry of the cylindrical shield. It would be interesting to measure the
residual fields as a function of the transverse position. This measurement would
likely reveal stronger fields nearer the walls of the shield, providing support
to the theory that the larger the inner diameter of a shield, the lower and more
uniform the field along the axis of symmetry. Intuitively, this reasoning stems
from the fact the magnetic field on axis of a small current loop decreases
as a third power of the distance away from the loop.

Of particular interest is how the fields develop after the shimming procedure.
To this end, I have followed their evolution over the course of three weeks,
finding that neither the average nor the \RMS\ value of any of the two
transverse components do not exceed the specified 10~$\mu$G (see
Fig~\ref{threeweeks}). The data in that plot has been obtained by measuring the
fields every hour while keeping all the controllable parameters of the shield
constant. Unfortunately, at the time of the measurement, I did not have
available the means to monitor the magnetic field external to the magnetic
shield, or at any other point inside the shield except on-axis.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
   \includegraphics[width=.75\textwidth]{figs/todo/threeweeks.png}
    \caption[Average magnetic field over three weeks.]{Average and \RMS\
    magnetic field components in the central 2.5~m of the shield over the course
    of three weeks.}
   \label{threeweeks}
\end{figure}

It is true that just because the shield stayed in a low-field condition for
three weeks once, it is not certain it will always stay that way for as long. In
other words, it would be useful to find a model for predicting how long the
shield stays this way, or what the probability is that is has exceeded
10~$\mu$G. If we model the average internal magnetic field as a random walk,
then we can associate a diffusion constant to this process and calculate the
probabilities. Of course, there is no obvious physical reason why the growth in
internal magnetic field should be a random walk; it would be of interest to
compare other possible stochastic models for this behaviour, e.g.\ using Allan
deviation.

It would also be interesting to measure the noise spectrum inside the shield to
see if it is of a similar nature (i.e., maybe random walk) as the slow drifts in
the residual field. However, the noise density of the sensor (see
Fig.~\fref{sensornoise}, page \pageref{sensornoise}) I used for these
measurements is too large to easily read out the spectrum inside the shield; in
the future, we could attempt to measure it in some other way.

Since degaussing is used to significantly reduce the internal magnetic field, it
is of interest to observe whether degaussing could be used to periodically
restore the initial low-field conditions even after shimming the fields. To
estimate the effect of periodic degaussing, I have followed the evolution of the
magnetic field with hourly measurements while degaussing the shield daily (i.e.,
every 24th measurement). The results (Fig.~\fref{longterm_degaussing}) do not
obviously appear to either recommend or proscribe regular degaussing.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/longterm_degaussing.png}
  \caption{Average internal magnetic field while degaussing every 24 hours.}
  \label{longterm_degaussing}
\end{figure}

A comparison of Figs.~\ref{threeweeks} and \ref{longterm_degaussing} may appear
to indicate that degaussing increases the variability of the residual field over
time. However, we are unable to make a direct comparison between the two since
in the former case, the lab temperature stayed constant (to within 0.5~K) over
most of the three weeks shown in the plot. By contrast, in the latter case the
laboratory steam system was under maintenance, causing regular temperature
swings of several degrees. By way of estimating the temperature dependence of
the average residual field, I have placed two electric space heaters on the two
opposite sides of the shield, but with the airflow not directed towards the
shield. Disconnecting the heaters after several hours, I was then able to track
the room temperature and the magnetic field internal to the shield over several
hours as the room cooled back down. Repeating this thermal cycle twice yields
two separate field-vs-temperature curves, as in Fig.~\fref{t_dep}, on account of
magnetic hysteresis. Given the lab temperature is actually controlled to better
than one degree, the corresponding temperature-driven field drifts should be
insignificant.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/t_dep.png}
  \caption{Temperature dependence of average field inside the magnetic shield.}
  \label{t_dep}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Dynamic shielding}

In addition to the static internal field, it is also important to ensure the
external magnetic field noise is sufficiently attenuated inside. My measurements
of the dynamic shielding factor place it around 30,000 at DC (see
Fig.~\fref{dynSF}), which should reduce the expected 1~mG variations in the lab
field well below the level at which they would impact the molecular-beam
measurements. The frequency dependence of the dynamic SF shown in that figure
shows an increase in shielding as frequency increases, which makes sense in
light of skin effect considerations. For mu-metal of 1~mm thickness and relative
permeability of around 50,000, a frequency of 6~Hz would correspond to a
skin depth comparable to the inner-layer thickness. (The decrease in shielding
once the maximum is achieved is likely not physical and reflects some limitation
of my measuring setup. I have, however, been so far unable to find out what
causes this.)

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/dynSF.png}
    \caption[Dynamic shielding vs frequency.]{Dynamic shielding factor vs
    frequency and amplitude of the external field perturbation.}
  \label{dynSF}
\end{figure}

The spatial dependence of the dynamic SF is as shown in Fig.~\fref{spatialSF}. We
see that the region where the SF is greater than the required 5,000 is almost
three meters long, such that the sensitive part of the molecular beam will be
entirely contained within the shielded region.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/spatialSF.png}
  \caption{Spatial dependence of dynamic field.}
  \label{spatialSF}
\end{figure}

It will be noted that the spatial pattern of dynamic SF depicted is asymmetric,
as if one side of the shield works better than the other. That is also unlikely
to be the case. Further work may establish what causes this observation. One
possibility is that in addition to attenuating the external excitation, the
shield also changes the direction of the external field. By measuring only one
component of the shielding, we are unable to distinguish between attenuation and
change of direction; a three-dimensional measurement may clarify the situation.
Another possibility is that the asymmetry is due to some relaxation (or low-pass
filtering) in the measurement, such that reversing the direction of sensor
motion may change the results. It could also be the case that despite my careful
axis centering, the transverse position of the sensor changes along the length
of the shield, such that the sensor is closer to the walls of the shield at one
end. The fields are likely larger near the walls of the shield, but more work is
needed to determine the transverse profile of the dynamic shielding factor.

The dynamic SF measurements on the final shield were all done with the external
excitation caused by the left-right pair of rectangular coils, corresponding to
an approximately horizontal transverse external field. We expect from the
cylindrical geometry of the shield that the longitudinal shielding is
significantly worse, though we have not yet measured it. Similarly, it would be
important to measure the shielding in the vertical transverse direction.

The dynamic shielding is likely affected by the magnitude of the external
stationary fields. In large lab field, the outer layers of the shield will
saturate and become less effective. On the other hand, given the relatively
small size of the fields at the inner layers of the shield, the shim coils are
unlikely to affect the dynamic shielding. However, neither of these claims have
as yet been explicitly verified with the final version of the magnetic shield.

\subsubsection{Magnetic noise}
\label{magnetic:noise}

Instead of measuring the dynamic shielding as the attenuation of an externally
applied sinusoidal excitation we can try to directly measure the spectrum of the
magnetic noise inside the magnetic shield. However, to do so we would need
either better magnetometers or more ambient magnetic field noise. Due to the
excellent dynamic shielding as reported in the previous section
($\mathrm{SF}>25,000$), the lab magnetic field noise (of the order of
$10\uG/\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ at 1~Hz as per Fig.~\fref{sensornoise}, page
\pageref{sensornoise}) would be attenuated approx.\ two orders of magnitude
below the noise floor of the sensor. This is well below the design requirement
of 50~nG/$\sqrt{\mathrm{Hz}}$ at 25~Hz.

\subsection{Discussion}

We have built a four-layer cylindrical magnetic shield combining the ease of
handling of metglas for the outer three layers with the superior residual field
characteristics of mu-metal for the inner layer. The dynamic shielding in excess
of $10^4$ is sufficient to reduce the magnetic noise to a level well below the
requirements of the \CENTREX\ experiment. The static residual field can be
controlled using shim coils to the level needed for the experiment.

We have explained the mechanism that causes the static shielding to be much
better than the dynamic shielding, but it is still not entirely understood what
determines the pattern of the residual field. It is likely something to do with
magnetic domains that remain pinned during degaussing, for example if they are
pinned on inhomogeneities caused by internal mechanical stresses or inclusions
of impurities in the material. The former can be reduced to a large degree by
annealing (heating the material to a high temperature in a controlled
environment), while the latter are determined by the time the alloy is prepared
at the factory. After the annealing, the mu-metal needs to be subjected to a
minimum of external stresses to prevent work-hardening of the material.

In the current design some amount of mechanical deformation of the sensitive
mu-metal layer is inevitable since the layer carries the weight of everything
contained inside it (shim coils, vacuum chamber, electrodes, etc.). This is less
than ideal since it can cause a gradual reversal of the annealing over time,
inducing a component of the magnetization that is not amenable to degaussing.

The magnetic shielding system would also be greatly improved if there was a
repeatable method to estimate on-axis magnetic field from sensors located around
the central axis of the shield. This would allow shimming away the residual
fields without requiring access to the central shielded region, where the vacuum
chamber of the molecular beam experiment is located, making it difficult to
place magnetic sensors there.

\subsection{Conclusion}

\keypoint{What have we learned here?}

In this chapter we have discussed theoretical aspects of the magnetic shield
performance, as well as the construction, performance, and other details of the
magnetic shield constructed for the \CENTREX\ experiment.

We have drawn the distinction between the performance of the
magnetic shield against static and dynamic external magnetic field
perturbations. The dynamic shielding has been explored sufficiently in the
literature, while the static shielding---in particular the excellent performance
after degaussing has been applied---has remained somewhat of a mystery.

In our theoretical model for the effect of degaussing, we assume that the final
magnetization of the shield material acts so as to minimize the total energy in
the magnetic field everywhere in space. This minimum energy state is described
by the so-called anhysteretic curve, which is typically much steeper (on the $B$
vs $H$ plot) than any hysteresis curve of the same material. The larger
permeability $\mu=\d B/\d H$ of the anhysteretic curve results in a larger
shielding factor against static fields after degaussing.

\keypoint{Why is it important for CeNTREX?}

We have seen that the \CENTREX\ magnetic shield, together with
the shim coil system, achieves an average static magnetic field of 10~$\mu$G,
averaged over the shielded volume along the central axis of the cylindrical
shield. The dynamic shielding factor is of the order of 30,000. These and other
performance indicators mean the shield is sufficient for the use of the
\CENTREX\ experiment.

\keypoint{Will we revisit it in later sections?}

In the next chapter, the magnetic shield idea will be instantiated yet again on
a much smaller, simpler scale as the single-layer magnetic shield around a
1/3-scale interaction region.

\keypoint{What are the biggest remaining challenges to getting a measurement?}

As we have warned in the text, the magnetic field around a complex microscopic
system, such as as the immense number of ferromagnetic domains in a large
magnetic shield, changes over time. Past performance is no guarantee of future
results. Future work needs to put more definite bounds on how far is the average
static field inside the shield is likely to diverge from the original shimmed
condition.
