\section{Producing Francium}

This section is a collection of notes about how to go about making a source of
radioactive francium atoms, as needed for the project assembling FrAg
molecules---an entirely separate project and wholly irrelevant for \CENTREX.

\subsection{Isotope sources}

The longest-lived isotope, francium-223, can be obtained from the decay chain of
actinium-227 as shown in Fig.~\fref{Ac227}. Alternatively, we can use Ac-225 that
decays into Fr-221. The key difference is that All of Ac-225 is converted into
Fr-221, whereas only 1.4~\%\ of Ac-227 is converted into Fr-223. Thus we need 72
times the activity of actinium to get the same flux of francium.

In the following, we consider adapting two possible approaches from literature
to obtain a beam of francium, either an ion beam, or a neutral atom beam. See
also comparison in Table~\ref{ion_vs_neutral}.

\paragraph{Ion beam}

Guckert et al.\ \cite{guckert1998magneto} report on using an ion-beam source of
rubidium to load a magneto-optical trap. The rubidium-82 is obtained from a
sample of the relatively long-lived strontium-82 (half life = 25 days) in the
form of strontium carbonate. The strontium sample, a mixture of Sr-82 and Sr-85,
is loaded into a tantalum crucible and heated to a temperature where ionization
efficiency is high for rubidium but low for the strontium parent. The ions are
accelerated to 20~keV and passed through a high-resolution mass separator
\cite{guckert1997coupling} to isolate Rb-82 ions. Finally, the ions enter the
trapping cell and are implanted into an yttrium catcher foil. An inductive
heater heats the foil to release the implanted rubidium as a vapor inside the
cell.

In principle, the same approach could be used for francium by loading the
long-lived Ac-227 sample (half life = 22 years) into a crucible, heating to the
correct temperature to preferentially ionize the Fr-233 decay product,
implanted into a catcher foil, and heated to release into a vapor cell.

For both the Ac-225 and Ac-227 decay chains, large amounts of long-lived
isotopes are present in the oven:

\begin{itemize}
   \item Ra-225 and Ac-225
   \item Ra-223 and Th-227
\end{itemize}

At a temperature needed to diffuse enough Fr, nearly 100\%\ of the long-lived
isotopes would also survive the diffusion time. How can we keep the Ra, Ac, Th
from escaping the beam source? We can use the different ionization probability
of different elements as a function of temperature to select only the isotope we
are interested in. According to the Languimur Saha equation
\cite{dinneen1996orthotropic}, the ratio of ions to atoms evaporated from a
surface of work function $\Phi$ at a temperature $T$, where the atoms have an
ionization potential $I$, is given by
%
\[
   \frac{n_+}{n_0}=\frac{\omega_+}{\omega_0}\exp\left[\frac{\varepsilon(\Phi-I)}{k_\text{B}T}\right],
\]
%
where $\omega_+/\omega_-$ is equal to 1/2 for alkali atoms and $\varepsilon$ is
the charge on the electron. The ionization probabilities for Fr, Ac, Ra, Th are
plotted in Fig.~\fref{ionization} for three different materials the isotopes
could be evaporating from (platinum, titanium, tantalum).

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/ionization.png}
    \caption[Radioisotope ionization vs work function.]{Selective ionization of
    radioisotopes vs work function of the material they are evaporating from.}
  \label{ionization}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Neutral atom beam}

A different approach was used by Gould et al.
\cite{dinneen1996orthotropic,lu1997efficient} to obtain a neutral beam of
Fr-221. A nickel foil disk containing Ra-225, which decays to Ac-225 and then to
Fr-221, was mounted inside the beam source oven. In the alpha decay of actinium
to francium, the daughter nucleus is accelerated with enough kinetic
energy towards the platinum-coated walls of the oven to cause the francium
atoms to be buried to a depth of about 400~\AA\ in the platinum coating. As the
oven is heated to about 950\degC, the Fr diffuses out of the platinum (work
function = 5.65~eV). The Fr is emitted as positive ions which are attracted to a
negatively charged neutralizer made of a low-work function material (yttrium,
3.4~eV), from which the ions reevaporate as neutral atoms. They can either leave
the oven through a small aperture opposite the neutralizer, or they strike the
platinum walls, get ionized, and repeat the process. The atoms that manage to
escape through the aperture form a highly directional beam.

During the radioactive decay, the daughter nucleus is emitted with a significant
kinetic energy and gets buried inside the platinum walls. To estimate how deep
it gets buried, consider that the enthalpy of atomization for platinum is
5.7~eV/atom. Thus 5.9~MeV of energy would be enough to atomize about $10^6$
atoms. A sphere of that many atoms would have the approximate radius
$r=1\;\text{\AA}\cdot(10^3)^{1/3}=100\;\text{\AA}$
\cite{sigmund2014particle,bohr1948penetration,gaite2017penetration}.

We can calculate the kinetic energy of the francium daughter nucleus from simple
momentum conservation. For a decay of a stationary actinium nucleus into an
alpha particle and a francium nucleus, the momentum conservation gives
%
\[
   p_\alpha+p_\text{Fr}=0.
\]
%
The speeds are low enough that the kinetic energy is simply $T=\frac{1}{2}mv^2$
with momentum $p=mv=\sqrt{2mT}$, so
%
\[
   T_\alpha=(1+m_\text{Fr}/m_\alpha)T_\text{Fr}.
\]
%
Total energy is $T=T_\alpha+T_\text{Fr}$, so the kinetic energy of francium is
%
\[
   T_\text{Fr}=\frac{T}{1+m_\text{Fr}/m_\alpha}.
\]
%
In the decay of Ac-225 into Fr-221, there is 5.9~MeV of total energy, of which
Fr-221 gets 105.5~keV. In the decay of Ac-227 into Fr-223, there is 5.0~MeV of
total energy, of which Fr-223 gets 88.9~keV. A simple simulation of francium
penetration into a platinum target using the SRIM software
\cite{ziegler2010srim} shows that the nuclei would be embedded to a depth of
about 100~\AA\ (see Fig.~\fref{SRIM}).

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/SRIM.png}
   \caption{Simulation of francium penetration into platinum.}
  \label{SRIM}
\end{figure}

After the nucleus has been embedded into the platinum wall, it can diffuse out
of it again. Experimentally it is found that at 950\degC, approx.\ 25\%\ of
Fr-221 escapes from the platinum walls \cite{dinneen1996orthotropic}. Since the
lifetime of Fr-221 is $T_{1/2}=4.8$~min, we can calculate that the relevant
diffusion time is
%
\[
   t_\text{Pt}=-t_{1/2}\log_2(0.25)=9.6~\text{min}.
\]
%
We can use this time to calculate the diffusion constant. In a solid, the
diffusion probability is \cite{mehrer2007diffusion}
%
\[
   C(x,t)=\frac{M}{\sqrt{\pi Dt}}\exp\left(-\frac{x^2}{4Dt}\right),
\]
%
with $M$ some constant. The characteristic diffusion length is
%
\[
   2\sqrt{Dt}=400~\text{\AA},
\]
%
allowing us to deduce the diffusion constant at 950\degC:
%
\[
   D_\text{Fr--Pt}=6.9\cdot10^{-15}~\text{cm}^2/\text{s}.
\]
%
We can compare this to the self-diffusion coefficient of platinum
\cite{cattaneo1962self} at the same temperature:
%
\begin{align*}
   D_\text{Pt--Pt}&=0.22\exp(-2.89~\text{eV}/k_\text{B}T)~\text{cm}^2/\text{s}\\
   &=2.8\cdot10^{-13}~\text{cm}^2/\text{s}.
\end{align*}

The diffusion coefficient itself often obeys the Arrhenius relation
\cite{mehrer2007diffusion}
%
\[
   D=D^0\exp\left(-\frac{\Delta H}{k_\text{B}T}\right),
\]
%
where the pre-exponential factor $D^0$ is of order 1~cm$^2$/s, and $\Delta H$ is
the activation enthalpy of diffusion. Setting $D^0 = 1~\text{cm}^2/\text{s}$, we
can estimate the activation enthalpy:
%
\[
   \Delta H_\text{Fr--Pt}=3.4~\text{eV}.
\]

We can use the above numbers to estimate what fraction of francium would survive
the time it takes for the atoms to diffuse out of the platinum walls. Noting the
similar decay energies of Fr-221 and Fr-223, we assume that after hitting the
walls, both get buried to the same depth in the wall. Assume also they have the
same diffusion coefficient. However, Fr-223 ($t_{1/2}=22.0$~min) lives longer
than Fr-221 ($t_{1/2}=4.8$~min). The predicted survival rates are plotted in
Fig.~\fref{survival}.

\begin{figure}
   \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.6\textwidth]{figs/todo/survival.png}
    \caption[Francium survival probability vs oven temperature.]{Estimated
    survival probability for Fr-221 vs Fr-223 as a function of oven
    temperature.}
  \label{survival}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Safety considerations}

\subsubsection{Dose rate vs activity}

If we would like $10^7$/s Fr-223 atoms emitted from the oven at approx.\ 25\%\
extraction efficiency, then we need an actinium source with activity
%
\[
   A=\frac{10^7\text{s}}{25\%\cdot1.38\%}=2.7~\text{GBq}=73~\text{mCi},
\]
%
noting the 1.38\%\ branching ratio from actinium-227 to francium-223. In the
decay, a number of gamma rays at different energies are emitted, giving rise to
a different absorbed dose of radiation for each species in the decay chain. In
the literature, this is summarized as the so-called gamma-ray dose constant
$\Gamma$, defined as
%
\[
   \Gamma=\frac{\text{dose @ 1 m away from a point source}}{\text{source
   activity}}.
\]
%
$\Gamma$ is normally given in mSv/h for a 1~MBq source. We may use the published
$\Gamma$ values \cite{unger1982specific,peplow2020specific} to deduce the
dose-equivalent rates at 1~m distance away from an Ac-227 source of activity
$A=73~\text{mCi}$ for a number of decay products. These rates are shown in
Table~\ref{radioactivity}.

\subsubsection{Shielding}

In order to reduce the radiation below background levels, e.g.\ to 0.5~mSv/yr,
we need a shielding factor of about 5000:
%
\[
   \text{SF}=\exp(-\mu x)=5000,
\]
%
where $\mu$ is the linear attenuation coefficient for a given material, and $x$
is the material thickness. Table~\ref{radioactivity} also shows these
coefficients lead shielding against the gamma rays from the listed nuclides.
Starting from a single-isotope source, the dose rate of course continuously
changes over time as the parents decay into daughter nuclides;
Fig.~\fref{lead_shield} shows the expected dose rates as a function of time and
thickness of lead shielding.
