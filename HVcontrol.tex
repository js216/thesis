\section{High Voltage Control}
\label{HV_chap}

High voltage (approx.\ 30~kV) is needed at two points in the experiment: first
at the electrostatic lens, and then in the main interaction region. The lens
region does not have strict requirements on the precision, stability, and noise
of the high voltage, whereas in the main interaction region, these parameters
can directly translate into systematic and/or noise effects on the final result.
In the following section, we discuss the limitations on the accuracy of the high
voltage system and present some methods of voltage measurement and control.

\subsection{Voltage measurement schemes}

In the `standard' voltage measurement scheme (Fig.~\ref{standard}), two separate
power supplies are used to create the $\pm30$~kV applied to the two interaction
region electrodes, and each is measured by its own resistive divider. Each
polarity is then connected to the electrodes via a (floating) picoammeter and a
set of two relays used to switch the electrode polarity.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/standard.png}
    \caption{The `standard' voltage measurement scheme.}
    \label{standard}
\end{figure}

Besides the voltage divider and voltmeter limitations discussed above, this
method is particularly sensitive to thermoelectric voltages that can develop at
all the low-voltage interfaces: between the resistor and the connector, between
the connector and the cable (on both sides of the cable), between the connector
and the voltmeter. As explained previously, these voltages (of order 3~$\mu$V/K)
are significant at the scale of the low voltages measured: $3~\mu\text{V/K}/3~\text{V}=1~\text{ppm/K}$.

The problems associated with the thermocouple voltages can be avoided by using
the `ammeter' technique illustrated in Fig.~\ref{ammeter}. Only the upper half
of the resistive divider (Fig.~\ref{resistor_box}) is used, with the large
resistor to `convert' the high voltage into a low current. The current is then
led into a current-measuring device, from whose measurement we can then
calculate the high voltage. While the problems with the resistor's temperature
coefficient remain, the thermocouple voltages become negligible. For example,
adding a (temperature-dependent) voltage of 3~$\mu$V/K in the connectorized
interface between the resistor and the ammeter is an effect of size
$3~\mu\text{V/K}/30~\text{kV}=0.1~\text{ppb/K}$.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/resistor_box.png}
    \caption[Potted 10~G$\Omega$ resistor.]{Potted, connectorized 10~G$\Omega$
    resistor for measuring high voltage as a low current.}
    \label{resistor_box}
\end{figure}

The `ammeter' in Fig.~\ref{ammeter} could be a stand-alone ammeter, such as a
digital multimeter. For example, the Keysight 3458A comes with a 1~pA resolution
in the 10~$\mu$A range, or 0.1~ppm, with a temperature coefficient of 10~ppm/K.
Better performance can be obtained by attaching a resistor with a low
temperature coefficient directly to the multimeter; values as high as
100~k$\Omega$ can be obtained with sub-ppm/K tempco.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/ammeter.png}
    \caption[The `ammeter' voltage measurement scheme.]{The `ammeter' voltage
    measurement scheme. \part{Left} Using two ammeters to monitor each polarity.
    \part{Right} Using a 1~M$\Omega$ single resistor to measure the entire
    voltage span. The amplifier is an instrumentation amplifier.}
    \label{ammeter}
\end{figure}

Ultimately, the most important thing is to keep the magnitude of the electric
field constant. This magnitude is obtained as the vector sum of the electric
fields due to each electrode and is proportional to the difference of the
positive and the negative voltages. Therefore, measuring both of the power
supplies independently is not necessary; it is enough measure the total voltage
drop between the positive and the negative supply. Thus in addition to the two
high-resistance resistors, one for each supply, all that is needed is a single
low-resistance resistance to measure the total voltage drop (Fig.~\ref{ammeter},
right). This single resistor can be measured using a digital multimeter, or, as
shown in the figure, using an instrumentation amplifier to subtract a constant
reference voltage from the measured resistor voltage.

Since all electronic components have a temperature coefficient, it is important
to be aware of the relative magnitude of these effects and either control the
temperatures or verify that the thermal drifts are not a problem in the sense of
causing large systematic errors in the \gls{EDM} measurement. When handling low
voltages, one must watch out for parasitic voltage sources (thermocouples,
contact potentials, rectified RF interference), and when using low currents, be
mindful of potential leakage currents through high (but not infinite) impedance
current paths (connector, cable insulation, temperature and humidity dependent
surface contamination).

\subsection{Sources of error}

\subsubsection{Voltmeter limitations}

The high voltage control electronics, discussed above, are calibrated using a
high-precision 8.5 digit digital multimeter (Keysight 3458A) with order 1~ppm
accuracy.

\subsubsection{Thermoelectric EMF}

A junction of dissimilar materials is a source of temperature-dependent voltage.
For example, a junction between copper and Sn--Pb solder generates
5~$\mu$V/K, while a copper to copper oxide junction can create 1~mV/K.
\cite[sect.~5.11.4B]{horowitz2016art}. On the high voltage side, these are
entirely insignificant.

The thermocouple voltages are significant whenever
we are trying to measure a low voltage very precisely. For example, if
monitoring the high voltage (30~kV) using a 1:10,000 resistive divider, we will
obtain a 3~V signal where 6~$\mu$V/K means a 2~ppm/K effect.

\subsubsection{Ground issues}

Care must be taken that the voltage source and the experiment use the same
low-impedance ground reference, otherwise there is potential for a random
voltage offset on the electrodes.

\subsubsection{RFI/EMI}

RF interference induces oscillating voltages in circuits. It can get rectified
in semiconductors to cause DC offsets and drifts. This can be eliminated by
shielding all sensitive connections (which should be done for safety reasons
anyway).

\subsubsection{Leakage currents}

In addition to potential for magnetic fields inducing systematic effects (see
section \ref{systematics:leakage_current}), the leakage currents that take place
after the large (order of 1~G$\Omega$) current-limiting series resistor can
translate into significant voltage drops experienced by the electrodes.

The current-limiting series resistor is present for safety reasons, as well as
as an integral part of the `picoammeter' (see section \ref{picoammeter_sect}
below) that will be used to monitor the presence of leakage currents.

The leakage current effect would be especially problematic if the leakage
currents are not constant in time, or worse still if they are somehow correlated
to certain experimental reversals.

\subsubsection{Contact potentials}

There are two kinds of contact potentials to watch out for, Galvani potentials
and triboelectric effects. Galvani potentials refer to voltage differences
developing at the interface between two surfaces, such as relays or connectors.

Triboelectric effects arise when friction causes charge separation. This can
cause voltage differences in high-impedance systems. For example, after relays
switch, there may be a need to wait for the triboelectric charge to dissipate.

\subsubsection{Voltage divider issues}

We do not propose to measure the high voltage directly. Instead, we use a
voltage divider to reduce it to a low voltage that can be applied to a voltmeter
or precision amplifier. Due to the high voltages involved, the resistances need
to be correspondingly high to limit the current flowing through the divider and
the resulting power dissipation and heating.

The high-resistance resistors are normally specified with a 50~ppm/K temperature
coefficient. Therefore the temperature of the resistors will need to be
stabilized on the time scales relevant for the bandwidth in which the voltage
accuracy is required.

Consider a voltage divider consisting of a 10~G$\Omega$ resistor and a
1~M$\Omega$ resistor. Despite the large value of the 10~G$\Omega$ resistor, it
has an equal contribution to the overall thermal noise as the small resistor. We
may think of a voltage divider as a two-step process: first, the large `top'
resistor converts the signal to be measured from a large voltage (30~kV) to a
small current (3~$\mu$A). Then, the small `bottom' resistor converts the small
current back into a voltage, but small enough (3~V) to be easy to measure. The
Johnson current noise introduced by the 10~G$\Omega$ resistor at room
temperature ($T=300~\text{K}$) is:
%
\[
    i_\text{n}=\sqrt{\frac{4\kB T}{R}}=1.3~\text{fA}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}},
\]
%
which is 40~ppb of the 3~$\mu$A current coming out of the 10~G$\Omega$ resistor.
On the other hand, the 1~M$\Omega$ resistor has a much more significant voltage
noise:
%
\[
    v_\text{n}=\sqrt{4\kB TR}=0.13~\mu\text{V}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}},
\]
%
which is 40~ppb of the 3~V voltage drop across the 1~M$\Omega$ resistor. Note
that real resistors have more noise than just thermal noise, depending on the
construction of each unit.

\subsection{Voltage control for lens}

As mentioned above, the electrostatic lens requires a source of high voltage
where the voltage stability is not a critical concern. Instead, what is needed
is just a way to control the high voltage using the same DAQ system that
controls most of the rest of the \CENTREX\ experiment. This is done using a
commercial high-voltage module (Spellman TOF3000) with a home-built PCB to
supply the analog control voltage and to read out the current from the
high-resistance resistor (the electronics box is shown in Fig.~\ref{HV_pic},
right).

\subsection{HV relays}

To switch the polarity of the electric field in the main interaction region, we
use a set of two SPDT relays (schematic of the connection in
Fig.~\ref{standard}). The relays are by Ross Engineering (p.n.\
E60-DTP-80-2U-40-P/BD) and are pneumatically actuated (Fig.~\ref{HV_relays}).
The pneumatic system is in turn controlled using solenoid valves (McMaster p.n.\
4639K933). This allows us to place the solenoids far away from the interaction
region, since they cause a magnetic field that changes in phase with the
electric field reversals.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/HV_relays.png}
    \caption[HV relays.]{HV relays are pneumatically actuated, with solenoid
    valves controlling the pneumatics.}
    \label{HV_relays}
\end{figure}

The solenoids will have to be shielded; Fig.~\ref{solenoid_valve} shows the
crude way we used to estimate the how much magnetic field the solenoids `emit'.
The solenoid was placed in a 6" diameter cardboard tube that was wrapped
circumferentially with two layers of metglas foil of the same type as used in
the interaction region magnetic shield (FINEMET Wide Format Film, Hitachi Part
MS-FR 470-100M-T0, containing 18~$\mu$m-thick FINEMET FT-3M metglas between
protective layers of plastic). The solenoid was oriented with its axis
perpendicular to the axis of the cardboard tube with a magnetic sensor located
approximately along the axis of the solenoid. As a function of the distance $d$
between the solenoid and the magnetometer, we measured the difference in the
magnetic field with the solenoid energized and disconnected; the result is
plotted in Fig.~\ref{solenoid_valve} as the negative reciprocal third power,
$B^{-1/3}$, since that is the approximate functional dependence for how the
strength of a magnetic dipole falls away with distance.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/solenoid_valve.png}
    \caption[Measuring magnetic field due to the solenoid valve.]{Measuring
    magnetic field due to the solenoid valve. \part{Left} How the field was
    measured. \part{Right} Measurement results.}
    \label{solenoid_valve}
\end{figure}

I repeated the measurement with the solenoid unshielded, as well as located
inside a carbon steel pipe (McMaster p.n.\ 7750K122, alloy A53, class A). The
slope of $B^{-1/3}$ versus distance $d$ is
%
\[
    \frac{\d B^{-1/3}}{\d d}
    ~[\text{G}^{-1/3}/\text{m}]
    =
    \begin{dcases*}
         8.9&\quad unshielded\\
        11.9&\quad in steel pipe\\
        37.2&\quad in metglas tube;
    \end{dcases*}
\]
%
Thus the field attenuation for the steel pipe is $(11.9/8.9)^3=2.4$ and for the
metglas tube, $(37.2/8.9)^3=73.0$. This is to be compared to the expected
transverse shielding factor for a permeable cylinder (eqn~\ref{singlelayer}), 
$S_\text{T}=\mu t/2R$. For the metglas tube ($\mu\approx70,000$,
$t=0.2$~mm, $2R=6$"), we expect $S_\text{T}\approx91.8$. Conversely, for the
steel pipe ($t=0.28$", $2R=6.6$", $S_\text{T}=2.4$) we can calculate
$\mu=2RS_\text{T}/t\approx57$.

The strength of the shielded magnetic field is to be compared to the sensitivity
of the experiment to magnetic fields. The slope of the \gls{NMR} resonance to
$B$ fields is of the order of $\d f_0/\d B\sim2.5$~mHz/$\mu$G. We would like the
systematic due to the solenoid magnetic field to be below the desired
experimental sensitivity of $\sim45$~nHz, so the solenoid field has to be below
$B_\text{max}=30$~pG. As a function of the solenoid shield shielding factor SF,
this occurs at a distance
%
\[
    d_\text{min}=
    (\text{SF}\cdot B_\text{max})^{-1/3}/
    \frac{\d B^{-1/3}}{\d d}
    =
    \frac{360~\text{m}}{\text{SF}^{-1/3}}.
\]
%
For example, if we want to locate the solenoids a distance $d_\text{min}=15$~m
away from the interaction region, then we need a shielding factor of
$\text{SF}=(360~\text{m}/d_\text{min})^3=13,800$. That is a relatively high
shielding factor at a significant distance away from the solenoids. It would be
worthwhile considering whether there are other experimental reversals that relax
the shielding requirement.

\subsection{Insulation leakage currents}

The simultaneous availability of a high voltage source and a nanoammeter
presents the opportunity for measuring high impedances. For example,
Fig.~\ref{cable_leakage} shows the direct measurement of the leakage current
across the silicone insulation of a 30~cm segment of a cable rated up to 30~kV.
The cable was wrapped in aluminum foil, which was grounded via the nanoammeter.
The cable was `terminated' in a blob of RTV silicone (Dow Dowsil 737). We expect
to see an initial surge in current as the cable charges up to the set voltage.
The capacitance $C$ per unit length $L$ of thus-constructed coaxial cable with
inner (outer) radius $a\approx1$~mm ($b\approx5$~mm) is
\cite[Problem~2.43]{griffiths2013introduction}
%
\[
    C/L=\frac{2\pi\epsilon}{\ln(b/a)}=100~\text{pF/m},
\]
%
assuming $\epsilon=3\epsilon_0$. If the cable charges to 25~kV in a millisecond,
we expect a current surge of $I=C\;\d V/\d t=1~mA$, followed by a constant
leakage current given by the cable's insulation resistance. In practice, we see
a slow relaxation of the current over a time scale of minutes to hours, which is
likely due to dielectric absorption \cite[1x.3.10]{horowitz2020art} and can be
modelled as a combination of several series $RC$ time constants
(Fig.~\ref{cable_leakage}, right). Eventually, the leakage current settles down
to approx.\ 1~nA over the $L=30$~cm cable segment, implying an insulation
resistance of $R=V/(IL)=8~\text{T}\Omega\cdot\text{m}$.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/cable_leakage.png}
    \caption[Measurement of cable insulation leakage current.]{Measurement of
    cable insulation leakage current. \part{Left} Photo of experimental setup.
    \part{Right} Schematic model of dielectric absorption
    \cite[Fig.~1x.71]{horowitz2020art}; measured current as a function of time.
    The number inside the plot gives the average and standard deviation of the
    current in the second half of the plot (i.e., $t>10$~min).  The orange line
    is data smoothed with a Hann window, 71 data points long, and the black line
    is the exponential fit $I~[\text{nA}]=1.91e^{-t/0.55}-t/87.5+1.24$ for $t$
    in minutes.}
    \label{cable_leakage}
\end{figure}

Similarly, we can measure the insulation resistance of the potted resistor. In
principle, the resistance should be high enough to result in a leakage current
unmeasurable using the technique used here: the resistivity of the potting
compound, of the order $10^{15}~\Omega\cdot\text{cm}$, should mean $\sim30$~pA
currents. In practice (Fig.~\ref{potted_leakage}), we again measure currents of
the order of nanoamperes which relax slowly over the course of tens of minutes.
This emphasizes the need to ground the resistor box, or else the leakage current
would charge it to tens of kilovolts within seconds.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/potted_leakage.png}
    \caption[Leakage current from potted 10~G$\Omega$ resistor.]{Measurement of
    leakage current through the potting compound of the 10~G$\Omega$ resistor.
    \part{Left} Test setup; measured current vs time. \part{Right} Schematic of
    resistor connections. Note that the aluminum box of the resistor is
    connected to the nA meter, whereas the HV out connection is left floating.
    The `$\mu$A out' connection goes to the control circuit to measure the
    voltage.}
    \label{potted_leakage}
\end{figure}

The leakage currents considered in this section may or may not be a problem for
the precision control of the high voltage electrodes, but it serves as a
reminded to not assume there is no leakage current just because a circuit is
insulated with a thick layer of silicone rubber, such as in a cable or potting
compound.

\subsection{Picoammeter}
\label{picoammeter_sect}

To monitor leakage currents in the high voltage connections of the interaction
region electrodes, we intend to employ a `floating' picoammeter similar to the
one described in ref.\ \cite{sauer2008robust}. Following the simplified
schematic in Fig.~\ref{picoammeter}, the device works by measuring the current
developed across a large sense resistor. A biasing circuit, consisting of the
$33.2:133.2$ resistive divider and the opamp follower, sets the lower terminal
of the sense resistor approx.\ 1.25~V above the PCB common. The upper terminal
is connected to the non-inverting input of a voltage-to-frequency converter,
whose output is transmitted to a frequency counter via an optical fiber and is
proportional to the voltage across the resistor, and in turn, to the current
flowing through the device.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/todo/picoammeter.png}
    \caption[Simplified schematic of the picoammeter.]{Simplified schematic of
    the picoammeter, inspired by \cite{sauer2008robust}. Note that the right
    amplifier symbol is a voltage-to-frequency converter. The $RC$ outside the
    dashed rectangle are the parasitics between the PCB and the enclosure.}
    \label{picoammeter}
\end{figure}

With a large enough sense resistor, we can in principle be sensitive to very
small currents. For example, a 1~pA current flowing through a 1~G$\Omega$
resistor means a voltage drop of 1~mV. In practice, the accuracy will be
degraded by any parasitic leakage currents, which are often temperature
dependent. These include the input bias of the voltage-to-frequency converter or
potential contamination of the surface of the PCB. Furthermore, as drawn, the
circuit has two independent 1.25~V references: a resistive divider connected to
the $+5$~V rail, which is as stable as the voltage regulator powering the entire
circuit, and a separate 1.25~V reference built into the voltage-to-frequency
converter. These two will very likely have a large difference in their voltage
temperature coefficients, resulting in a drift of the offset frequency over time
and temperature.

A potentially more severe problem with this circuit is that the HV common $V_0$,
i.e.\ the ground point of the PCB, is not well defined with respect to the
ground point the HV power supplies, and vacuum chambers, are connected to. It is
possible that $V_0$ will charge to some random voltage through various parasitic
impedances. This is problematic since the opamp follower's output is connected
directly to the sensitive (high-impedance) trace $V_2$, which is in turn
connected to the electrodes. The electrode voltage is thus 1.25~V above $V_0$,
which itself is not well defined. In a sense, this ammeter determines, rather
than measures the current. It would be preferable if both terminals of the sense
resistor were connected to high-impedance inputs of a precision instrumentation
amplifier, for example AD8221 with input bias/offset current temperature
coefficient of 1~pA/\deg C. An example implementation is suggested in
Fig.~\ref{modern_pA}.
